# Videography Skill Development Overview

## Purpose

This outline is designed for a team member who is developing practical videography skills from a near-beginner level. The emphasis is on independent, reliable field production: operating a camera for interviews and events, collecting useful coverage, setting up basic lighting with a minimal kit, and recording quality sound.

The goal is not to make the learner a full cinematographer or editor immediately. The goal is to build enough technical control, production judgment, and repeatable workflow discipline that she can support real team projects with usable footage and sound.

---

## 1. Camera Fundamentals

### Purpose

Make the camera predictable and controllable before adding the complexity of interviews, events, lighting, or audio.

### Subjects to Cover

- Camera body orientation: buttons, menus, media slots, batteries, ports
- Basic camera setup workflow
- Sensor size, lenses, focal length, and field of view
- Exposure triangle:
  - Aperture
  - Shutter speed
  - ISO / gain
- Frame rates: 23.976, 24, 29.97, 30, 59.94, 60 fps
- Shutter angle / shutter speed conventions
- Resolution and codecs
- White balance
- Picture profiles / log / standard profiles
- Focus modes:
  - Manual focus
  - Continuous autofocus
  - Face/eye detection
  - Focus peaking and magnification
- Zoom lenses versus prime lenses
- Tripod use and leveling
- Handheld shooting basics
- Image stabilization
- Monitoring exposure:
  - Histogram
  - Zebras
  - Waveform, if available
- Monitoring focus and framing

### Practical Outcomes

By the end of this section, the learner should be able to:

- Set up a camera from scratch.
- Select appropriate resolution, frame rate, shutter speed, aperture, ISO, and white balance.
- Capture a properly exposed, focused, and color-balanced image.
- Avoid common beginner errors such as incorrect frame rate, bad white balance, soft focus, overexposure, underexposure, and unstable framing.

## 2. Composition and Visual Framing

### Purpose

Build the visual judgment needed to create clean, intentional, and usable footage.

### Subjects to Cover

- Rule of thirds
- Headroom, lead room, and looking room
- Interview framing:
  - Medium shot
  - Medium close-up
  - Tight close-up
  - Two-camera interview framing
- Eye line and interviewer placement
- Background control
- Depth and separation
- Avoiding visual distractions
- Foreground, midground, and background
- Camera height and perspective
- Motivated framing for events
- Shooting for different aspect ratios:
  - 16:9
  - 9:16
  - 1:1
- Safe framing for captions and graphics
- When to use static shots versus movement

### Practical Outcomes

By the end of this section, the learner should be able to:

- Frame a professional-looking interview.
- Identify and correct distracting backgrounds.
- Compose footage that is stable, intentional, and usable in an edit.
- Make framing choices that support the final delivery format.

## 3. Audio Fundamentals

### Purpose

Prioritize clean dialogue and usable sound, since poor audio can make otherwise acceptable footage unusable.

### Subjects to Cover

- Why audio quality is often more important than image quality
- Microphone types:
  - Lavalier
  - Shotgun
  - Handheld
  - Boundary / tabletop
  - Camera-mounted mic
- Wired versus wireless microphones
- Recorder versus camera audio
- Setting audio levels
- Avoiding clipping
- Monitoring with headphones
- Signal chain basics
- Mic placement
- Clothing noise and lav placement
- Room tone
- Ambient sound
- Redundant audio recording
- Syncing audio in post
- Common sound problems:
  - HVAC noise
  - Echo / reverb
  - Handling noise
  - RF interference
  - Low levels
  - Distorted levels
  - Clothing rustle
- Audio checklist before recording
- Event audio:
  - Getting a feed from a mixer
  - Recording speakers
  - Capturing backup sound
  - Understanding limitations when no house feed is available

### Practical Outcomes

By the end of this section, the learner should be able to:

- Record clean interview audio.
- Set and monitor appropriate audio levels.
- Place lavalier and shotgun microphones effectively.
- Troubleshoot common audio problems in the field.
- Capture acceptable event audio or recognize when additional support is needed.

## 5. Shot Strategy and Coverage

### Purpose

Teach the learner to think beyond simply recording what is happening and instead capture intentional, editable footage for interviews, events, and general production needs.

### Subjects to Cover

- Understanding the purpose of each shoot
- Translating a request or event agenda into visual priorities
- Identifying what footage an editor or stakeholder will need
- Thinking in sequences rather than isolated clips
- Capturing wide, medium, close-up, detail, and reaction shots
- Establishing shots and environmental context
- Interview coverage:
  - Framing a subject
  - Managing eyeline
  - Capturing clean starts and stops
  - Recording room tone
  - Getting alternate takes or pickups
- Event coverage:
  - Identifying key moments in advance
  - Capturing speakers, audience, interactions, arrivals, transitions, and details
  - Working unobtrusively
  - Anticipating action
- B-roll:
  - Capturing action, reaction, context, and texture
  - Matching B-roll to likely narration, interview content, or edit needs
  - Avoiding random or repetitive footage
- Holding shots long enough for editing
- Maintaining stable framing
- Knowing when to move and when to stay locked off
- Shooting for horizontal and vertical deliverables when needed
- Capturing safety shots and cutaways
- Taking simple field notes for the editor
- Recognizing when enough coverage has been captured

### Practical Outcomes

By the end of this section, the learner should be able to:

- Plan and capture useful footage for interviews, events, and general assignments.
- Collect footage that supports an edit rather than merely documenting activity.
- Anticipate key moments and visual needs without a shot-by-shot script.
- Return with enough variety for a short finished piece, recap, instructional segment, or archival record.

- Syncing audio and video
- Basic color correction awareness
- File naming and folder structure
- Proxy workflows, if used
- Selecting usable takes
- Marking notes for editors

### Practical Outcomes

By the end of this section, the learner should be able to:

- Shoot with post-production in mind.

- Identify footage, audio, or coverage problems during review.
- Understand how production decisions affect editing efficiency and final quality.

## Suggested Practice Assignments

These assignments should be used throughout the progression, increasing in complexity as the learner gains confidence.

1. Set up a camera, white balance, expose, and focus correctly in three different rooms.
2. Frame the same subject in a medium shot, medium close-up, and tight close-up.
3. Record lavalier audio and shotgun audio, then compare the results.
4. Film a 60-second seated interview with clean audio and stable framing.
5. Light the same subject three ways: window light only, one-light setup, and two-light setup.
6. Shoot a five-shot sequence of someone performing a task.
7. Capture B-roll for a hypothetical one-minute recap video.
8. Cover a small internal meeting or informal event and return with a usable footage folder.
9. Scout a room and write a short recommendation: usable, usable with fixes, or not recommended.
10. Shoot an interview where she is responsible for camera, audio, and lighting.
11. Review her footage with an editor and identify what helped or hindered the edit.
12. Complete a low-risk independent shoot and hand off the media using the team’s preferred folder and naming structure.

---

## Evaluation Criteria

The learner is ready to operate more independently when she can consistently:

- Set camera exposure, focus, white balance, resolution, and frame rate correctly.
- Capture clean dialogue audio and monitor it during recording.
- Set up basic interview lighting with a small kit.
- Frame subjects professionally.
- Capture stable, usable B-roll and event coverage.
- Think in terms of shots, sequences, and edit needs.
- Cover key event moments without constant prompting.
- Organize and hand off media correctly.
- Notice technical problems while there is still time to fix them.
- Explain setup choices clearly.
- Produce footage that an editor can use without major rescue work.
