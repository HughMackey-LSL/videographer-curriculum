Shot Lists and Production Schedules

~11 min read5 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the difference between a shot list and a script.
  • Build a prioritized shot list from a production brief.
  • Construct a production schedule that includes realistic buffer time.
  • Distinguish between a production schedule and a run-of-show.
  • Reprioritize coverage when a schedule collapses on shoot day.

What a Shot List Is — and Isn't

A shot list is not a script. A script tells you what people say and do. A shot list tells you what the camera needs to capture. These are related but not the same document, and confusing them leads to one of two problems: either the camera operator goes rogue and invents coverage that does not serve the edit, or the director tries to plan every line of dialogue spatially and the result is a rigid over-planned mess that falls apart the moment reality diverges from the plan.

A shot list is a prioritized coverage plan. It answers the question: "If I only have time to get some of these shots, which ones does the edit absolutely need?" That prioritization is the most important thing the shot list does. Without it, a camera operator under time pressure does not know what to sacrifice and what to protect.

Building a Shot List from a Brief

The starting point is the brief — the deliverable description, the purpose, and the content outline. From the brief, extract the must-have shots, the nice-to-have shots, and the B-roll needs. These three tiers form the structure of your shot list.

Must-have shots

These are the shots without which the edit cannot be completed. For an interview video, the must-haves are the main camera angle covering the full interview, clean audio throughout, and any specific moments or statements the client has flagged as essential. For an event video, the must-haves are the key moments: the speaker at the podium, the award presentation, the product reveal. If you get these and nothing else, the edit is possible. Everything on this list gets shot before anything on the lower-priority lists.

Nice-to-have shots

These shots improve the edit but are not required for it to function. A second camera angle on the interview. A wide establishing shot of the venue. An audience reaction shot. If time allows, you get these. If time runs short, you do not, and the edit will be fine.

B-roll needs

B-roll is the supplementary footage that covers cuts, illustrates narration, and gives the edit visual variety. For a staff interview, B-roll might be: the subject walking through the office, working at their desk, in a meeting, or using equipment relevant to their role. For an event, it might be: crowd shots, signage, details of the venue, catering tables, registration desk activity. B-roll is listed by topic or scene, not by specific shot — you have creative latitude here, but you need enough variety that the editor has options.

Shot list format

Keep it simple. A numbered list works well. Each item has: a shot number, a brief description, the location, and any technical notes (focal length, movement, frame size). Here is an example for a simple interview:

  1. Main interview — wide, 85mm equivalent, static, boardroom — A-ROLL PRIORITY
  2. Main interview — tight on face, 135mm equivalent, static, same position — nice-to-have
  3. Subject walking through office lobby — handheld, medium — B-roll
  4. Subject at desk, typing — handheld, medium close — B-roll
  5. Product demo — hands on equipment, close-up — B-roll PRIORITY
  6. Wide of boardroom with crew visible — end of day only — B-roll

Priority labels make it obvious what to protect. The person running the shoot should be able to glance at this list and immediately understand what cannot be cut.

Tip

When building a shot list for an interview, ask the interviewer or producer: "If you could only use three minutes of this interview in the final video, what would those three minutes be about?" Their answer tells you which B-roll topics are actually essential and which are just nice to have.

Shot Lists by Shoot Type

Interview shot list

The primary goal is clean, usable A-roll — the main camera on the subject, with good audio. Everything else supports that. Your secondary goal is a second angle for variety and to cover any edits in the A-roll. Your third goal is B-roll that illustrates what the subject is talking about. When building the B-roll list, map it to the interview topics — if the subject discusses three different aspects of their work, you need B-roll that illustrates each one. Do not plan generic office B-roll; plan specific, relevant B-roll.

Event shot list

Events are linear — they happen in a fixed order and cannot be repeated if you miss a moment. Your shot list should follow the run-of-show chronologically. Flag any moment that cannot be missed with a clear visual marker (bold, asterisk, or highlight). Then identify which nice-to-have shots can be captured during transitions between key moments — when one thing is ending and the next has not started, that is your window for establishing shots, crowd coverage, and detail work.

The Production Schedule

The production schedule is the plan for the day: what happens, when, where, and who is involved. It is the document that coordinates everyone — the camera operator, the director, the subject, the location contact, any additional crew. Without a shared schedule, people make assumptions about timing that conflict with each other, and the day becomes a series of improvised handoffs.

Building the schedule

Start with the hard constraints — times that cannot move. The subject is available from 10 a.m. to noon. The venue must be cleared by 5 p.m. The lunch break is noon to 1 p.m. These are your anchors. Build the rest of the schedule around them.

For each item in the schedule, estimate how long it will realistically take. Then add 20 to 30 percent buffer. This is not pessimism — it is the statistical reality of production. Gear takes longer to set up than planned. Subjects arrive late. A technical problem adds 15 minutes. A conversation that was supposed to take 10 minutes takes 25. If you schedule exactly what you think each item will take, you will be running behind before lunch.

A simple schedule block for an interview shoot might look like this:

  • 8:30 a.m. — Crew arrives, load-in begins
  • 9:00 a.m. — Camera, audio, and lights set up in boardroom
  • 9:30 a.m. — Lighting and audio checks complete, rehearsal frames set
  • 10:00 a.m. — Subject arrives, mic fitted, camera roll begins
  • 11:30 a.m. — Interview wraps, B-roll begins in office areas
  • 12:00 p.m. — B-roll complete, wrap begins
  • 12:30 p.m. — Gear loaded, space cleared

Sharing the schedule

Send the schedule to everyone involved before shoot day. The subject needs to know when to arrive and what to expect. The location contact needs to know when you will arrive, how long you will be there, and when you will be done. Any additional crew needs the call time and the address. A schedule that lives only in your head is not a schedule — it is a plan that cannot be coordinated.

Schedule vs. Run-of-Show

A production schedule governs what the crew does and when. A run-of-show governs what the event or program does and when. For most shoots, these are different documents, and you need both. The run-of-show tells you what is happening so you know where to point the camera. The production schedule tells you when the crew needs to be set up, how long each location move takes, and when wrap begins. They must be compatible with each other — if the run-of-show has a key moment at 2 p.m., your production schedule must have the camera in position and rolling by 1:55 p.m.

When the Schedule Collapses

Schedules collapse. Not sometimes — regularly. A location becomes unavailable. A key subject is pulled out of the shoot by an urgent meeting. Rain makes the outdoor portion impossible. The interview runs long and cuts into B-roll time. When this happens, the response is not panic — it is triage.

Return to the shot list and identify the must-haves that have not yet been captured. These are non-negotiable. Then identify what time you actually have left and what you can realistically accomplish in that time. Communicate the change to the director or producer immediately — they may be able to buy back time or adjust the scope. Make the call early, not when you are already out of time.

The camera operator who stays calm during a schedule collapse, protects the must-haves, and communicates clearly is infinitely more valuable than one who either panics or tries to hide the problem until it is too late to fix.

Note

The difference between an experienced operator and a new one is rarely technical — it is usually the ability to reprioritize under pressure. Building this skill starts with having a clear shot list and schedule to return to when things go sideways.

Key Takeaways

  • A shot list is a prioritized coverage plan, not a script — its most important function is telling the operator what cannot be sacrificed under time pressure.
  • Divide shots into must-haves, nice-to-haves, and B-roll needs so that triage decisions are already made before the pressure of shoot day.
  • A production schedule coordinates the crew; a run-of-show coordinates the event — both are needed for event shoots, and they must be compatible with each other.
  • Always add 20 to 30 percent buffer time to every schedule block — production consistently takes longer than planned.
  • When a schedule collapses, triage immediately using the shot list: protect must-haves, communicate the change, and drop nice-to-haves.
