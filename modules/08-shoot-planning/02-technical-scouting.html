Technical Scouting

~10 min read5 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Distinguish between a general location scout and a technical scout.
  • Assess power availability and identify potential electrical load issues.
  • Evaluate ambient noise conditions and identify sources that cannot be controlled.
  • Analyse sunlight direction and timing relative to a planned shoot window.
  • Identify at least one plan B camera position for every primary setup.

Location Scout vs. Technical Scout

A location scout answers the general question: "Can we work here?" A technical scout answers the specific question: "How exactly will we work here?" The distinction matters because the two activities require different mental modes and different information. You can do both on the same visit if you are experienced, but beginners benefit from treating them separately — first confirm the space is viable, then work out the production details.

On a technical scout, you are thinking like a production designer and an audio engineer and an electrician all at once. You are not just looking at the room — you are measuring it, listening to it, and stress-testing your planned setup against its reality.

Power Assessment

Power problems on location tend to emerge at the worst possible moment: just after you have positioned and aimed two LED panels, balanced the color temperature, and are about to roll. Identifying power limitations in advance costs you ten minutes on the scout; discovering them on shoot day can cost you the entire setup.

Circuit count and outlet locations

Walk the room and count every accessible outlet. Note whether outlets are on the same wall — outlets on the same wall are almost always on the same circuit, which means they share a capacity limit. In a typical commercial building, a single circuit handles 15 or 20 amps at 120 volts, giving you 1800 or 2400 watts of usable capacity (with some headroom to avoid tripping the breaker). Two 150-watt LED panels plus a laptop and a camera charger is well within that range. A set of old tungsten fresnels is not.

Extension cord and strip requirements

Note the distance from the nearest outlet to where you plan to place your lights and your charging station. If you need a 25-foot extension cord, you need to bring one. If you need to run two legs of extension cord across a pedestrian area, you need gaffer tape to secure them safely. Both of these are planning tasks, not improvised solutions.

Important

Always ask building management before running extension cords across hallways or through doorframes. In many facilities this requires prior authorization, and in some it is prohibited for safety reasons. Getting permission in advance is faster and less embarrassing than being told to pack up mid-setup.

Noise Assessment

Ambient noise is the single most common audio problem in field production, and it is also the problem that operators most often fail to anticipate. The human brain is remarkably good at filtering out background noise in a familiar space — the HVAC hum, the street traffic, the refrigerator in the break room two doors down. A microphone does not filter. It captures everything.

Standing in silence

When you arrive on the technical scout, stand still in the primary camera position and close your eyes. Listen for 30 seconds. List everything you hear: air handling units, fluorescent light buzz, distant traffic, a nearby stairwell, voices from an adjacent office, elevator machinery. Now ask yourself: which of these can I control, and which cannot be controlled?

HVAC systems can sometimes be switched off for the duration of the shoot by contacting building facilities — this is worth asking about. Traffic noise can sometimes be reduced by closing windows. The elevator shaft cannot be moved. The compressor in the server room next door is not going anywhere. Knowing this in advance lets you plan: if the ambient noise floor is too high for dialogue, you may need to move the shoot to a different room, schedule it at a quieter time of day, or switch from a room mic to a lavalier that is physically closer to the subject's mouth.

Time-of-day noise variation

Ask your location contact what the noise level is like at different times of day. A lobby that is quiet at 7 a.m. may be unusable for audio at 10 a.m. when the building is fully occupied. A conference room adjacent to a busy street may be fine on a Sunday but problematic on a weekday. If you have any flexibility in scheduling, use the technical scout to identify the quietest window and build the shoot around it.

Tip

Bring a pair of headphones on your technical scout and hold your camera's on-board microphone in the position where the subject will be speaking. Play back the recording. What you hear in the headphones is much closer to what the microphone will capture than what your ears alone will tell you.

Room Layout and Geometry

The room layout assessment answers a deceptively simple question: where exactly does the camera go to produce the shot you want? This requires thinking about three variables simultaneously — camera position, subject position, and background — and finding the combination that works for all three.

In a small room, your options may be limited: the camera must go in one corner to get sufficient distance, which means the subject must sit in a specific chair, which means the background is whatever is on that wall. In a large room, you have choices, and the technical scout is where you make them deliberately rather than arbitrarily on shoot day.

Look at the room from camera height — crouch down to tripod level and look at what the lens would see. The ceiling tiles that are invisible at standing height may dominate the frame at camera height. The window that seems beside the subject from where you are standing may be directly behind them from the camera's perspective.

Sunlight and Window Direction

Sunlight is the most powerful and least controllable light source in field production. Understanding what it will do at the time of your shoot is a core technical scouting task.

Check which direction the windows face. A north-facing window (in the northern hemisphere) provides consistent, soft indirect light throughout the day — ideal for interviews. An east-facing window provides warm direct light in the morning and shadow in the afternoon. A west-facing window does the reverse. A south-facing window may flood the room with direct sunlight at midday.

Cross-reference the window direction with your shoot time. If you are shooting a two-hour interview in a room with west-facing windows starting at 3 p.m., you will have changing light conditions as the sun moves and eventually shines directly through the glass. You either need to control that window with a curtain or blind, or you need to know about it in advance so you can schedule differently.

Background Options

Every camera position in the room has a background. On the technical scout, evaluate each candidate position and its corresponding background independently. Ask: Is this background clean and uncluttered? Does it have appropriate depth and visual interest, or is it flat and uninspiring? Does it contain anything that will distract the viewer — a window with bright outdoor exposure, a cluttered shelf, a sign or logo that the client has not approved? Can furniture or objects be moved to improve it?

Document your preferred background and at least one alternative in your location report. If the client changes the subject's position on shoot day, you want to already know which background works from the new angle.

Identifying Plan B Positions

Every primary setup should have an identified plan B. Plan B does not need to be as good as plan A — it just needs to be workable if plan A is unavailable. The facilities contact who promised you the boardroom may have double-booked it. The window that was perfect on the scout may be unusable because of direct sun that was not present during your morning visit. The outlet you were depending on may be dead.

On the scout, identify one fallback camera position, one fallback subject position, one alternative power source, and, if possible, one alternative room. Write these down in your location report. If you need any of them on shoot day, you will be glad you thought of them when you were calm and had time to think.

Common Mistake

Evaluating the room at a different time of day from the planned shoot, then being surprised by how different the light is on shoot day. If you scout at noon and shoot at 4 p.m., the light in the room will be completely different. Either scout at the same time of day as the shoot, or explicitly note the time of your scout and reason through what will change.

Key Takeaways

  • A technical scout answers specific production questions about power, noise, layout, light, and background — it goes deeper than a general location assessment.
  • Assess power by counting circuits and outlets, calculating load requirements, and noting the distance from outlets to planned gear positions.
  • Stand still and listen on every technical scout — ambient noise that is invisible to your ears while you are moving becomes obvious when you stop and pay attention.
  • Evaluate sunlight based on window direction and shoot time, not just on how the room looks when you visit.
  • Document a plan B camera position, power source, and room for every primary setup before shoot day.
