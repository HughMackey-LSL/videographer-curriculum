Day-Of and End-Of-Shoot Checklists

~11 min read5 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Execute a complete day-of setup checklist before rolling on any shot.
  • Explain what each setup checklist item is testing and what failure looks like.
  • Execute a complete end-of-shoot checklist before leaving any location.
  • Articulate why checklists remain valuable for experienced operators, not just beginners.
  • Estimate the real downstream cost of a missed checklist item in post-production.

Why Checklists Work

Aviation, surgery, and nuclear power plant operations all rely on checklists — not because the people performing these tasks are incompetent, but because expertise does not protect against the specific type of error that checklists prevent: the item you know perfectly well but forget to do in the pressure and distraction of the moment.

Production is exactly this kind of environment. You know what white balance is. You know how to set it. And you can still roll 20 minutes of interview footage on Tungsten white balance because you were talking to the director when you powered up the camera and the light in the room changed between your scout and shoot day. The checklist does not add knowledge — it enforces execution at a moment when distraction is highest.

The habit of running through a checklist every time — not just when you are new, but always, for every shoot — is one of the clearest markers of a professional operator. The people who skip the checklist because they are experienced are the same people who occasionally show up in post-production horror stories.

The Day-Of Setup Checklist

Run through this checklist physically, in order, before rolling on any shot. Do not do it from memory — use the physical list. Check each item explicitly rather than assuming it is fine because you set it up a moment ago.

Camera and support

  • Camera on tripod — is it level? Check the bubble level on the head. An unlevel camera is a subtle but immediately visible problem in the edit, especially on shots with visible horizontal or vertical reference lines.
  • Lens attached — is it clean? Remove the lens cap, hold the front element up to a light source, and look for dust, fingerprints, or moisture. A single fingerprint in the center of the lens will appear as a soft glow in high-contrast scenes. Clean it with a lens cloth — not a shirt.
  • Battery inserted — is it fully charged? Check the battery indicator in the camera's status display. If it is not showing full, replace it with a fresh one now, before the shoot begins, not when it dies in the middle of a take.
  • Card inserted — is it formatted? How much space remains? Confirm the card is the one you formatted the night before. Check the remaining space indicator. Know how long you can record before you need to swap.

Camera settings

  • Resolution and frame rate. Confirm you are recording in the correct resolution (likely 4K for a professional deliverable) and frame rate (24p for cinematic, 25p or 30p for broadcast, 60p or higher for slow motion). These settings cannot be fixed in post without significant quality loss.
  • Shutter speed. For 24p footage, your shutter speed should be 1/48 or 1/50. For 30p, use 1/60. For 60p, use 1/120. Running a double-frame-rate shutter gives you the motion blur characteristic of natural-looking video. A wildly incorrect shutter speed (1/1000 on 24p footage) will make motion look choppy and strobe-like — again, not fixable in post.
  • ISO. Is it set to a native ISO or a reasonable working value? An accidentally elevated ISO from a previous shoot situation can result in noise-heavy footage that degrades the image.
  • White balance. Set a custom white balance if possible, or confirm the correct preset for the light source (daylight, tungsten, fluorescent, or the specific Kelvin value from your scout). Mismatched white balance on an interview is visible, unprofessional, and difficult to fully correct in post.
  • Picture profile. Confirm you are in the correct color profile — Log for footage that will be color graded, or a standard profile for footage delivered without grading. Log footage delivered to a client without grading is washed out and unusable.
Important

Settings errors — wrong frame rate, wrong white balance, wrong picture profile — are among the most common production mistakes and among the most difficult to correct in post. A resolution error cannot be fixed at all. Frame rate mismatches create playback problems. Incorrect white balance on Log footage can be partially corrected but requires significant grading time and may not fully match other shots. The setup checklist exists specifically to catch these errors before recording begins.

Audio

  • Audio levels monitored. Put headphones on and listen to what the microphone is capturing. Listen specifically for: the ambient noise floor, any unexpected noise sources, and whether the microphone is actually recording — some audio inputs default to disabled after a camera restart.
  • Levels set — no clipping. Have the subject speak at normal interview volume and watch the audio meters. Peaks should land around -12 to -6 dBFS for dialogue. If levels are clipping (hitting 0 dBFS and going red), reduce the gain. Clipping cannot be repaired in post — the audio is distorted and will remain so.
  • Correct input selected. Confirm the camera is recording from the input you intend — external microphone, not the on-camera microphone; or the correct wireless receiver channel. It is easy to have a wireless lavalier plugged into channel 1 while the camera records from channel 2.

Framing and focus

  • Lights positioned and powered. Check that each light is aimed at the intended subject, that barn doors or diffusion are as planned, and that no light is creating an unexpected reflection or shadow in the frame.
  • Framing confirmed. Look through the viewfinder or at the monitor with a critical eye. Does the composition match what you planned? Is there anything in the frame that should not be there — a cable, a bag, a stand leg, an unintended reflection?
  • Focus checked. Zoom in to the focus point (using peaking or magnification) and confirm that the subject's eyes are sharp. This is especially important if you are using a shallow depth-of-field setup where small focus errors are visible.
  • Record a test clip. Roll for 30 seconds with the subject in position. Play it back on the monitor. Confirm the image looks as expected, the audio is clean, and the recording is actually writing to the card. A test clip is the final safety net before committing to real takes.

The End-Of-Shoot Checklist

The end of a shoot is a moment of fatigue and distraction. Gear gets left behind. Cards get lost. Stands get set down somewhere and forgotten. The end-of-shoot checklist is the last line of defense against mistakes that range from mildly inconvenient (a forgotten cable) to catastrophic (a lost card with unverified footage).

Media and storage

  • All camera cards safely stored and labeled. Every card that came out of a camera goes into a card wallet or case, labeled or taped to indicate that it contains unverified footage. Confirm the count — if you shot on three cards, three cards should be in your case.
  • Media confirmed before formatting. If you are doing field ingestion (copying footage to a drive on location), do not format any card until the full ingestion workflow — two verified copies — is complete. If you are not doing field ingestion, do not format any card on location at all.

Cables and accessories

  • All cables packed. HDMI, USB-C, XLR, power — they all need to come home. Cables are the most commonly left-behind item in production because they are draped over things and obscured by other gear. Walk the cable run explicitly.
  • All lights powered off and cooled. LED lights are generally safe to pack immediately after powering off. Any tungsten or HMI fixtures need cooling time before packing — the housings reach temperatures that can melt bags and burn hands. Note this specifically in your wrap routine.
  • Stands lowered and capped. Light stands and C-stands should be lowered to their minimum height before packing. Stand legs extended and raised are a trip hazard during wrap. Check that all stands have protective caps on any sharp spigot ends.
  • Audio gear powered off, batteries removed from wireless gear. Wireless transmitters and receivers that sit in a bag with their batteries in will drain overnight and may leak. Remove the batteries when packing. This extends battery life and protects the units.

Location and communication

  • Room sweep for left items. After all gear is packed, do a slow walk of the entire space with fresh eyes. Look under tables, behind doors, and on ledges. Check outlets for adapters and power strips that may have been plugged in and pushed behind furniture. Check the bathroom if the crew used it.
  • Handshake with the location contact. Thank the contact by name, confirm that the space has been returned to its original condition, and confirm any follow-up details (when they will receive the footage, what the next step in the project is). This professional close leaves a good impression and maintains the relationship for future access.
Note

A left-behind cable is a minor inconvenience — a quick call to the location contact and it is usually retrievable. A left-behind card with unverified footage is potentially a major problem. Treat media handling with more care than gear handling during wrap.

The Real Cost of a Missed Checklist Item

Checklist failures feel abstract until you have lived one. The real cost of a missed item is almost never "we reshoot" — it is "we do the best we can with what we have," which means hours of extra work in post, a compromised deliverable, or a difficult conversation with a client.

Wrong white balance on two hours of interview footage means a colorist spends three hours trying to match it to correctly-balanced B-roll, the results are still imperfect, and the client notices. Wrong frame rate means the footage may not be usable with other footage shot correctly. A left-behind lens means the shoot you planned for the second half of the day cannot happen and the schedule collapses. A lost card means footage that cannot be recreated is gone permanently.

The checklist takes four minutes on setup and three minutes on wrap. The cost of what it prevents is measured in hours, dollars, and professional reputation. Run the list every time.

Tip

Print your setup and wrap checklists and laminate them. Keep the laminated cards in your camera bag and use a dry-erase marker to check off items during the shoot. When the shoot is done, wipe and reset. A physical checklist that is always with your gear is used; a checklist on your phone that requires unlocking, finding the note, and scrolling is occasionally skipped.

Key Takeaways

  • The setup checklist covers: tripod level, lens clean, battery charged, card formatted and spaced, all camera settings verified (resolution, frame rate, shutter speed, ISO, white balance, picture profile), audio monitored and leveled, lights positioned, framing confirmed, focus checked, and a test clip recorded.
  • The end-of-shoot checklist covers: media safely stored and labeled, cables packed, lights off and cooled, stands lowered, audio gear off and batteries removed, room sweep completed, and a professional handshake with the location contact.
  • Checklists prevent the specific type of error that expertise does not protect against: the thing you know perfectly well but forget in the distraction of a live shoot environment.
  • The real cost of a missed checklist item is measured in post-production hours, compromised deliverables, and professional reputation — not in the four minutes the checklist takes to run.
  • A physical, laminated checklist that lives in your camera bag is more reliable than a digital checklist that requires navigating to.
