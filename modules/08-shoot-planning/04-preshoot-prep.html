Pre-Shoot Preparation

~10 min read5 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Execute a complete night-before preparation routine for a field shoot.
  • Calculate battery requirements for a given shoot duration.
  • Estimate media card space needed based on resolution, codec, and duration.
  • Load a vehicle in a way that prioritises efficient access to gear on arrival.
  • Describe the morning-of steps that set up a shoot for success.

The Night Before

The night before a shoot is when the real preparation happens. Not on the morning of — by then, the decisions are already made and the gear either works or it does not. Rushing the night before is far less consequential than rushing the morning of. On shoot morning, every minute you spend searching for a cable or waiting for a battery to charge is a minute you are not setting up, and a late setup is a compressed shoot.

Build a consistent night-before ritual and execute it every time, for every shoot. Not just when you think the shoot is complicated — every time. The routine catches problems that you would not have caught because you were not looking for them.

Charge everything

Every rechargeable battery in your kit goes on charge the night before: camera batteries, wireless transmitter and receiver batteries, on-camera monitor batteries, LED light batteries, and any portable power stations. Do not charge them the morning of — a battery that charges overnight is full; a battery that charges for 45 minutes while you eat breakfast may not be. Check each one when you pull it off the charger and confirm the indicator shows a full charge.

Format your cards

Format cards in-camera, not on the computer. In-camera formatting creates the correct file system structure for that specific camera and eliminates any card errors that might cause problems during recording. Do not rely on a card that you formatted six months ago and has been sitting in a bag — format it fresh. If you have cards that are not yours or that have footage still on them, set them aside and do not format them until you have confirmed the footage is backed up elsewhere.

After formatting, check the remaining space on each card and confirm it is correct. A 128GB card that was supposedly empty but shows only 40GB free has a problem that needs to be resolved before shoot day.

Review the brief and shot list

Read through the production brief, shot list, and schedule one more time the night before. You will notice things you did not notice the first time — a shot that requires a specific lens you have not packed, a location detail that raises a question, a time constraint you had not fully registered. Make a note of any questions and send them to the relevant contact that evening so there is time to get an answer before you leave in the morning.

Check weather and logistics

Look up the weather for the shoot location. If there is any chance of rain and you are working outdoors or in transit with gear, plan for waterproofing. Check traffic and confirm the route. Confirm the parking situation. If you need to sign in or be met by a contact, confirm the name and phone number of that person and make sure you can reach them before you arrive.

Lay out all gear

Place everything you intend to bring on a flat surface and look at it. Cross-reference it against your kit list. What is missing? A power adapter. A specific lens. The wireless receiver. The spare SD card. A lens cloth. These are the things you discover on the floor of your home studio, not in the parking lot of the location.

Tip

Keep a printed or digital kit list that matches your standard loadout for different shoot types (interview kit, event kit, run-and-gun kit). The night-before gear check is a matter of laying out what is in your bags and comparing it to the list — not trying to remember from memory what should be there.

Battery Math

Battery management is a calculation, not a feeling. If you feel like you have enough batteries but have not done the math, you may be wrong. The math is simple: divide the total shoot duration by the runtime of one battery, then add at least one spare.

Example: Your camera battery lasts 90 minutes under typical shooting conditions (continuous recording with the screen on). Your shoot is 3 hours. Three hours divided by 90 minutes equals two batteries at minimum. But you should never plan for the minimum — a battery that runs low at a critical moment costs you the shot. Plan for three batteries on a 3-hour shoot: one in the camera, one warm spare in your pocket, one charging in the car or at the venue if power is available.

The same logic applies to wireless microphone transmitters and receivers. A lavalier transmitter on a 9V battery may last 4 to 6 hours, but "may" is not good enough for a long shoot day. Bring fresh batteries and swap at a natural break — after the first hour, before the last hour. Battery failure in an audio transmitter mid-interview is one of the more demoralizing production problems because it is entirely preventable.

Important

Battery runtime specs from manufacturers are measured under ideal, controlled conditions. Real-world runtime is typically 15 to 25 percent shorter when recording 4K, using autofocus continuously, or operating in cold weather. Apply a conservative multiplier to manufacturer specs rather than taking them at face value.

Card Space Math

Media space is also a calculation. The key variables are your recording format (resolution and codec) and your shoot duration. Approximate data rates for common formats:

  • 1080p H.264 at moderate bitrate: roughly 15 to 25 GB per hour
  • 4K H.264 at high bitrate: roughly 50 to 80 GB per hour
  • 4K ALL-I (intraframe) or ProRes: roughly 100 to 250 GB per hour depending on compression level

For a 3-hour shoot in 4K H.264, plan for 150 to 240 GB of raw footage — that is before any B-roll or additional takes. If you are shooting a long interview where the camera runs continuously, add a generous margin. If you are shooting B-roll where you roll and stop frequently, the actual recorded footage is a fraction of the shoot duration, but plan conservatively.

Always have more card space than you think you need. A blank card costs nothing to carry; running out of space mid-interview costs you the shot and the subject's trust.

Loading the Vehicle

Load the car in reverse order of use — the last thing you need is loaded first, and the first thing you need is loaded last so it is accessible without unpacking everything else. On arrival at a location, the first thing out of the vehicle is typically the camera bag and the tripod. The last thing you need is the backup hard drive, the catering supplies, and the wardrobe bag. Pack accordingly.

Fragile gear — lenses, monitors, audio recorders — goes in padded bags and is loaded last, on top, where it will not be compressed by other equipment. Heavy items like light stands and sandbags go in first, low, and secured so they cannot slide. A light stand that falls across a camera bag during a sharp turn can crack a lens.

Morning Of

Arrive at the location early enough that you are set up and ready to roll at least 15 minutes before the shoot is scheduled to begin. "Early enough" is not at the call time — it is at least 30 to 60 minutes before the first recording is needed, depending on the complexity of the setup.

Check in with the location contact immediately on arrival. Confirm the room is available, access is cleared, and nothing has changed since your last communication. If something has changed — the room is double-booked, access requires an escort, a previously available outlet is now behind a locked panel — you want to know as early as possible, not five minutes before the subject arrives.

If something goes wrong the morning of — a piece of gear is missing, a battery will not charge, you are stuck in unexpected traffic — communicate immediately. Do not wait until you are certain it will be a problem; communicate as soon as you suspect it might be. A client who knows a shoot is starting 20 minutes late because you told them an hour in advance is far more understanding than one who has been standing in the lobby wondering where you are.

Common Mistake

Doing battery charging and card formatting the morning of the shoot rather than the night before. This compresses the preparation window significantly and means that if any battery fails to charge fully or any card has an error, you have no time to resolve it before departure.

Key Takeaways

  • The night-before ritual — charging, formatting, reviewing the brief, laying out gear — is when preparation happens; the morning of is when you execute, not when you prepare.
  • Battery math: divide shoot duration by battery runtime and add at least one spare; apply a 15 to 25 percent real-world reduction to manufacturer specs.
  • Card space math: know your codec's approximate data rate and multiply by shoot duration with a generous margin; always bring more capacity than you think you need.
  • Load the vehicle in reverse order of use so the gear you need first is accessible without unpacking everything else.
  • Arrive early enough to be set up before the shoot begins, check in with the location contact immediately, and communicate problems as soon as you suspect them — not after they have become unavoidable.
