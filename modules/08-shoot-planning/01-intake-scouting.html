Intake Questions and Location Scouting

~10 min read5 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Identify the core intake questions to ask before accepting any shoot request.
  • Explain why clarifying the final deliverable matters before planning begins.
  • Describe what to look for when scouting a location in person.
  • Use remote scouting techniques when a physical visit is not possible.
  • Produce a brief location report that communicates key findings to others.

Why Intake Matters

Most production problems that surface on shoot day were actually created weeks earlier — in the gap between what the camera operator assumed and what the client actually needed. Intake is the discipline of closing that gap before any gear is loaded into a case. A single 15-minute conversation before the shoot often saves hours of reshooting or editing workarounds afterward.

Intake is not just about gathering information. It is about surfacing assumptions. Clients often have a clear picture in their head of what they want but no vocabulary to describe it technically. Your job during intake is to ask questions that draw that picture out into shared, concrete terms.

The Eight Core Intake Questions

These questions apply to virtually every shoot type — interview, event, training video, product demo, or documentary-style coverage. Adapt the language to the context, but do not skip any of them.

1. What is the final deliverable?

A one-minute social clip, a 20-minute training video, and a highlight reel for a conference all require fundamentally different approaches. Knowing the deliverable tells you how much coverage you need, what aspect ratio to shoot in (16:9 for widescreen, 9:16 for vertical social), and how many distinct scenes or locations matter. Ask specifically: How long is the final video? Where will it be published? On what screen will most people watch it?

2. When is the deadline?

Deadlines shape everything from how much time you have to scout properly, to whether you can afford to reschedule if something goes wrong, to how quickly media needs to be handed off to an editor. A "we need this for next week" situation is managed differently from "this is going live in three days."

3. Who is the subject and what is the purpose?

Understanding who will be on camera — their comfort level, their role in the organization, whether they have been filmed before — changes how you prepare the location and how you direct on the day. Understanding the purpose clarifies tone: a staff recruitment video needs warmth and approachability; a compliance training video needs clarity and formality.

4. Where is the location?

Get the full address, the specific room, and the name of the contact who will let you in. Ask whether parking is available, whether there are loading restrictions, and whether you need to sign in or obtain any access passes in advance. A location you cannot actually get into on shoot morning is a serious problem.

5. How long is the shoot?

This tells you how many batteries to bring, how many cards you need, and whether you can afford to do multiple takes. It also tells you whether you need to eat, whether there is a hard out time, and whether you are working alone or with a crew.

6. Are there access or policy restrictions?

Some locations prohibit photography of certain areas, require a media escort, or have union rules about who can operate what equipment. Hospitals, schools, government buildings, and corporate campuses often have media policies. Find out before you arrive, not after you set up.

7. What is the audience?

Internal staff training videos and public-facing marketing videos are shot and edited differently. The audience also tells you whether on-screen text needs to be large and clear, whether you need open captions, or whether the video will be viewed with or without sound.

8. What does success look like to the client?

This open-ended question often surfaces the most important information. Ask the client to describe what a great outcome looks like, and listen carefully. They may tell you about a previous shoot that went poorly, a specific style they admire, or a constraint that did not come up in the earlier questions.

Tip

Send a short written intake form before your first conversation with a new client. Even if they only fill in half the fields, their answers tell you which topics need more discussion — and the form itself signals that you are professional and organized.

Location Scouting

A location scout is a visit to the shoot space before shoot day, with the explicit goal of answering production questions rather than watching what happens there. Even a 20-minute walk-through can eliminate the five most common on-location surprises: the background that does not work, the noise source that cannot be silenced, the light that creates a problem, the outlet that does not exist, and the room that is smaller than expected.

What to Look For on a Scout

Camera positions. Where will you physically place the tripod? Is the floor level? Is there enough distance between the camera and the subject to achieve the framing you want with the lens you have? Is there an obstruction — a pillar, a desk, a piece of equipment — that limits your options?

Subject placement. Where will the subject sit or stand? What is behind them from the camera's point of view? Is the background interesting, neutral, or distracting? Can furniture be moved to improve it?

Background elements. Look at what the camera will actually see, not the room in general. A branded wall that looks great to the naked eye may be out of focus and underlit at the shooting distance you need. Conversely, a plain wall two steps to the left may give you exactly the clean, professional look the client wants.

Power sources. Locate every outlet in the room. Count them. Note which ones are on the same circuit and whether anything that cannot be unplugged is drawing power from that circuit. If you need to run lights, you need to know whether the building can handle the load.

Light sources. Look at where natural light is coming from and estimate what it will do at the time of the shoot. A window behind a subject creates a silhouette problem. A window beside a subject creates one half of an excellent natural key light. Note whether blinds or curtains can control it.

Taking Reference Photos

Photograph the room from every potential camera position. Photograph the background options. Photograph the outlet locations. Photograph anything that might be a problem. These images become your reference when planning gear, and they can be shared with a director, producer, or second camera operator who cannot scout in person. Take more photos than you think you need — you will be glad you did when you are sitting at your desk the next day trying to remember what the ceiling height was.

Remote Scouting

When a physical visit is not possible — the location is in another city, access is restricted before shoot day, or time simply does not allow for it — remote scouting is your next best option. It is not as good as being there in person, but it is far better than arriving with no information at all.

Google Maps Street View can give you the exterior of a building and any outdoor spaces. It tells you where the building faces relative to the sun and what the surrounding environment looks and sounds like.

Photos from the client are valuable but must be interpreted carefully. A photo taken on a phone with a wide-angle lens in portrait mode looks nothing like what a cinema camera on a tripod will capture. Ask for photos of the specific room from multiple angles, and ask what time of day they were taken so you can judge the light.

A video call from the location is the most useful remote scouting tool available. Ask your contact to walk you through the space on a video call, pan slowly, and hold the camera still at the positions you care about. You can direct them in real time: "Can you move to the left? What is behind you from that angle?"

Writing a Location Report

After any scout — physical or remote — write up a brief location report. It does not need to be formal. A one-page document or a shared note covering the following is enough: the address and contact name, the confirmed shoot room and any access instructions, key camera positions with their pros and cons, power outlet locations and any concerns, noise concerns and possible mitigation, light conditions and any required equipment (curtains, ND gels, reflectors), and any unresolved questions that need follow-up before shoot day.

Sharing this report with anyone else involved in the shoot — a director, a producer, a second camera operator — ensures everyone is working from the same information and reduces the chance that someone shows up with different expectations.

Common Mistake

Relying on the client's verbal description of the location without seeing it yourself or requesting reference photos. Clients frequently underestimate how much the space affects production — "it's a nice conference room" can mean anything from a well-lit, spacious boardroom to a windowless room with fluorescent overhead lighting and no space to set up a tripod.

Key Takeaways

  • The eight intake questions cover deliverable, deadline, subject, location, duration, restrictions, audience, and success criteria — skipping any one of them creates risk.
  • A location scout answers production questions specifically: where does the camera go, where does the subject go, what is the background, where is the power, and what is the light doing?
  • Reference photos from the scout give you and your collaborators a shared visual reference for planning gear and positions.
  • Remote scouting via Google Maps, client photos, or a live video call is a valid alternative when an in-person visit is not possible.
  • A brief written location report ensures everyone involved in the shoot is working from the same information.
