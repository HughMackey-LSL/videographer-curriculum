Equipment Failures

~9 min read5 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Respond to battery, card, and mic failures with calm, pre-planned actions.
  • Identify safe camera support alternatives when a tripod head fails.
  • Recover a locked or frozen camera without losing recorded footage.
  • Handle RF interference changes mid-shoot at an appropriate moment.
  • Apply the golden rule: never risk losing footage you have already captured.

The Right Mindset for Equipment Failures

Equipment fails. It fails at inconvenient moments, in unfamiliar environments, under the eyes of stakeholders who are watching to see how you respond. The operator who handles a failure without visible agitation maintains control of the situation. The operator who reacts with alarm transfers that alarm to the subject and everyone in the room.

The preparation that makes calm possible is simple: before every shoot, confirm your backups are charged, formatted, and within reach. A spare battery that is dead, or a spare card that is full, is not a backup — it is a false sense of security. Check everything the night before.

Important

The golden rule of field production: never attempt to fix a problem in a way that puts recorded footage at risk. If you have footage on a card, do not format that card to troubleshoot. If you have a file being written, do not remove the card. Footage that exists is salvageable; footage that has been lost is gone permanently.

Dead Battery in the Camera

The camera warns you of a low battery. Or it does not, and it simply turns off mid-take. Either way, the response is the same:

  1. Stop at the next natural break (end of a sentence, a pause in the action).
  2. Remove the depleted battery and insert a charged spare.
  3. Allow the camera to complete its startup sequence before rolling again.
  4. Confirm that all settings (frame rate, ISO, white balance, recording format) have been retained. Some cameras reset certain settings on power cycle.

Battery management is one of the most preventable causes of field failure. Charge every battery in your kit the night before a shoot, not the morning of. Rotate batteries in use — do not drain one completely before switching to the next, as this allows you to confirm how much charge each has remaining.

Wireless Mic Failure

The wireless receiver shows no signal, or the audio degrades suddenly mid-shoot. Wireless systems are among the most failure-prone items in a typical field kit. Work through this sequence:

  1. Check the transmitter. Is it still on? Has it gone into standby mode? Does it show battery level? If the transmitter battery is low, replace it immediately — wireless transmitters often fail without warning when the battery drops below a threshold.
  2. Check the receiver. Is it showing the correct frequency? Has it lost lock with the transmitter? On most systems, you can see whether the link is active.
  3. Switch to a wired connection. If you have a wired XLR option, connecting the mic directly to the camera bypasses the wireless system entirely. Keep a wired lav or XLR cable in your kit for exactly this scenario.
  4. Use the camera-mounted microphone as emergency backup. A camera mic is not ideal for interview audio — it picks up camera noise, room ambience, and the operator — but it is significantly better than no audio at all. In a worst case, it provides enough reference for the edit and may be usable for portions of the interview where the subject's voice is loud and clear.
  5. Ask to reshoot the most critical moments. If the failure occurred during a key answer that cannot be covered in editing, a brief and tactful reshoot request is appropriate: "That answer was excellent — I want to make sure we have it clearly. Could we go through that once more?"
Tip

Carry spare batteries for your wireless transmitter in your shirt pocket or a belt pouch during a long shoot. Transmitter batteries can deplete faster than expected if the transmitter runs on sleep for the walk to the location and then transitions to full power for the interview.

Full Memory Card

The camera stops recording or shows a card full warning. This is an entirely preventable failure — confirm available card space before every shoot — but the fix when it happens is simple: stop recording, swap to a spare card, confirm the camera is recording to the new card, and resume. Note the cut point in your shot log so the editor knows where the card change occurred.

If you do not have a spare card, you have two difficult options: review the existing footage and delete clearly unusable takes (out-of-focus, badly exposed, false starts) to free space, or connect the camera to a laptop or external drive and transfer files on the spot. The latter can take significant time and should only be attempted during a long natural break.

Card That Cannot Be Read

You swap cards, the camera cannot read the new card, or a card that was previously working now shows an error. This is a more serious situation:

  1. Try the card in a different slot on the camera, if one is available.
  2. Try a different card entirely — the card may be genuinely failed.
  3. Do not attempt to format the card unless you are absolutely certain it contains no unreplaced footage. Formatting erases all data. If you are unsure, leave the card as-is and use another.
  4. If a card fails mid-recording, the camera may be able to recover files when it next starts up. Allow the camera to perform any recovery procedures before removing power.

Light That Fails

A battery-powered LED panel dies or a mains light fails during the shoot. Assess quickly:

  • Was it the key light? Losing the key light has the most impact. Check whether the room's available light — windows, practicals — can substitute. Reposition the subject relative to the available light source if needed.
  • Was it a fill or background light? Loss of fill light creates harder shadows; loss of background light removes background separation. These are workable — adjust your framing to hide the worst of it and continue.
  • Can remaining lights be rearranged? A single light that was functioning as fill can sometimes be repositioned to serve as a key. A reflector can replace a fill in daylight situations.

Always carry a reflector. It weighs almost nothing, costs very little, and can substitute for a fill light in almost any daylight or mixed-light situation.

Common Mistake

Continuing to use a lighting configuration that is clearly underperforming because you do not want to interrupt the subject. A brief pause to reposition a light produces better footage for the entire remainder of the shoot. The cost is 60–90 seconds; the benefit is every subsequent take.

Tripod Head That Won't Lock

A tripod pan/tilt head that does not lock is a camera safety issue, not just an inconvenience. A loose head can allow the camera to tip forward or tip to the side under the weight of a lens, potentially resulting in a dropped camera. Do not attempt to use a loose head by balancing the camera manually — this is how cameras fall.

Alternatives in order of preference:

  1. Switch to a different tripod if one is available.
  2. Use a monopod if available — a monopod supports weight even without a locking mechanism.
  3. Shoot handheld. A handheld interview is not ideal but is far preferable to a damaged camera or an operator straining to hold a position manually throughout a long take.
  4. Place the camera on a stable flat surface (a table, a shelf, a pile of cases) and frame accordingly.

Camera Lockup or Freeze

The camera stops responding — the record button does not engage or disengage, the menu will not open, the display is frozen. This happens occasionally with all digital cameras:

  1. Wait 10–15 seconds. Some cameras pause briefly when writing large files or performing internal operations that look like a freeze but are not.
  2. If genuinely frozen, remove the battery. This is a forced shutdown — some cameras warn against it, but it is typically the only option when the camera is completely unresponsive.
  3. After reinserting the battery and restarting, allow the camera to complete any file recovery process (many cameras check the card on startup after an unclean shutdown).
  4. Check whether recorded footage is intact before proceeding. Review the most recent clips.
  5. Note the incident in your shot log. If the camera continues to behave erratically, check for a pending firmware update, which can resolve known stability issues.

RF Interference Mid-Shoot

Wireless audio interference can develop mid-shoot as other wireless devices come online in the building — a presentation system in a nearby room, additional Wi-Fi access points, a mobile phone brought into the space. If you hear the interference developing through your headphones:

  1. Continue recording through the current take. Do not interrupt the subject mid-sentence.
  2. At the next natural break, access the wireless receiver's frequency scan function.
  3. Scan for a clean frequency and note it.
  4. Re-pair the transmitter to the new frequency (requires a brief moment with the subject).
  5. Do a quick audio check before resuming.

The entire process takes about two minutes if you are familiar with your wireless system. Practice this procedure before your first field use — navigating an unfamiliar menu while a subject waits is stressful and slow.

Key Takeaways

  • Check and charge all batteries and format spare cards the night before every shoot — prevention is the best response to equipment failure.
  • When wireless mic fails, work through transmitter, receiver, wired alternative, and camera mic in that order.
  • Never use a loose tripod head — the risk of a camera fall is not acceptable under any time pressure.
  • Never attempt to fix a problem in a way that risks footage you have already recorded.
  • Camera freezes are usually resolved by a battery pull followed by a restart and file recovery check.
