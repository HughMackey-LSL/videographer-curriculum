Diagnosing Visual Problems

~8 min read5 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Apply a systematic diagnostic sequence instead of making multiple simultaneous adjustments.
  • Identify and correct exposure problems by working through aperture, ISO, and shutter speed in order.
  • Recognise white balance casts and correct them without interrupting the subject.
  • Eliminate LED flicker by matching shutter speed to the power line frequency.
  • Troubleshoot soft or out-of-focus images using a structured checklist.

Start With the Monitor

When something looks wrong on set, the first question is always: is this a display problem or a capture problem? Before you adjust any camera setting, look at your monitor critically. Is it calibrated? Is the brightness cranked up so high that a correctly exposed image appears overexposed? Is there strong ambient light washing out the screen? Confirm that the monitor itself is not the source of confusion before you touch the camera.

Once you are confident the monitor is reading accurately, begin your systematic diagnosis. The most common beginner mistake is changing multiple camera settings simultaneously — you end up not knowing which change fixed (or worsened) the problem. Always change one variable at a time, observe the result, and then decide on the next step.

Tip

Use your histogram rather than the monitor image alone when diagnosing exposure. A histogram gives you objective data about highlights and shadows that monitor brightness cannot distort.

Exposure Problems

Overexposure

If the image is too bright — highlights are blowing out, skin tones look washed out — work through the following sequence:

  1. Aperture first. Close down the aperture by one stop (e.g., from f/2.8 to f/4). This is the most direct control for exposure without affecting motion rendition.
  2. ISO second. If you are already at a wide aperture for depth-of-field reasons, reduce ISO to its native value (typically 100 or 160).
  3. Shutter speed last. Raise the shutter speed to the next standard value above the 180-degree rule limit (e.g., from 1/50 to 1/100 on a 25fps camera). Understand that this will slightly affect motion blur — use it as a last resort in still or slow-moving interview situations.
  4. Add ND filtration. If you have an ND filter available, this is often the cleanest solution for outdoor or bright-window situations — it lets you maintain aperture and shutter without compromise.

Underexposure

If the image is too dark — shadows are crushed, faces are muddy — work through the same sequence in reverse:

  1. Aperture first. Open the aperture (e.g., from f/5.6 to f/2.8). Be aware that this also reduces depth of field, so confirm focus remains sharp after the change.
  2. ISO second. Raise ISO incrementally. Know your camera's acceptable ISO ceiling — for most modern cameras this is ISO 3200 to 6400 before noise becomes distracting in a professional context.
  3. Add light. If you have any light source available — a battery-powered LED panel, a reflector, repositioning a practial lamp — add it now.
  4. Shutter speed. Lowering shutter speed below the 180-degree rule will introduce motion blur. This is generally not acceptable for interviews but may be acceptable in static B-roll.
Common Mistake

Raising ISO before opening the aperture when underexposed. Always try aperture first — a wider aperture costs you nothing except depth of field, while high ISO introduces noise that cannot be removed in post.

White Balance Problems

Colour casts are one of the most immediately visible signs that something is wrong, yet they are also one of the quickest problems to fix.

  • Orange cast: The camera's white balance is set to Daylight (5600K), but the scene is lit by tungsten or warm LED sources (around 3200K). The camera is expecting blue light and over-corrects the warm light toward orange. Fix: set the white balance to Tungsten or to the matching Kelvin value of your lights.
  • Blue cast: The camera is set to Tungsten, but the scene is lit by daylight or cool LEDs. Fix: set white balance to Daylight or the appropriate Kelvin value.
  • Green or magenta cast: Often caused by fluorescent or mixed-source lighting. Use custom white balance by pointing the camera at a white or neutral grey surface in the scene and using the camera's custom WB function. This corrects for unusual tints that preset values cannot address.

Setting Custom White Balance Mid-Shoot

You can perform a custom white balance without interrupting the subject. Ask the subject or an assistant to hold a white card or grey card in the subject's position. Take the custom white balance reading from that card, then remove it and continue recording. The whole process takes under 30 seconds. If no white card is available, a white wall, white clothing, or even a blank white email on a phone screen can serve as a temporary target.

Flicker From LED Fixtures and Monitors

LED lights and monitors that are not flicker-free can pulse at the frequency of the local power supply. When your camera's shutter speed does not align with that frequency, you will see horizontal banding or a rhythmic brightness flicker in the image.

The fix is straightforward: set your shutter speed to a multiple of the power line frequency.

  • 50Hz (PAL regions — most of Europe, Asia, Africa, Australia): Use 1/50, 1/100, or 1/200.
  • 60Hz (NTSC regions — North America, Japan, parts of South America): Use 1/60, 1/120, or 1/240.

If you are unsure which frequency the location uses, try 1/50 and then 1/60 and observe which eliminates the flicker. Note that some LED fixtures flicker at twice the line frequency — if 1/50 does not solve it, try 1/100.

Reflections and Glare

Glare from windows, glass surfaces, or shiny objects is most efficiently addressed by changing the camera angle rather than moving the light source. Even a subtle horizontal shift of the camera position changes the angle of incidence and often eliminates the reflection entirely. If repositioning is not possible, a circular polarising filter on the lens can significantly reduce specular reflections, particularly from non-metallic surfaces like glass and water. As a last resort, adjust the offending light — flag it, diffuse it, or reposition it.

Soft or Out-of-Focus Images

A blurry image during an otherwise smooth shoot typically has one of these causes, in rough order of likelihood:

  1. The subject moved out of the focal plane. If you focused on the subject's eyes and they leaned forward or back, they may now sit behind or in front of your depth of field. Refocus and, if depth of field is shallow, consider stopping down slightly.
  2. Autofocus has lost the subject. If using continuous AF, the system may have hunted to the background. Switch to manual focus for a locked-off interview setup.
  3. The focus mode is wrong. A camera left in Single AF from a previous shoot will not track a moving subject. Check your focus mode setting.
  4. Lens cap or filter partially obstructing the lens. This sounds obvious, but a UV filter that has become partially dislodged, or a lens cap that was not fully removed, causes a soft edge that can be mistaken for focus error. Check the front element.
Note

When using a monitor or EVF to assess focus, zoom in to 100% (using your camera's focus assist zoom function) on the subject's eye before confirming critical focus. What looks sharp at normal view may be soft at pixel level.

The Systematic Approach

Experienced operators develop a mental diagnostic tree that they run through quickly when something looks wrong. The underlying principle is always the same: identify the symptom precisely, then eliminate possible causes one at a time. Changing multiple settings simultaneously when under pressure is the fastest route to a confused camera and a compromised shoot. Slow down by 20 seconds, diagnose clearly, make one change, and confirm.

Key Takeaways

  • Check the monitor calibration first — a bad display creates phantom problems.
  • Fix exposure by adjusting aperture, then ISO, then shutter speed — in that order.
  • Orange cast means the white balance preset is too cool for the light source; blue cast means the preset is too warm.
  • Eliminate LED flicker by matching shutter speed to the regional power line frequency (1/50 for 50Hz, 1/60 for 60Hz).
  • Change one variable at a time when diagnosing — simultaneous changes obscure the root cause.
