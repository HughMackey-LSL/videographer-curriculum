Fixing Audio Problems

~9 min read5 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Use headphones as the first diagnostic step for any audio problem.
  • Trace a no-signal problem through a systematic checklist of possible causes.
  • Identify and fix buzzing and humming caused by ground loops and RF interference.
  • Manage HVAC noise, clothing rustle, echo, and distortion in the field.
  • Recognise when a take's audio is compromised and take immediate corrective action.

Put On Headphones First

The single most important rule in field audio troubleshooting is this: always monitor through headphones. The built-in speaker on a camera or recorder is too small, too quiet, and too susceptible to ambient noise to give you a reliable picture of what is actually being recorded. A problem that is completely obvious through headphones — a buzz, a dropout, clothing rustle — can be entirely invisible on a camera meter that shows apparent signal.

Before you begin every shoot, plug in your headphones, roll a short test, and listen. Do not rely on levels alone. A meter showing signal does not mean the signal is clean, undistorted, or coming from the right microphone. Your ears are the instrument.

Important

Never hand over footage with audio problems you did not notice on set. Editors cannot fix what you did not catch. Monitoring through headphones is a professional non-negotiable, not a preference.

Silence or No Signal

When you hear nothing — no room tone, no signal at all — work through this checklist before assuming the microphone is broken:

  1. Is the microphone powered on? Wireless transmitters and some condenser mics have their own power switches. Check the transmitter's LED or display.
  2. Is the cable connected at both ends? XLR connectors can look fully seated but not be locked. Unplug and re-seat both the mic end and the camera/recorder end.
  3. Is phantom power enabled? Condenser microphones require 48V phantom power from the recorder. If you are using a dynamic mic and phantom power is on, that is usually fine — but a condenser with phantom power off will produce no signal.
  4. Is the input gain above zero? On some cameras, the gain can be set so low (or the input level accidentally set to zero) that the meter shows nothing. Raise the gain briefly to confirm whether the mic is working.
  5. Is the correct input selected? Many cameras allow you to choose between an internal mic, an XLR input, and a 3.5mm input. Confirm the camera is listening to the right source.
  6. Is the wireless receiver paired to the transmitter? Some wireless systems can lose pairing after a power cycle. Refer to the system's pairing procedure.
Tip

Tap or scratch the microphone capsule gently to produce a rough sound. If you hear it through headphones, the signal chain is working from that point forward. If you hear nothing, the problem is between the capsule and your headphones.

Buzzing and Humming

Ground Loop Buzz

A low, continuous 50Hz or 60Hz hum — often described as a mains buzz — is almost always a ground loop. This occurs when two pieces of equipment are connected via audio cable and are also both connected to AC power at different ground potentials. The difference in ground potential drives a small current through the audio cable, which the recorder picks up as hum.

Solutions in order of ease:

  • Plug both devices into the same power outlet or the same power strip. This equalises the ground potential.
  • Use a DI box (direct injection box) between the signal source and the recorder. DI boxes include an isolation transformer that breaks the ground loop.
  • If the hum appears only when a laptop or phone charger is connected, try running that device on battery power for the duration of the recording.

RF Interference

A buzzing or clicking that is less regular, or that sounds like a digital stutter, is more likely RF interference on a wireless mic system. Other wireless devices, Wi-Fi networks, and even mobile phones can inject noise into a wireless audio system operating on a congested frequency.

Fix: during a natural break in the shoot (not while the subject is talking), access the wireless system's menu and scan for a cleaner frequency. Most professional wireless systems have an auto-scan function. Pair the transmitter to the new frequency on the receiver. This takes under two minutes on most systems.

Common Mistake

Assuming wireless mic buzz is a ground loop, or assuming mains buzz is RF interference. The sound is different: ground loop buzz is a steady, low tone that matches the power line frequency; RF interference is irregular, clicking, or has a digital quality. Misidentifying the cause leads to the wrong fix.

HVAC and Air Conditioning Noise

Institutional locations — offices, hospitals, schools — almost always have audible HVAC systems. This is one of the most common and least solvable audio problems in field production. Your options, in order of effectiveness:

  1. Turn it off. Ask the facilities contact whether the HVAC can be disabled for 20–30 minutes. Many systems can be temporarily switched off at a local thermostat. Confirm it will not restart automatically mid-interview.
  2. Move the microphone closer to the subject. Every time you halve the distance between the mic and the subject's mouth, you increase the relative level of their voice compared to the room noise. Closer is always better for noise rejection.
  3. Use a directional microphone. A hypercardioid or shotgun mic aimed at the subject's mouth has significant rejection off its sides and rear. Position the mic so that the HVAC duct is in the mic's null zone (usually directly to the sides or rear).
  4. Accept the noise floor and document it. Note the issue in your shot log. Record room tone — 30 seconds of the HVAC noise alone at the end of the session. This gives the editor reference material for noise reduction in post.

Clothing Rustle on a Lavalier

A lavalier mic that rubs against fabric or jewellery produces a low-frequency rumble and rustle that is extremely distracting. Addressing it requires physical intervention on the mic placement:

  • Move the clip position — a mic clipped at the lapel edge where the jacket moves will rustle; a mic placed at the centre of the sternum where fabric does not move will be quieter.
  • Use medical tape (Transpore or similar) to secure both the cable and the mic capsule against the fabric, preventing any relative movement.
  • If the subject is wearing a stiff or noisy fabric (taffeta, nylon, heavily starched cotton), tuck the mic under a layer rather than clipping it on the surface, or suggest they change — tactfully and privately (see Lesson 6).
  • For wireless lav setups, ensure the cable between the mic and the transmitter pack is not hanging loose. Any loose cable can swing against clothing or the body and transfer movement noise to the capsule.

Distorted or Clipped Audio

Audio clipping — when the signal exceeds the recorder's maximum input level — produces a harsh, crackling distortion that cannot be fixed in post. If you notice clipping while recording:

  1. Reduce the input gain immediately. Even mid-sentence, a gain reduction is less destructive than continued clipping.
  2. Note the take as potentially compromised in your shot log.
  3. For the next take, set your gain so that the subject's loudest moments (a laugh, a name or word they emphasise) peak no higher than -12 dBFS, with the average dialogue sitting around -18 to -12 dBFS. This leaves headroom for unexpected peaks.
  4. If the camera allows it, enable a safety track at -10 to -12 dB below the main track. This second, quieter recording serves as insurance against unexpected peaks.

Echo and Reverb

Large, hard-surfaced rooms (glass offices, foyers, gymnasiums) produce significant reverb that makes speech intelligible but difficult to follow. The most effective fix is always to get the microphone physically closer to the subject's mouth — this increases direct sound relative to reflected sound. Beyond that:

  • Move soft goods into the room if available: a couch, curtains, carpet, or even your equipment bags placed around the shooting area will absorb reflections.
  • Reposition the interview so that the subject is facing a soft, absorptive surface rather than a reflective one.
  • If reverb remains severe, consider whether this location can be changed. A reverberant room is far more damaging to audio usability than a less visually interesting room with good acoustics.

No Audio on Playback

You finish the shoot, review the footage, and hear nothing. This is one of the most distressing outcomes on a production. Common causes and prevention:

  • Recording to the wrong track. Some cameras have multiple audio channels. Confirm during your pre-roll test that the track you are recording to is the track that plays back during review.
  • Wireless mic in standby mode. Some transmitters enter a power-saving mode after a period without sound. Verify the transmitter is in record mode, not standby.
  • Input source mismatch. The camera was recording from the internal mic or a 3.5mm input while the XLR mic was connected but not selected. Roll a test before every setup change.

The only reliable prevention is a deliberate, monitored pre-roll test at the start of every setup. Roll 30 seconds before the subject arrives, listen through headphones, play back that 30 seconds and confirm audio is present. This one habit prevents the most catastrophic audio failures.

Key Takeaways

  • Always monitor through headphones — meters show level, not quality.
  • No signal? Check power, cable connections, phantom power, and gain in that order before assuming hardware failure.
  • Ground loop buzz is a steady power tone; RF interference is irregular or digital-sounding — the fix for each is different.
  • Moving the mic closer to the subject is the most effective response to HVAC noise, reverb, and most ambient noise problems.
  • Clipped audio cannot be repaired; reduce gain immediately and flag the take.
