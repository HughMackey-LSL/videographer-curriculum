Eye Line and Background Control

~10 min read 5 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Explain what eye line is and why it matters in interview production.
  • Position an interviewer correctly relative to the lens to achieve a natural on-screen gaze.
  • Identify what makes a background work versus what makes it distract.
  • Apply three techniques for managing a problematic background on location.
  • Make smart decisions when you cannot move the camera or the subject.

What Is Eye Line?

Eye line is where your subject's eyes are directed when they are speaking on camera. In interview production, this has nothing to do with makeup — it refers to the angle and direction of the subject's gaze relative to the lens.

There are two broad categories: the subject looks directly into the lens (used in news and direct-address formats), or the subject looks just off-lens toward an interviewer (used in documentary and most editorial interview formats). For most of the work you will do, the subject will be looking slightly off-camera — toward the person asking questions — and that gaze needs to look natural and deliberate on screen.

Positioning the Interviewer

The interviewer's physical position determines where the subject's eyes go, which determines how the shot feels on screen. The closer the interviewer sits to the lens axis, the more directly the subject appears to address the camera. The further the interviewer moves to the side, the more the subject appears to be looking past camera — which can feel evasive or disengaged if the angle is too extreme.

The standard position: the interviewer sits directly beside the camera, as close to the lens as physically possible without actually being in the shot. Their eye level should be roughly the same as the camera lens. When the subject looks at the interviewer, their gaze appears to land just off-camera — natural, engaged, and comfortable.

Common Mistake

Placing the interviewer too far to the side — say, three feet away from the camera — causes the subject to look at a sharp off-axis angle. On screen, this reads as the subject staring at something beside the camera rather than talking to someone in the room. The further off-axis, the more awkward it looks. When in doubt, have the interviewer pull their chair as close to the camera as possible.

The Effect of Interviewer Height

Where the interviewer sits vertically — and therefore where the subject's gaze angle goes — changes the screen gaze. If the interviewer sits much lower than the camera, the subject's eyes will angle slightly downward on screen. If the interviewer sits higher than the camera, the subject's eyes will angle upward, which can look like they are searching the ceiling for answers rather than speaking to someone.

The rule of thumb: the interviewer's eyes should be at roughly the same height as the camera lens. This produces a gaze that reads as horizontally level — natural and direct. Adjust the interviewer's chair height as part of your setup, not as an afterthought.

What Makes a Background Work

A good background does one or more of the following: it adds contextual information relevant to the subject's story, it provides visual texture that creates depth, or it is neutral enough to stay out of the way entirely. What it never does is compete with the subject for attention.

Backgrounds with gentle texture — a bookshelf, a plant, a softly lit wall — work because they register as interesting without drawing the eye away from the face. Backgrounds with depth — where the camera is positioned so the subject is in the foreground and the background recedes — work because they create a three-dimensional feel that flat backgrounds lack.

Contextually relevant backgrounds add story: a doctor interviewed in a clinical space, a chef in a kitchen, an engineer in front of a machine. These backgrounds do work without the editor needing to explain where the subject is.

Backgrounds That Compete and Distract

Certain backgrounds consistently cause problems. Once you learn to spot them, you will be able to address them before rolling:

  • Bright windows: A window behind your subject will blow out to white and create a silhouette unless you are controlling exposure specifically for it. Even if exposed correctly, the bright area behind the subject competes with the face.
  • Clutter: Messy backgrounds — stacked boxes, open office spaces with visual chaos, piles of anything — read as unprofessional and distract the eye. They also date a video if visible branding or products are included unintentionally.
  • People walking through: A background with foot traffic is a problem in disguise. A person walking behind the subject at the wrong moment will pull every viewer's eye away from the interview, regardless of how important the content is.
  • Visible text and signage: Signs, screens, posters, and logos in the background create legal and editorial complications. Signage with readable text distracts viewers. Visible brand logos may require clearances you do not have.
  • Visual competition: Bright colors, strong geometric shapes, and high-contrast patterns that sit directly behind the subject's head compete with the face for the viewer's attention.
Tip

When scouting a location, take out your phone and photograph potential backgrounds from camera height. What looks fine when you are standing and scanning the room often looks very different through a lens — especially once you frame the shot and see what the actual camera angle captures. The phone photo shows you what the camera will see before you commit.

Managing a Bad Background

You will not always have the luxury of a perfect background. On location, you work with what is there. Here are three practical strategies:

Reposition the camera angle. Small changes in camera position can dramatically change the background. Moving the camera two steps to the left might shift the background from a cluttered wall to a clean one. Moving closer to the subject and increasing focal length throws the background further out of focus.

Use depth and aperture. The wider your aperture (lower f-number), the more out of focus the background becomes. A background that looks distracting in sharp focus often reads as soft, pleasant texture when properly defocused. Getting the subject physically further from the background also increases the separation.

Dress the background. On shoots where you have setup time, you can add elements — a plant, a simple fabric panel, portable lighting aimed at a background wall — to improve it. Many professional interview setups use portable background stands precisely for this reason.

When You Cannot Move

Sometimes the location is fixed and you have no ability to reposition, redress, or change the background. In that case, your tools are lens choice, aperture, and subject distance. Move the subject as far forward from the background as the space allows. Open your aperture as wide as your exposure permits. Choose a longer focal length if you have the room to back the camera up — telephoto lenses compress depth and blur backgrounds more aggressively than wide lenses at the same aperture.

If none of that resolves the problem, be honest in your shot notes: flag the background issue so the editor and producer know what they are working with. A difficult background that is acknowledged is far easier to manage in post than one that surprises the edit team.

Key Takeaways

  • Eye line is controlled by the interviewer's position; keep them as close to the lens as possible, at lens height.
  • Off-axis interviewer placement creates awkward screen gazes — the closer to the lens, the more natural the result.
  • Good backgrounds add texture or context without competing with the subject's face.
  • Bright windows, clutter, foot traffic, and visible signage are consistent background problems to solve before rolling.
  • When you cannot move, use aperture, focal length, and subject distance to manage the background optically.
