Interview Framing

~10 min read 5 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Identify and correctly execute the three standard interview framings.
  • Explain what each framing communicates to the viewer emotionally and contextually.
  • Set up a two-camera interview so both angles are editable.
  • Create variety in a single-camera interview without moving the subject.
  • Treat the interview frame as a deliberate editorial choice rather than a default.

The Three Standard Interview Framings

Every interview you shoot will call for a framing decision. The three industry-standard options each communicate something different, and knowing which one to use — and why — is one of the most practical skills you can develop.

Medium Shot (Waist Up)

A medium shot shows the subject from roughly the waist up. You see their hands, their posture, and the way their body is oriented in space. This framing is warm and conversational. It places the subject in their environment and feels approachable — like you are sitting across a table from them.

Use the medium shot when context matters: when the environment contributes to the story, when the subject's body language is part of what they are communicating, or when you want a relaxed, documentary-style feel. It is also your widest option, which means the background plays a larger role. Make sure that background is doing something useful before you commit to this framing.

Medium Close-Up (Chest Up)

The medium close-up — chest or collarbone up to just above the head — is the workhorse of professional interview production. It is the most common interview framing for a reason: it is focused enough to feel engaged, but not so tight that it feels aggressive. The viewer's attention is drawn to the face and eyes without the environment dropping away entirely.

When in doubt, this is your default. It is versatile, edits cleanly, accommodates lower-third graphics, and works across almost every interview context from corporate to documentary to news.

Tight Close-Up (Chin to Just Above Head)

A tight close-up fills the frame almost entirely with the face — from just below the chin to just above the top of the head. At this framing, every microexpression is visible. The viewer feels close, almost uncomfortably so. That emotional intensity is powerful, but it is not sustainable for long stretches.

Use the tight close-up for moments of high emotion, a key revelation, or a quote that deserves extra weight. Cutting to it at the right moment can be one of the most effective tools an editor has — but only if the operator captured it. As the camera operator, think about when in the interview those moments might arrive, and be ready to offer that framing as an option.

Tip

When framing any interview shot, check that you have enough room at the bottom of the frame for a lower-third graphic. For a medium close-up, the subject's collar or upper chest should be visible at the bottom of frame — that gives the editor a clean landing zone for text overlays without covering the face.

Two-Camera Interview Setups

When you have two cameras available, you have the opportunity to create coverage that makes the editor's job much easier and the final product more dynamic. The key requirement is that the two framings must be different enough to cut between cleanly.

What does "different enough" mean in practice? If Camera A is shooting a medium close-up and Camera B is shooting a slightly wider medium close-up — say, they are only a zoom click apart — the cut between them will feel like a jump cut. The subject's size in frame barely changes, but their head position shifts slightly, creating a jarring visual stutter.

The standard approach: Camera A shoots a medium close-up (the primary interview framing), and Camera B shoots either a medium shot for variety or a tight close-up for emotional moments. These two framings are visually distinct enough that cutting between them reads as intentional and clean.

Common Mistake

Positioning both cameras at nearly the same focal length and distance is one of the most common two-camera interview errors. If the cameras are on the same side of the subject and at similar zoom levels, the editor cannot cut between them without a disorienting pop. Always establish a clear size difference — at least one full framing category apart.

The Interview Frame as a Deliberate Choice

Before you start rolling, ask yourself: what does this interview need to communicate? A CEO talking about company vision might call for a medium shot that places them in a recognizable office environment — location tells part of the story. A crime victim recounting a difficult experience might call for a tight close-up that centres their emotion and strips away distracting context. A scientist explaining a process might benefit from a medium close-up that keeps their face readable while leaving room for graphics.

The framing is not just technical — it shapes how the viewer receives everything the subject says. A tight frame says "this matters; pay close attention." A medium frame says "this is a conversation; you are here with us." Once you start thinking this way, every interview setup becomes an editorial decision, not just a camera position.

Creating Variety with One Camera

Single-camera interviews are more common than two-camera setups, especially in run-and-gun or documentary work. The challenge is giving the editor something to work with beyond a single locked framing for the entire interview.

One practical approach: shoot the entire interview at your primary framing, then — after the interview is done — ask the subject to repeat one or two of their key answers while you reframe to a different size. Editors call this "getting a different size for coverage." Many subjects are happy to do this, and it gives the editor the ability to use a tight cut during an emotional moment even if you only had one camera.

Another option: if the interview is long and you have a natural break (the subject is drinking water, you are adjusting audio), quietly reframe to a slightly different size and shoot the remainder of the interview from there. The editor can use the reframe as a natural cut point if needed.

Key Takeaways

  • The three interview framings — medium, medium close-up, tight close-up — each carry a different emotional weight and editorial purpose.
  • The medium close-up is the professional default; use it unless you have a reason to do otherwise.
  • Two-camera interviews require framings that are at least one full category apart to edit cleanly.
  • Even with a single camera, you can create coverage by reframing after the interview ends and re-shooting key answers.
  • Every framing decision shapes how the viewer receives the subject's words — treat it as an editorial choice from the start.
