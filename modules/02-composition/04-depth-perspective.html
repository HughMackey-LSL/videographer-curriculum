Depth, Perspective, and Camera Height

~10 min read 5 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Use foreground, midground, and background elements to add visual depth to a frame.
  • Explain how lens choice affects perspective and spatial feel.
  • Describe the emotional effect of camera height: eye level, low angle, and high angle.
  • Recognize perspective distortion from wide lenses used close to a subject.
  • Apply telephoto compression and negative space as deliberate compositional tools.

Foreground, Midground, and Background

A flat image is one where everything exists on a single visual plane — the subject and background appear to occupy the same space. A deep image uses three distinct layers — foreground, midground, and background — and the separation between those layers creates a sense of three-dimensional space even on a two-dimensional screen.

Foreground elements are objects placed between the camera and the subject. They might be slightly blurred and visible at the edge of the frame — a leaf, a window frame, a shoulder, a piece of furniture. Even a blurred foreground object tells the viewer's visual system that space exists between camera and subject, and that alone makes the image feel more cinematic and immersive.

The midground is where your subject typically lives. The background recedes behind them. When all three layers are present and distinct, the frame has visual weight that a flat setup simply cannot achieve.

Tip

When you arrive at a location and think about where to place the camera, actively look for foreground elements you can include. A branch, a doorframe, a piece of equipment in the foreground — even slightly visible and out of focus — can transform a flat, two-plane composition into something with real depth. You do not need to plan it in advance; you just need to train your eye to see it.

How Lens Choice Affects Perspective

The focal length of your lens changes how the camera represents spatial relationships. This is one of the most misunderstood concepts in camera operation, but once you feel the difference in the viewfinder, you will not forget it.

Wide-angle lenses (roughly 16mm to 35mm equivalent) exaggerate depth. Objects close to the lens appear large; objects in the background appear small and far away. The visual distance between foreground and background is stretched. Spaces feel bigger. Wide lenses are excellent for establishing shots, environmental portraits, and any situation where you want to convey the scale of a space.

Telephoto lenses (roughly 85mm and above) compress depth. Foreground and background appear to be much closer together than they actually are. A subject standing 20 feet in front of a building will look like they are nearly touching it when shot on a telephoto. This compression makes backgrounds feel closer and more enveloping, and it creates a pleasing separation of subject from background when combined with a wide aperture.

Perspective Distortion in Interviews

Wide-angle lenses used close to a subject produce perspective distortion — the parts of the subject closest to the lens appear disproportionately large. In interview situations, this means a nose can appear too prominent, a forehead too wide, or a face generally unflattering in ways that immediately register to viewers without them understanding why.

As a general rule: do not shoot interviews on a focal length wider than 35mm equivalent, and prefer 50mm to 85mm for most interview work. If you are in a tight space and need to fit a medium close-up but only have room for a short camera distance, resist the temptation to zoom wide. Either find more room, or accept a tighter framing at the correct focal length.

Common Mistake

Using a wide-angle lens to compensate for a cramped shooting space is one of the fastest ways to create an unflattering interview. If you shoot a medium close-up at 24mm equivalent with the camera three feet from the subject, the subject's face will distort noticeably. Back the camera up and use a longer focal length — the subject will look more natural, and the background will compress more pleasingly behind them.

Camera Height and Emotional Effect

Where you place the camera vertically relative to your subject changes the viewer's relationship to the subject on an emotional level. This is not subtle — it works on viewers without them realizing it, and it is one of the strongest tools you have.

Eye level places the lens at the same height as the subject's eyes. This is the neutral position. It puts the viewer on equal footing with the subject. Most interviews default to eye level because it is comfortable, direct, and non-manipulative. It says: "Here is a person. Listen to them."

Below eye level (low angle) places the camera below the subject, looking up. The subject appears taller, more powerful, more authoritative. Villains and heroes both get low-angle shots for this reason — it magnifies them. In interview work, a subtle low angle can make a subject seem more commanding, but an extreme low angle looks stylized and may read as deliberate rather than naturalistic.

Above eye level (high angle) places the camera above the subject, looking down. The subject appears smaller, more vulnerable, less powerful. This can be useful for conveying diminishment or vulnerability — a subject recounting trauma, for example — but it can also simply make a subject look weak when that was not the intent. High angles in interviews need a strong reason.

Using Telephoto Compression Intentionally

Telephoto compression is a tool, not a side effect. When you back the camera up and zoom in to shoot your subject with a longer focal length, the background moves visually closer behind the subject. This is useful in several ways: it makes a plain background feel richer because more of it fills the frame behind the subject, it allows you to use bokeh (background blur) more aggressively at the same aperture, and it gives the overall image a polished, professional look that shorter focal lengths at close distances cannot replicate.

Negative Space as a Compositional Tool

Negative space is the empty area in a frame — sky, a blank wall, open floor, open air around a subject. Rather than filling every corner of the frame with visual information, negative space gives the subject room to exist and the viewer's eye a place to rest.

Used deliberately, negative space can be extraordinarily powerful. A subject placed small in a wide landscape conveys isolation. A figure against a vast expanse of sky conveys smallness in relation to the world. Even in interview work, a subject placed on one third of the frame with open space on the other side can feel more thoughtful and considered than a tightly cropped shot where every edge of the frame is occupied.

The difference between effective negative space and wasted frame space is intention. If you can answer the question "what does this empty area tell the viewer?" — you have made a compositional decision.

Key Takeaways

  • Using foreground, midground, and background elements together creates visual depth that flat compositions cannot achieve.
  • Wide lenses exaggerate depth and can distort faces; telephoto lenses compress depth and are more flattering for interview subjects.
  • Camera height is an emotional tool: eye level is neutral, low angle empowers, high angle diminishes.
  • Never use a wide-angle lens close to an interview subject — back up and zoom in instead.
  • Negative space and telephoto compression are deliberate compositional choices, not accidents or limitations.
