Lenses and Focal Length

~12 min read 5 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Understand how focal length affects field of view and visual perspective.
  • Explain how crop factor changes the effective focal length on different sensor sizes.
  • Compare zoom lenses and prime lenses for typical institutional video work.
  • Understand perspective distortion and telephoto compression — and why they matter for people.
  • Build a practical lens kit for interview and event production.

Focal Length and Field of View

Focal length, measured in millimeters, describes the optical distance from the lens's point of convergence to the image sensor. In practical terms, it determines how wide or narrow your field of view is — how much of the scene the camera captures — and it has a profound effect on the visual character of the image.

A wide angle lens (roughly below 35mm on a full-frame camera) captures a broad view of the scene, showing more of the environment around the subject. Wide lenses are useful for establishing shots, architecture, and confined spaces where you cannot step back far enough to frame with a longer lens. They also exaggerate the sense of distance between objects — a subject 3 feet from the camera looks far from a background 10 feet away.

A normal lens (around 35–50mm on full-frame) approximates the perspective of the human eye. Objects look neither unnaturally large nor unnaturally small relative to each other. These focal lengths produce images that feel natural and unforced — a quality that makes them extremely useful for conversation, documentary, and corporate work where you want the viewer to feel present without drawing attention to the lens choice.

A telephoto lens (above 70mm on full-frame) narrows the field of view, bringing distant subjects closer. More importantly, telephoto lenses compress the apparent distance between objects in the frame — the subject and the background look closer together than they actually are. This compression is generally flattering for faces and useful for event coverage where you are working at a distance from your subjects.

Sensor Size and Crop Factor

The focal length printed on a lens is based on a full-frame sensor (the same size as a 35mm film frame). If your camera uses a smaller sensor — called a crop sensor or APS-C — the sensor captures only the center portion of the image the lens projects, which effectively narrows the field of view. This is described as a crop factor or multiplier.

A common APS-C crop factor is 1.5x (Sony, Nikon) or 1.6x (Canon). This means a 50mm lens mounted on an APS-C camera behaves like a 75mm (Sony) or 80mm (Canon) lens in terms of field of view. This can work in your favor — a 50mm lens on a crop camera gives you a flattering portrait focal length — but it can also be limiting if you need a true wide angle and your widest lens becomes a normal lens on the crop sensor.

Note

When comparing focal lengths across different cameras, always think in terms of "full-frame equivalent" focal length. A 24mm lens on a Micro Four Thirds camera (2x crop) behaves like a 48mm lens in terms of field of view — not a wide angle at all. Crop factor affects your lens choices significantly when building a kit.

Zoom Lenses vs. Prime Lenses

A zoom lens covers a range of focal lengths — a 24–70mm zoom goes from moderate wide to moderate telephoto with a twist of the zoom ring. This versatility is enormously practical for documentary and event work where you cannot change lenses and may need to reframe quickly without moving the camera. A single versatile zoom lens can replace three or four prime lenses in a run-and-gun kit.

The tradeoffs of zoom lenses are aperture and optical quality. Most zoom lenses have a smaller maximum aperture than fast prime lenses — f/2.8 is considered an excellent maximum aperture for a zoom, while prime lenses commonly reach f/1.4 or f/1.8. Zoom lenses also tend to have more optical aberrations (distortion, chromatic aberration, vignetting) at their extreme ends, though this has improved significantly in modern designs.

A prime lens has a single fixed focal length — a 50mm prime is always 50mm. To change your framing, you move the camera or change lenses. This constraint sounds limiting, but it has a distinct advantage: prime lenses are optically simpler and can be designed with very wide maximum apertures at lower cost. A 50mm f/1.4 prime lens costs significantly less than a 24–70mm f/2.8 zoom while letting in more light and often producing a sharper, more cinematically pleasing image.

Perspective Distortion

One of the most practically important things to understand about focal length is how it affects the appearance of faces. Wide angle lenses close to a subject produce perspective distortion — the nose, being closer to the lens than the ears and the rest of the face, appears disproportionately large. The face looks wider, the features exaggerated. This is unflattering for portraits and can make interview subjects look subtly strange even if they cannot articulate why.

The solution is to use a longer focal length and step back. A 70–100mm equivalent focal length on a full-frame camera, positioned further from the subject, produces essentially zero perspective distortion — the face looks the way it looks in person. This is the main reason experienced videographers default to 50–100mm equivalent focal lengths for interview work rather than framing tight with a wide lens.

Common Mistake

Zooming out or switching to a wide lens and moving close to fill the frame looks the same as zooming in from further away on the screen, but the perspective is entirely different. Close + wide = distorted face; far + telephoto = natural, flattering face. Always prefer the latter for people.

Aperture in Lenses: f-stop vs T-stop

The f-stop marked on a lens is a calculated ratio based on the lens's focal length and aperture diameter. It describes the geometric opening accurately, but it does not account for light lost to absorption and reflection inside the lens. A cinema lens rating system called T-stops (transmission stops) measures actual light transmission — accounting for every piece of glass the light passes through. Two f/2.8 lenses may transmit slightly different amounts of light, but both T2.9 lenses transmit identically.

T-stops matter in cinema production where multiple camera angles must match exposure precisely. For most institutional video work with a single camera, f-stops are fine. But if you ever work on a production that mixes footage from cameras with different lenses and needs to match exposure for color grading, T-stops become relevant.

Autofocus Performance by Lens Type

Not all lenses autofocus equally well, especially in video. Lenses designed for still photography often use slower stepper motors or linear motors that produce audible noise during focus pulls — noise that may be picked up by the camera's built-in microphone. Lenses designed for video use fast, quiet linear motors or ultrasonic motors optimized for smooth, silent focus transitions. If you rely on AF-C during interviews, investing in video-optimized lenses (such as Sony G Master or Canon L-series RF lenses) is worth the cost.

A Practical Lens Kit for Institutional Video

If you are building a kit for corporate, documentary, and event work, a two-lens approach covers most situations effectively. A versatile zoom — something like a 24–70mm f/2.8 — handles establishing shots, group coverage, and the flexibility needed when you cannot predict subject position. A single fast prime — a 50mm f/1.4 or 85mm f/1.8 — provides the wide aperture needed for low-light interviews and the flattering focal length for close portrait work. This two-piece kit is compact, effective, and more affordable than a full set of primes.

Tip

Before investing in a second or third lens, master the one you already own. Many operators use a single 24–70mm zoom for 90% of their work and only reach for specialty glass when a specific creative or technical need arises. Knowing your lens's limitations is more valuable than having more lenses you know less well.

Key Takeaways

  • Focal length determines field of view (wide vs. narrow) and the perspective of the image — how near and far objects appear relative to each other.
  • Crop sensors multiply the effective focal length of any lens. Always think in full-frame equivalent terms when comparing focal lengths across cameras.
  • Wide lenses close to people cause perspective distortion — exaggerated noses and wider-looking faces. Use 50–100mm equivalent focal lengths for flattering portrait work.
  • Telephoto lenses compress background distance and are flattering for faces and useful for event work at a distance.
  • A zoom plus one fast prime covers most institutional video situations. Master what you have before expanding the kit.
