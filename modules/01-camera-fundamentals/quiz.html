Question 1

You are shooting a training video at 24fps. According to the 180-degree shutter rule, what shutter speed should you use?

The 180-degree shutter rule states that shutter speed should be double the frame rate. At 24fps, that means 1/48 — the closest available setting on most cameras is 1/50. This produces the natural motion blur that makes footage look cinematic rather than strobing or smeared.
Question 2

You raise the ISO from 400 to 3200. What is the primary side effect of this change?

ISO amplifies the electrical signal from the sensor to make the image brighter, but amplification always amplifies imperfections along with the signal. This produces random noise — grain-like specks of color and brightness — particularly visible in darker areas of the image. Higher ISO = more noise.
Question 3

Which aperture setting produces the shallowest depth of field and would make the background most blurred?

Aperture f-stop numbers are counterintuitive: a lower number means a wider opening, which lets in more light and produces shallower depth of field. f/1.8 is the widest (most open) aperture of the options listed, producing the least depth of field and the most background blur.
Question 4

When is it most appropriate to shoot log (e.g., S-Log, C-Log) rather than a standard Rec.709 picture profile?

Log gamma is a tool for post-production workflows, not a shortcut. It only makes sense when you have 10-bit recording to support the color data, a monitoring LUT so you can evaluate exposure accurately on set, and a color grading step in post where the flat footage will be transformed. Without all three, a well-configured standard profile produces a better result.
Question 5

What is the key difference between continuous autofocus (AF-C) and manual focus for video production?

Modern AF-C with face/eye detection is highly capable and genuinely useful for solo operators, but it has specific failure modes: low contrast scenes, low light, multiple subjects, extreme shallow depth of field. Manual focus requires skill and practice but gives you total control. Professional operators know both and choose based on the situation.
Question 6

Zebras are set to 95 IRE on your camera and stripes appear on a white wall in the background of your interview shot. What should you do?

Zebras at 95 IRE warn you that those areas are very close to clipping — bright white with potential detail loss. The correct response is to reduce overall exposure, or address the lighting so the background is not so much brighter than the subject. Raising the threshold just hides the warning without fixing the problem. Blurring the background does not affect its exposure.
Question 7

You want to create slow-motion footage that will play back at 24fps in your editing timeline. What frame rate should you record at?

Slow motion is achieved by recording more frames per second than the delivery timeline requires. At 60fps played back in a 24fps timeline, the camera captured 60 frames of action in one second but the timeline displays only 24 of them per second — so one second of action takes 2.5 seconds to play back. This is true slow motion without software interpolation.
Question 8

You are shooting an indoor interview under tungsten lights. The correct Kelvin value to set for white balance is approximately:

Tungsten and incandescent light sources produce light at approximately 2800–3200 Kelvin — the warm orange light of a traditional light bulb or film lighting instrument. Setting white balance to 3200K tells the camera to treat that warm orange light as neutral white, producing correctly balanced colors. 5600K is daylight; 7500K is shade; 10000K is an extreme blue sky.
Question 9

For a corporate interview shoot, your lens kit consists of a 24–70mm f/2.8 zoom and an 85mm f/1.8 prime. At what focal length should you frame a close single-shot interview on a full-frame camera?

The 50–100mm range (full-frame equivalent) is the standard for interview work because it produces natural perspective — the face looks proportional, with no exaggerated nose from a wide lens at close range. The 70–85mm range with a subject at conversational distance creates a slightly compressed, flattering look. Wide lenses close to faces cause perspective distortion that is visually unflattering even if viewers cannot identify why.
Question 10

Your histogram shows most pixel values compressed toward the left side of the graph, with very little data on the right. The image appears very dark on the monitor. What is the correct response?

A histogram crowded to the left indicates significant underexposure — the image is too dark. While slight underexposure can be lifted in grading, severe underexposure produces noisy footage and crushed shadows with no detail. The correct response is to increase exposure until the distribution is centered, while monitoring the right edge to avoid clipping highlights. Raising ISO to the maximum overcorrects and adds unnecessary noise.
