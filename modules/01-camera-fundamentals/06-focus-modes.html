Focus Modes

~11 min read 5 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the difference between manual focus and continuous autofocus.
  • Understand how face and eye detection autofocus works and when it fails.
  • Use focus peaking and magnification to verify critical focus.
  • Identify situations where autofocus is unreliable and manual focus is preferable.
  • Apply a practical approach: starting with autofocus and knowing how to override it.

Why Focus Is Different in Video

In photography, a slightly soft image can still be usable — viewers forgive a small focus miss in a still frame. In video, soft focus is immediately obvious and often fatal to a shot. Viewers watching moving footage can see blur and sharpness in real time, and out-of-focus video is one of the clearest signals of an inexperienced operator. Getting focus right — and keeping it right through a take — is one of the most technically demanding parts of professional video production.

You have two fundamental approaches: let the camera's autofocus system do the work, or control the focus ring yourself. Neither is always superior. The best operators know both well enough to switch between them mid-shoot as the situation demands.

Manual Focus

Manual focus means turning the focus ring on the lens by hand until the image is sharp, and holding it there throughout the shot. This gives you complete control — the camera will not hunt, breathe, or suddenly shift focus to the wrong subject — but it requires skill, practice, and good monitoring tools.

The technique for manual focus in video is distinct from photography. You turn the ring slowly and deliberately, paying close attention to the sharpness of specific edges in the frame (usually the subject's eyes). Small adjustments — a quarter turn, an eighth turn — make a significant difference, especially at wide apertures. Develop a feel for which direction tightens focus on near subjects versus far subjects on your specific lens, because lenses vary. What feels like muscle memory to an experienced operator is genuinely something that has to be practiced on each piece of glass you use regularly.

Manual focus is the professional choice in predictable, controlled situations: a locked-off interview where the subject is sitting still, a scripted scene where the subject's position is marked, or any situation where the autofocus behavior would be unreliable or distracting. On a narrative film set, everything is pulled manually by a dedicated focus puller. For institutional video with one operator, the choice is more nuanced.

Continuous Autofocus (AF-C)

Continuous autofocus (called AF-C on most cameras) means the camera is constantly analyzing the image and adjusting the focus to keep the selected area sharp. Modern mirrorless cameras — particularly Sony, Canon, and Panasonic flagship models — have dramatically advanced AF-C systems that use phase-detection pixels embedded in the sensor and machine-learning algorithms to identify and track subjects with impressive accuracy.

When AF-C is working well, it is genuinely remarkable. A Sony A7S III or FX3 with face/eye detection engaged can track a moving subject through a complex scene, maintain sharp focus on their eyes even as their head turns, and handle the kinds of unpredictable subject movement that would challenge a skilled manual focus puller. For a solo operator handling camera, sound, and interview simultaneously, reliable AF-C is a significant practical advantage.

Note

AF-C speed and "breathiness" — the slight in-and-out focus hunting that can appear while the system tracks — can usually be configured in the camera's AF settings. Slowing the AF sensitivity and adjusting tracking sensitivity reduces hunting at the cost of slower reaction to sudden changes. For interview work, a slower, more deliberate AF-C response is generally preferable to a fast, reactive one.

Face and Eye Detection

Face/eye detection is a specific autofocus mode where the camera prioritizes human faces (and specifically the nearest eye) over any other element in the frame. When it works, it is the most practical autofocus mode for interview and documentary work — it locks onto the person, follows them if they lean in or out, and switches automatically between eyes as the subject's head turns.

Understanding when it fails is equally important. Face detection struggles in low-contrast lighting conditions where the face does not separate cleanly from the background. It can lose lock on a subject wearing glasses if the lens flares on the glass, or on a subject wearing a face covering. When multiple people are in the frame, the camera makes its own decision about which face to prioritize — which may not match what you intend. At wide apertures where depth of field is very shallow, the AF system can sometimes lock on an ear or a shoulder instead of the eye, because the focus plane is so thin that the nearest detected point is not always the eye. These failures are not bugs — they are the limits of current technology.

Tap to Focus

Many cameras allow you to tap the touchscreen to move the autofocus point to a specific location in the frame. This is useful for repositioning focus during a shot — tapping on the subject when the AF has drifted to the background, or directing the camera's attention to a specific person in a group. It is fast and intuitive, and on cameras with good AF systems, tapping to confirm a face or an eye works reliably. Make sure the touchscreen autofocus feature is enabled in your camera's AF settings.

Focus Peaking

Focus peaking is a display overlay that highlights the edges in the image that are currently in sharp focus with a colored fringe — typically red, yellow, or white. As you turn the focus ring, the peaking overlay shifts to show which parts of the image are sharpest. It is not perfectly precise — peaking responds to edge contrast rather than optical sharpness, which means it can trigger on high-contrast edges that are not actually in focus — but it is significantly better than trying to judge focus by eye alone on a small LCD screen.

Use white or yellow peaking for best visibility without being confused by skin tones (red peaking on a person's face can be hard to distinguish from the skin). Keep peaking sensitivity on a medium setting — too sensitive and everything edges glow, making it useless; too low and it only triggers on extremely sharp contrasts and misses subtle focus.

Focus Magnification (Punch-In)

Focus magnification zooms the display to 4x or more on a selected area, showing you a close-up view that makes focus accuracy much easier to judge. On most cameras, pressing a dedicated button or double-tapping the screen activates magnification; you can then pan around the magnified view to find the area you care about. This technique is ideal for confirming focus before a take rather than pulling focus during one — the magnified view hides too much of the frame to use while actually recording a documentary-style shot.

Tip

Use focus magnification to check focus before every interview take. Frame your shot, have your subject sit still for a moment, magnify to their eyes, confirm sharpness, return to full view, then roll. This three-second habit catches the majority of focus problems before they end up in the recording.

Common Autofocus Failure Scenarios

Even the best autofocus systems have predictable failure modes that you should know and plan around. Low contrast scenes — filming a person in front of a similarly-toned background — give the system little information to work with. Subjects that move unpredictably, turning their back to camera or walking out of frame, can cause the AF to hunt. Low light gives the sensor less detail to analyze, reducing accuracy. Shooting through glass, mesh, or other obstacles confuses the system. When any of these conditions are present, switching to manual focus and pre-focusing is often more reliable than fighting an AF system that is not performing well.

Pre-Focus Technique

For shots where you know your subject will be in a specific position — standing at a lectern, sitting in a chair, walking through a doorway — you can pre-focus manually to that position before the subject arrives. Place an object (or your own hand) at the position the subject will occupy, focus on it precisely using magnification and peaking, then lock the focus and remove the stand-in. When the subject arrives, they step into your pre-focused plane. This technique eliminates AF uncertainty entirely on predictable shots.

Common Mistake

Never assume autofocus found the right thing just because the focus indicator shows "locked." Always verify that the camera locked on the subject's eyes — not the background, not a bystander, and not an edge of the frame. Build the habit of looking at the focus confirmation indicator and visually verifying sharpness before you commit to recording a take.

Key Takeaways

  • Soft focus is immediately obvious in video and one of the clearest signs of an inexperienced operator. Getting and maintaining focus is a critical skill.
  • Manual focus gives you complete control with no hunting or drift — ideal for controlled, predictable situations.
  • Continuous AF with face/eye detection is highly capable on modern mirrorless cameras and a practical solution for solo operators in documentary and interview work.
  • Focus peaking highlights sharp edges to aid manual focus; focus magnification provides a zoomed view for precise focus confirmation before recording.
  • Know when AF will fail — low contrast, low light, obstacles, unpredictable subjects — and be ready to switch to manual focus in those situations.
