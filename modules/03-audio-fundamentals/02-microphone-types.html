Microphone Types

~10 min read 5 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Identify lavalier, shotgun, handheld, boundary, and camera-mounted microphones and describe when each is appropriate.
  • Explain the difference between wired and wireless lavalier systems.
  • Describe cardioid, supercardioid, and omnidirectional polar patterns and what each captures.
  • Select the appropriate microphone type for common institutional video scenarios.
  • Understand why the camera's built-in microphone is not a production solution.

Matching the Mic to the Job

There is no single microphone that works perfectly in every situation. The right choice depends on the recording environment, the distance from the subject, whether the mic can be visible in frame, how much movement the subject will make, and how much time you have to set up. Understanding the capabilities and limitations of each microphone type lets you make that choice quickly and confidently.

Lavalier (Lapel) Microphones

A lavalier — often called a lav — is a small microphone clipped to the subject's clothing, typically on the sternum 6 to 8 inches below the chin. Because the mic is positioned close to the mouth and moves with the subject, it captures clean, consistent dialogue regardless of where the person turns their head. This makes it the standard choice for sit-down interviews, documentary subjects, and any situation where a subject is speaking on camera for an extended period.

Lavs come in two forms. Wired lavs connect via a thin cable that runs from the mic, down through clothing, and out to the camera or audio recorder. They are reliable, require no batteries, and introduce no RF concerns — but the cable limits the subject's movement and must be routed carefully to avoid being visible. Wireless lavs use a small bodypack transmitter clipped to a belt or tucked into a pocket, sending the signal to a receiver connected to your camera or recorder. Wireless systems give subjects freedom to move but require charged batteries, careful frequency coordination to avoid interference, and more setup time.

Tip

For a standard sit-down interview where the subject will not be moving around, a wired lav is often the more reliable choice. You eliminate wireless interference as a variable and have one less battery to worry about. Save wireless for situations where freedom of movement genuinely matters.

Shotgun Microphones

A shotgun microphone is a long, highly directional mic that captures sound from a narrow angle in front of it and rejects sound from the sides and rear. It is mounted either on a boom pole held above the subject by an operator, or on a camera-mounted shoe. Shotguns are the standard tool for documentary and event work where you cannot place a lav on the subject — or where you want a second, backup audio source.

On a boom pole, a well-positioned shotgun can capture excellent dialogue from a meter or more away, as long as the operator keeps the mic pointed at the speaker's mouth. The boom operator role is a real skill; a poorly operated boom produces audio that wanders in quality as the mic drifts off-axis. On a camera mount, the shotgun is further from the subject and subject to camera handling noise, making it less ideal for primary dialogue capture but useful as a guide track or backup.

Handheld Microphones

Handheld mics are held by an interviewer, presenter, or subject and pointed toward the speaker's mouth. Because the mic is close to the mouth and visible in frame, they work well in situations where the mic being seen is acceptable or expected — news-style interviews, conference Q&A sessions, man-on-the-street formats, and run-and-gun news coverage. They are simple to set up and require no complex rigging, but they demand that someone actively hold and point the mic, and they do not work for situations where the mic must be hidden.

Boundary and Tabletop Microphones

Boundary microphones sit flat on a surface — a table, a podium, or a floor — and capture sound in the hemisphere above that surface. They are used for conference room recordings, panel discussions, and board meetings where multiple people are speaking and no one will be wired individually. Audio quality from a boundary mic is typically less intimate than a lav, and the mic captures everyone at the table equally, which can be either an advantage or a disadvantage depending on the situation.

Camera-Mounted Microphones

The microphone built into a camera body is not a production tool. It is positioned far from the subject, captures everything around the camera (including handling noise and ambient room sound), and typically has small capsules with limited frequency response. Use the on-camera mic only as a guide track — a scratch audio reference that helps you sync dual-system audio in post — or as emergency backup if all other options fail.

Common Mistake

Many beginners rely on the camera's built-in mic because setting up a separate microphone takes time and feels complicated. The result is interview audio full of room reverb and background noise that will sound amateurish alongside even modest b-roll. The camera mic is almost never acceptable as your primary audio source.

Polar Patterns

A microphone's polar pattern describes which directions it picks up sound from and how much it rejects sound from other directions. Understanding polar patterns helps you choose the right mic and position it correctly.

PatternWhat It CapturesCommon Use
OmnidirectionalSound equally from all directions — no rejectionLavalier mics (picks up subject regardless of head turn)
CardioidFront and sides, rejects rear — heart-shaped pickup zoneHandheld, general-purpose condenser mics
Supercardioid / HypercardioidNarrow front pickup, strong side rejection, small rear lobeShotgun microphones for boom or camera mount

Most lavalier microphones are omnidirectional, which means they do not need to be precisely aimed — they capture the subject's voice regardless of which way the subject turns. Shotguns use supercardioid or hypercardioid patterns to focus tightly on one direction and reject ambient sound from the sides, which is why placement and aiming are so important with a shotgun.

Practical Recommendation for Institutional Video

For most institutional video work — interviews, documentary segments, presentations — the following combination covers nearly all scenarios:

Primary tool: lavalier microphone. For any situation involving a single subject speaking on camera, a lav provides the most consistent, highest-quality dialogue capture. Use wired for stationary interviews, wireless for moving subjects or wider coverage areas.

Secondary tool: shotgun microphone. For events, conferences, and situations where you cannot or should not wire the subject, a shotgun on a boom or camera mount is your primary option. On critical shoots, run both a lav and a shotgun simultaneously — if one has a problem, the other saves the take.

Key Takeaways

  • Lavaliers capture clean, consistent dialogue from close range and are the standard choice for sit-down interviews; wired systems are more reliable, wireless systems offer freedom of movement.
  • Shotgun microphones are highly directional and suited for boom or camera-mount use when a lav cannot be placed on the subject.
  • Handheld mics work for formats where the mic being visible is acceptable; boundary mics cover conference room scenarios with multiple speakers.
  • The camera's built-in microphone is only acceptable as a guide track or emergency backup — never as a primary dialogue source.
  • Polar patterns determine what a mic hears: omni captures all directions, cardioid rejects the rear, and supercardioid/shotgun patterns focus narrowly in front while rejecting the sides.
