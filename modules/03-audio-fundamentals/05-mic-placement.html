Microphone Placement

~10 min read 5 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Place a lavalier microphone in the standard sternum position and route the cable correctly.
  • Identify fabrics and clothing types that cause noise problems and apply practical fixes.
  • Describe correct shotgun microphone positioning on a boom pole relative to the subject.
  • Explain why mic distance from the subject matters for both lavs and shotguns.
  • Identify potential placement problems by listening on headphones rather than by guessing.

Lavalier Placement: The Standard Position

The standard lavalier placement is on the center of the chest, roughly 6 to 8 inches below the chin. At this position, the mic captures the full resonance of the subject's voice — low enough to avoid the proximity effect of breathing, high enough to stay in line with the chest cavity where voice originates. The mic capsule should face upward, toward the mouth, not lie flat against the clothing or face downward.

Clip the lav to a shirt placket, lapel, tie, or any firm fabric edge near the center of the chest. Avoid clipping to soft, floppy material that will shift and create rustle. The cable should drape downward from the clip, not pull sideways, and should be routed under or along the inside of clothing to keep it out of frame and prevent it from being snagged. Leave a small loop of cable at the clip point — a "stress relief loop" — so that if the cable is pulled, the tension is absorbed by the loop rather than yanking the mic out of position.

Tip

Ask your subject to turn their head all the way left, then right, while you listen on headphones. This reveals whether the lav is positioned where their clothing movement will cause rustle when they naturally look around. Adjust now rather than discovering the problem in the middle of a take.

Concealment and Visible Lavs

Concealing the lavalier depends on the context. In documentary and institutional video, a visible lav — sitting on the outside of a shirt or jacket — is generally acceptable. Audiences understand that the subject is miked. In scripted content or interview setups where the speaker should appear "natural," concealment matters more.

For concealment, the mic is routed under clothing. The capsule can be placed at the neckline of an undershirt, behind the subject's tie (the tie creates a natural windscreen), or taped to the chest with medical-grade tape. Concealed placement requires more setup time and more care to avoid clothing rustle, but it produces a completely invisible mic in the finished video.

Clothing Noise: The Biggest Lav Problem

Clothing rustle is the most common problem with lavalier microphones and one of the most frustrating to deal with, because it sounds terrible and it hides until you are in the middle of a take. When fabric rubs against the mic capsule — or against other fabric near the mic — it produces a crackling, swishing sound that can completely obscure dialogue.

Some fabrics are significantly worse than others:

Fabric TypeNoise RiskNotes
Nylon, polyester, synthetic blendsHighCreates loud, persistent crackling — very difficult to work with
Stiff cotton (dress shirts)Medium–HighThe placket area can crinkle with movement
Soft cotton, jersey knitLowBest fabric for lav placement
Wool, fleeceMediumCan muffle the mic; place carefully
Leather, vinylHighSurface noise from any flex or crinkle

When you encounter a problematic fabric, several solutions are available. The simplest is to use a small piece of medical tape or specialized mic tape (Moleskin, Rycote Stickies) to secure the mic capsule to the fabric, preventing it from moving against the clothing. You can also place the mic between two fabric layers, or apply a small piece of Moleskin around the capsule to decouple it from direct fabric contact. On very noisy fabrics, the only reliable solution is to route the mic to a different placement position — a tie, an undershirt neckline, or a hair-line position if appropriate.

Common Mistake

Testing for clothing noise by asking the subject to sit perfectly still gives you no useful information. Ask them to move as they naturally would during the interview — gesturing with their hands, shifting in their seat, turning their head. Clothing noise appears with movement, not with stillness. Always test with movement while monitoring on headphones.

Shotgun Placement on a Boom Pole

A shotgun microphone on a boom pole captures the best audio when it is as close to the subject as possible without entering the frame. The standard position is above and slightly in front of the subject, angled down toward the mouth at approximately 30 to 45 degrees. This angle keeps the mic pointed at the sound source while placing it above the frame line.

The boom operator must maintain consistent positioning throughout the take. If the mic drifts back, the subject moves further off-axis, and the audio quality drops — it becomes thinner and more reverberant. If the boom dips into the shot, it ruins the take visually. The boom operator should watch the frame edge carefully and communicate with the camera operator about where the safe zone is.

A shotgun can also be positioned below the subject, pointing upward at the same angle, which works well in seated scenarios where the boom cannot be positioned above without catching in the frame. The tradeoff is that a low boom position may pick up more clothing rustle or table noise. Try both positions and listen on headphones to determine which sounds cleaner in a given setup.

Distance and the Inverse Square Law

The fundamental rule of microphone placement is simple: closer is better. As the distance from the sound source doubles, the sound level drops by 6 dB — this is the inverse square law. A microphone 30 cm from a subject captures a dramatically stronger, cleaner signal than a mic 60 cm away. The ratio of dialogue to background noise — the signal-to-noise ratio — improves significantly as you move the mic closer.

For a lav, this is automatic — it is 6 to 8 inches from the mouth. For a shotgun on a boom, you are working with more distance, which is why boom operators try to get the mic as close to the frame edge as possible without crossing it. Every additional foot of distance costs you signal quality.

Conference and Tabletop Scenarios

When wiring individual participants is not possible, place boundary microphones at the center of the conference table, close enough to capture all participants but avoiding placement directly in front of any HVAC vents or in positions where papers will be slid across the mic surface. In longer tables or larger groups, use two boundary mics and pan them slightly apart in the recording. Listen on headphones to confirm all seats around the table are being captured at acceptable levels before the meeting starts.

Key Takeaways

  • The standard lav position is 6–8 inches below the chin on the center of the chest, capsule facing upward — not pressed flat against clothing or facing sideways.
  • Clothing rustle is the most common lav problem; test with natural movement while monitoring on headphones, and use medical tape or Moleskin to secure the capsule against noisy fabrics.
  • A boom-mounted shotgun should be above and slightly in front of the subject, angled down at 30–45 degrees, kept as close to the subject as possible without entering the frame.
  • Closer is always better — as distance doubles, audio level drops 6 dB and background noise makes up a greater share of the recording.
  • Headphones are the only reliable way to confirm placement is correct — looking at the mic's position tells you much less than listening to what it actually hears.
