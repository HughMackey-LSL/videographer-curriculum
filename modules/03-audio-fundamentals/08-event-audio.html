Event Audio

~10 min read 5 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Explain why a direct feed from a venue's audio mixer produces better results than ambient capture.
  • Identify the types of outputs available from a house audio system and how to connect to each.
  • Describe proper etiquette when working with a house audio engineer.
  • Record simultaneous backup audio alongside a house feed for redundancy.
  • Understand the limitations of shotgun capture when no house feed is available.

Why a House Feed Is Always Better

When you film an event — a conference, a panel discussion, a corporate presentation, an award ceremony — the speakers are almost always amplified through the venue's sound system. That system has microphones on the speakers, those signals go through a professional mixing console, and the result comes out of speakers positioned throughout the room. The house audio engineer has already done the work of capturing the speech: microphones are on the right people at the right levels.

If you record that event by pointing a camera or a boom mic at the room, you are recording the loudspeaker output mixed with audience noise, room reverb, and whatever else is happening in the environment. You are getting a secondhand version of the audio that was already processed and amplified. The quality will be adequate at best and poor in most large rooms.

A direct feed from the mixer gives you the same signal the house audio engineer is working with — clean, mixed, at the right level, without the room's acoustics added on top. This is almost always the correct approach when one is available, and asking for it should be one of your first calls when you get a venue contact.

When connecting to a professional line-level output from a house mixer, you will need to attenuate the signal before it enters your camera's mic-level input — a line-level signal fed into a mic-level input will clip immediately. Either connect to a line-level input if your recorder has one, or use an in-line attenuator (pad) to reduce the signal before it enters a mic-level input. Set the camera's input gain to its lowest usable setting and confirm your levels before the event starts.

Direct Injection Boxes

A DI (direct injection) box converts a signal from one format to another — typically from an unbalanced instrument-level signal to a balanced XLR line signal, or from a consumer-level output to a professional signal. In event audio scenarios, a DI box is useful when the house system only has consumer-level or unbalanced outputs. Passive DI boxes require no power and handle the conversion simply. Active DI boxes include a preamp and can boost a weak signal, but they require a power source (battery or phantom power). Connect the house output to the DI box input, and the XLR output from the DI box connects to your recorder.

Tip

Carry a small DI box and a variety of adapter cables in your kit — XLR to 1/4" TRS, RCA to 3.5mm, 3.5mm to XLR. You will encounter different house systems at different venues, and having the right adapter means you can take a feed from almost any system. Not having adapters means you leave empty-handed.

Setting Levels from a House Feed

Do not assume the level from the house feed is appropriate for your recorder. Ask the audio engineer to do a microphone check — have someone speak into the stage microphone at presentation volume — while you watch your recorder's meters. Set your input gain so peaks land in the -12 to -6 dBFS range, the same target as any other dialogue recording. Ask the engineer not to change the feed level during the event without warning you; sudden level changes will require you to re-set your recording levels.

Request a "pre-fader" or "direct out" from the mixer if possible. A pre-fader send means your feed level is set at the channel level and is not affected when the house engineer adjusts the main output or the monitors. A post-fader send will change in level when the engineer adjusts their mix, which can throw off your recorded levels unpredictably.

Recording Backup Ambient Audio

Even with a perfect house feed, record backup ambient audio simultaneously on a second channel or a separate recorder. Point a microphone — a shotgun on a stand, a room mic, or the camera's built-in mic — at the stage or the room, and let it record throughout the event. This backup serves several purposes: if the house feed has a dropout, an interference issue, or an unexpected level change, the ambient recording may save the take. It also captures audience reactions, applause, laughter, and the natural feel of the event — content that the direct feed cannot provide. Editors use ambient audio to establish the event atmosphere and to smooth cuts.

Note

Always tell the house audio engineer that you are taking a feed before you plug anything into their system. Connecting to a live mixer output without notice can disrupt their setup, create unexpected feedback loops, or pull phantom power from a circuit that was not designed for it. A brief conversation and advance notice prevents most problems.

Working with the House Audio Engineer

The house audio engineer is your ally at an event. They know the system, they have the outputs you need, and they can make your audio significantly better or worse depending on how you approach the conversation. Come prepared: know what output type you need, what connectors you have, and where your recorder will be positioned. Introduce yourself early — before the event starts and before the engineer is in the middle of managing a sound check.

Keep requests simple and specific. "Can I get an XLR line out from the mixer for a video feed?" is clear. "I need audio somehow" leaves the engineer guessing. Confirm the output will be available throughout the event and ask if there is a convenient point on the signal chain — sometimes there is a video feed output already patched, which is exactly what you need.

Plug in, check your levels, and then leave the engineer alone to do their job. Do not adjust or touch the mixer without asking. If there is a problem with the feed during the event, catch the engineer's attention and ask — do not reach into their system and try to fix it yourself.

When No House Feed Is Available

At outdoor events, small conferences, informal gatherings, and venues without a PA system, there is no house feed to tap. In these situations, you are capturing audio from the room.

For a single presenter at a podium, a shotgun on a boom or stand positioned close to the front of the room can capture acceptable dialogue. The closer to the speaker, the better — a shotgun 2 meters away in a quiet, carpeted room may produce perfectly usable audio. The same shotgun 8 meters away in a hard-surface conference hall will not. Know the difference before you commit to the setup.

For panel discussions or multiple speakers without individual mics, the challenge is significantly harder. A single shotgun cannot capture several speakers simultaneously at consistent quality. In this scenario, consider whether boundary mics on the table are feasible, whether wireless lavs can be placed on all speakers, or whether the production requirements should be scaled to match what is actually achievable with the available setup.

Multi-Camera Events and Audio Routing

When shooting a multi-camera event — a conference with cameras at the back, side, and stage — a single house feed should go to a central audio recorder rather than to each camera individually. The camera operators can receive a copy of the audio (a "hop") for reference, but having a single clean recording that all cameras sync to in post is far more manageable than reconciling six different audio feeds with varying levels and quality. Designate one person responsible for the audio connection and the primary recording, and make it their job for the duration of the event.

Key Takeaways

  • A direct feed from the venue's mixer is always preferable to ambient capture — it provides clean, already-processed dialogue without room acoustics on top.
  • The best output is a balanced XLR line out; adapt to whatever the venue offers using DI boxes and appropriate adapter cables carried in your kit.
  • Request a pre-fader send so your feed level is not affected by the engineer's mix adjustments during the event.
  • Always record redundant ambient audio alongside the house feed — a camera mic, a room mic, or a second recorder provides backup if the house feed fails.
  • Introduce yourself to the house audio engineer early, keep your requests specific, and ask permission before touching the mixer.
