Common Audio Problems

~10 min read 5 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Identify and mitigate HVAC noise in a recording environment.
  • Describe what echo and reverb sound like and explain practical ways to reduce them.
  • Diagnose RF interference on a wireless system and apply field fixes.
  • Recognize clothing rustle, handling noise, and distortion by ear and trace each to its source.
  • Apply a quick diagnostic checklist to isolate audio problems during a shoot.

HVAC and Air Conditioning Noise

HVAC noise is one of the most common location audio problems, and it is easy to miss on arrival because the human brain quickly filters out steady background sounds. The microphone does not filter. Put on headphones and listen carefully before you commit to a shooting position — you may be surprised by how prominent the air conditioning roar is once you are focused on hearing it.

HVAC noise typically sounds like a broad-spectrum hiss or rumble, often with a low-frequency component (the duct vibrations) and higher-frequency turbulence (the air movement past the vent). It sits under dialogue and competes with speech intelligibility, particularly in the lower midrange frequencies where many consonants live.

Mitigation approaches, in order of effectiveness:

  • Turn off the HVAC temporarily. Ask the building manager or facilities contact if the HVAC can be shut down for 30 to 60 minutes during the interview. In a single-zone office building, this is often possible. Confirm that it will stay off long enough for your interview — some systems restart automatically on a timer.
  • Move away from the source. Vents and ducts are louder than open floor area. Moving the interview setup to the far side of the room from the nearest vent can reduce the noise by several dB.
  • Move the mic closer to the subject. Better signal-to-noise ratio means the dialogue is stronger relative to the HVAC noise. A lav 8 inches from the mouth captures far better dialogue-to-noise ratio than a camera-mount shotgun 10 feet away.
  • Accept and adapt. If none of the above are options, keep the interview tight and specific, plan for more cuts to b-roll (where the dialogue is not being heard), and note in your shoot report that audio will need noise reduction treatment in post.
Tip

Before every location shoot, arrive early and spend two minutes wearing headphones and listening to the space with your microphone running. Walk around the room and listen for HVAC vents, electrical hum, refrigerators, computer fans, and exterior traffic. Identify problems before the subject arrives, not after the camera is rolling.

Echo and Reverb in Large Rooms

Large rooms with hard parallel surfaces — conference halls, lobbies, gymnasiums, stairwells, tiled bathrooms — create echo and reverb that make dialogue sound washy and difficult to understand. What you hear on headphones is the direct sound of the voice, followed fractions of a second later by reflections off the walls, floor, and ceiling. The result sounds hollow, cave-like, or "echoey."

Unlike HVAC noise, echo cannot be removed in post without severe artifacts — noise reduction that targets reverb also targets the direct sound, producing robotic-sounding, smeared dialogue. Prevention is the only reliable approach.

The most effective mitigation is getting the microphone closer to the subject. As the direct sound level increases and the reflected sound level stays constant, the ratio improves. A lav on the chest captures primarily the direct voice with relatively little room contribution. A shotgun from across the room captures the same reverb level but less direct signal, making the ratio worse. If you must use a shotgun in a reverberant space, keep the boom as close to the subject as possible.

Acoustic treatment also helps: placing your interview subject next to a bookshelf, against a curtained wall, or in a furnished corner reduces the number of hard, reflective surfaces contributing to the reverb. Avoid placing subjects against bare concrete, glass, or wood-paneled walls in a reverberant room.

RF Interference on Wireless Systems

Wireless microphone systems transmit on radio frequencies, which means they can pick up interference from other wireless devices operating on nearby frequencies — other wireless mics, Wi-Fi access points, two-way radios, and in dense environments, even cellular signals. RF interference sounds like a buzzing, hissing, or pulsing noise underneath the dialogue, sometimes breaking up into digital dropouts (sudden silence or stuttering).

When you hear RF interference, the diagnostic steps are:

  1. Check the receiver's signal meter. If it shows a good signal, the interference is entering the system somewhere else — possibly through a cable or a bad connection. If the signal meter is low or fluctuating, the wireless link itself is the problem.
  2. Change the frequency. Most professional wireless systems allow you to scan for clear frequencies and select one with no interference. Do this before the shoot when possible, not mid-take.
  3. Move the receiver closer to the transmitter. Wireless range degrades with obstacles (walls, the human body) and competing RF energy. A receiver on the camera may be too far from a subject who moves behind a wall or around a corner.
  4. Identify and disable competing devices. Ask if nearby Wi-Fi access points can be switched off temporarily, or move the interview setup away from the wireless interference sources.
Common Mistake

Never wait to scan frequencies until you are in the middle of setup with the subject waiting. Scan frequencies when you arrive at the location, before you have committed to a shooting position. Changing frequencies after you are set up, wired, and ready to roll is stressful and wastes everyone's time.

Handling Noise and Mechanical Noise

Handling noise is mechanical sound picked up by the microphone from direct contact or vibration — your hand touching the mic body, a cable rubbing against a surface, a boom pole shifting in the operator's grip, a stand vibrating from footsteps on a hollow floor. On headphones, handling noise sounds like a low thudding, rubbing, or clicking that often appears as a sudden artifact during otherwise clean audio.

Handling noise is prevented, not fixed. Use a shock mount on any microphone on a stand or boom pole — a shock mount suspends the mic on elastic bands or springs that isolate it from vibration. Route cables carefully so they are not under tension and do not rub against hard surfaces. If a wireless transmitter bodypack is in a pocket, make sure it is not bouncing against a phone or keys.

What Distorted Audio Sounds Like

Clipped, distorted audio has a specific, unmistakable sound once you know it: a harsh, raspy crunch on loud consonants and vowels, a "buzzing" quality on sustained notes or vowels, and a squashed, aggressive character on peaks. Compared to clean audio, distorted audio sounds like it is breaking apart at the loud moments.

If you hear distortion in your headphones during a take, the source is almost always too-high input gain causing the signal to clip at the recorder's input stage. Reduce the input gain immediately — but ideally, stop, correct the level, and retake. Light distortion on a few peaks is sometimes salvageable with limiting plugins in post. Heavy distortion throughout a take is not. If the distortion is constant even at low gain settings, the problem may be in the cable or at the mic connection — check the connections.

Low Levels and Thin Audio

If dialogue sounds distant, thin, or buried in room noise, the most common causes are: too-low input gain; a mic that is placed too far from the subject; a wireless transmitter that has lost signal or has a dying battery; a cable or connection issue that is degrading the signal. Start at the headphones, trace the signal chain backward — mic position, cable connection, transmitter battery, receiver signal meter, preamp gain — and fix the first problem you find.

A Quick Diagnostic Checklist

When something sounds wrong and you need to isolate the problem quickly:

  1. Listen on headphones — confirm the problem is real and is not your headphone cable or monitor speaker.
  2. Check the input gain setting on the camera or recorder.
  3. Check the cable connection at both ends — the mic end and the camera/recorder input.
  4. Check the wireless receiver signal meter and transmitter battery level.
  5. Check phantom power is enabled if using a condenser microphone.
  6. Swap the cable with a known-good cable to isolate a bad cable.
  7. Test the mic directly (with a cable) to isolate a wireless problem from a mic problem.

Key Takeaways

  • HVAC noise is best mitigated by temporarily shutting off the system, moving away from vents, or getting the mic closer to the subject — it cannot be reliably removed in post.
  • Echo and reverb in reverberant spaces must be prevented by getting the mic closer and placing subjects near absorptive surfaces — de-reverb tools in post degrade dialogue quality.
  • RF interference on wireless systems is fixed by scanning for a clear frequency before the shoot, moving the receiver closer, or removing competing wireless devices from the environment.
  • Distorted audio sounds like a harsh crunch on loud peaks — it signals that input gain is too high, and heavy distortion cannot be repaired in post.
  • Diagnose audio problems by listening on headphones first, then tracing the signal chain backward: gain settings, cable connections, wireless signal, phantom power.
