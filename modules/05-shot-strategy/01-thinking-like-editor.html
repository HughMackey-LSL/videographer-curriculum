Thinking Like an Editor

~8 min read4 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Describe the mental shift from documentation to production thinking.
  • Identify what an editor needs from field footage to build a sequence.
  • Translate a vague shoot brief into a prioritized shot list.
  • Explain the consequences of returning with insufficient coverage.

From Recording to Gathering

Most people who pick up a camera for the first time think about what they are pointing at. A videographer who thinks like an editor thinks about what happens after they put the camera down. Those are two very different mental models, and the shift between them is the foundation of professional field production.

When you record an event, you capture what happens in front of you. When you gather material for an edit, you capture what the editor will need to construct a story. The event is temporary. The edit will be watched repeatedly. The editor cannot go back to reshoot what you missed — but they will absolutely notice the gap.

What Editors Actually Need

An editor building a sequence needs choices. That word — choices — is the key. A single long clip of a speaker at a podium gives the editor no choices. They can either use it or not. A set of clips that includes a wide shot of the room, a medium on the speaker, a close-up of their face during a key moment, a detail shot of the audience material, and a reaction shot of two attendees listening — that gives the editor the ability to construct a scene that breathes, that has rhythm, and that can be trimmed to fit any target length.

Editors need variety of shot sizes, clean heads and tails on every clip, enough cutaways to cover narration and transitions, and options to cut away from talking heads or continuous action. If you return with thirty minutes of footage but it is all the same angle and the same size, you have given the editor very little to work with regardless of how long you were rolling.

Important

The editor cannot fix missing coverage in post. If you did not shoot a cutaway, a reaction, or a detail shot on location, those shots do not exist. Plan your coverage before you arrive, not after you leave.

Documentation vs. Production

Documentation means making a record. A security camera documents. A phone propped on a shelf documents. Production means making material that will be shaped into something watchable for an audience with a purpose. The difference is intentionality.

When you arrive on location as a production-minded videographer, you are not just present — you are solving a problem. The problem is: how do I return with enough raw material that a competent editor can construct a compelling, accurate, and complete piece? Every shot you choose, every angle you set up, every moment you hold or cut should be answering that question.

Understanding the Purpose Before You Arrive

Before you touch your camera on any shoot, you should be able to answer three questions: What is the final product? Who is the audience? What story must it tell? A two-minute highlight reel for social media has completely different coverage requirements from a ten-minute documentary segment. A video aimed at prospective students needs different emotional content than an internal training archive.

When a brief is vague — "just get some footage of the event" — your job is to interrogate it before you accept it. How long will the finished piece be? Where will it be distributed? What moments are non-negotiable to capture? Is there narration, interview audio, or will the visuals stand alone? The answers to these questions determine your shot list.

Tip

Before any shoot, write down the three most important shots you must not leave without. These become your floor — everything else is a bonus. If you run out of time, the floor still gets covered.

Seeing Through the Edit

When you walk into a location, try to see it not through your viewfinder but through the timeline. Stand in the room and ask: if I were editing this scene together, what would I need? Where is the establishing shot? What is the detail that will ground the viewer in this environment? Where is the action that tells the story of this place? What is the reaction that tells me how people feel about being here?

This practice — mentally constructing the edit before you roll — changes how you move through a location. Instead of reacting to what happens, you begin to anticipate it. Instead of hoping something interesting appears, you position yourself where interesting things are likely to occur. You shift from observer to director of the documentary record.

Key Takeaways

  • The shift from documentation to production thinking is the foundation of professional field work.
  • Editors need variety — different shot sizes, clean clips, and cutaway options to build a sequence with choices.
  • Missing coverage cannot be fixed in post. Plan your shots before you arrive.
  • Always clarify the final product, audience, and non-negotiable moments before you shoot.
  • Walk every location with the edit in mind, not just the viewfinder.
