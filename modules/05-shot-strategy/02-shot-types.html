Shot Types and Sequences

~9 min read4 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Name and describe the six standard shot sizes and what each contributes to a scene.
  • Apply the five-shot rule to any observable action.
  • Explain why coverage means more than capturing the main subject.
  • Describe what a jump cut is and how shot variety prevents it.

The Shot Size Hierarchy

Shot size is how much of the frame your subject fills. Each size does a different editorial job, and understanding those jobs helps you decide which shot to reach for at any moment. You will generally move between these six sizes depending on what information the audience needs at each point in the story.

The Extreme Wide Shot (EWS) or Establishing Shot shows the environment at scale. The subject, if visible at all, is small. This shot answers the question: where are we? Use it to open a scene, reintroduce a location after a time cut, or show the relationship between a subject and their environment. A wide shot of a conference hall, a city street, or a mountainside gives viewers a spatial anchor.

The Wide Shot (WS) shows the full subject — head to toe for a person — with context around them. It orients the viewer to the action without the intimacy of a close-up. The Medium Shot (MS) frames the subject from roughly the waist up. This is the most common conversational shot — it is close enough to read facial expression but wide enough to see gesture and posture.

The Medium Close-Up (MCU) frames from chest to top of head. This is the standard interview framing — close enough for emotional connection, wide enough to feel natural in conversation. The Close-Up (CU) fills the frame with the face. Viewers feel the subject's emotion directly. Use it at moments of significance. The Extreme Close-Up (ECU) isolates a feature — an eye, a mouth, a hand on a surface — for impact or emphasis.

The Detail or Insert Shot is a close-up of an object, text, or texture that is editorially important. A document being signed, a name badge, a piece of equipment being adjusted — these give the editor cutaway options that are specific rather than generic.

Note

Shot size is relative to your subject, not to the lens you use. A close-up of a building and a close-up of a person require very different focal lengths, but both frame the subject to fill the frame with the relevant detail.

The Five-Shot Rule

The five-shot rule is a practical formula for achieving coverage of any observable action. For any action you need to cover — a craftsperson at their workbench, a chef plating a dish, a student in a seminar — capture at least these five types of shots:

  • Wide: Shows the full context of the activity and where it happens.
  • Medium: Shows the person performing the action, readable gesture and posture.
  • Close-up: Focuses on the person's face or the primary action in detail.
  • Detail/Insert: An extreme close-up of a specific object, surface, or element involved in the action.
  • Reaction: A shot of someone else watching, responding to, or affected by the action — or a cutaway that contextualizes it.

With these five shots, an editor can construct a scene of any length. They can expand it by dwelling on shots, or compress it by cutting quickly. Without all five, the editor will hit a wall — usually when they try to trim the wide and find they have nothing to cut to.

Establishing and Reaction Shots

Establishing shots are easy to forget because they feel redundant when you are on location — you already know where you are. But the viewer does not. An establishing shot at the start of a scene tells the viewer what kind of space they are in, how many people are present, and where the important action is happening within that space. Without it, every cutaway feels like it came from nowhere.

Reaction shots are equally critical and equally neglected. A reaction shot shows a person responding — listening to a speaker, reacting to an announcement, observing an action. It tells the editor and the audience what is at stake emotionally. When a speaker makes a surprising point, the viewer wants to know how the room responded. A reaction shot delivers that information. It also gives the editor a clean cut point away from a stumble, an audio problem, or a moment they want to shorten.

Tip

After finishing your primary coverage of any action, always spend five minutes shooting reaction and detail shots. These are the clips that give the editor the most flexibility — and the ones most commonly forgotten in the field.

Shooting Sequences, Not Clips

A clip is a single shot. A sequence is a set of shots that, cut together, tells a complete micro-story. Think of every action you cover as a sequence problem: you need a beginning (establishing), a middle (medium and close-up coverage of the action), and enough cutaways to move through time without a jump cut.

A jump cut happens when two clips of the same subject from the same angle are edited together after a time cut — the subject appears to "jump" position because the intervening moments are missing. You prevent jump cuts by cutting to a different shot size or angle between any two clips of the same subject. Your detail shots and reaction shots are what make those cuts possible.

Coverage means having enough of these different angles and sizes that the editor can move freely through the material without being trapped by visual continuity problems. A covered scene is one where the editor has options at every cut point.

Key Takeaways

  • Six shot sizes serve different editorial purposes: EWS, WS, MS, MCU, CU, and ECU/detail.
  • The five-shot rule — wide, medium, close-up, detail, reaction — gives editors the building blocks for any sequence.
  • Establishing shots orient viewers; reaction shots show them what to feel.
  • Jump cuts happen when an editor has no cutaway to place between two clips of the same subject.
  • Coverage means returning with enough variety that the editor has a cut option at every point in the timeline.
