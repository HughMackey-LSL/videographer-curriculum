Shooting for the Edit

~9 min read4 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Explain why handles on clips are essential and how long to hold shots.
  • Describe the hard stop problem and how to avoid it.
  • Identify what a safety shot is and when to use it.
  • Develop a field habit of mentally reviewing coverage before leaving a location.

Holding Your Shots

One of the most reliable ways to frustrate an editor is to stop recording the moment an action ends. If a subject stands up from their desk and you cut immediately as they clear frame, the editor gets a clip that ends in motion and cannot be trimmed. If a speaker finishes a sentence and you stop rolling on the last syllable, the clip has no tail — no silence, no breathing room, no handle — and any audio edit made at that point will click and pop.

The standard practice is to roll for at least five to ten seconds before the expected action and at least five to ten seconds after it ends. These extra seconds are called handles, and they are what give the editor the ability to trim and transition cleanly. A clip with good handles on both ends can be placed anywhere in the timeline without creating an audio or visual problem at the cut. A clip without handles can only be used in very specific conditions and will often be passed over in favor of something cleaner.

Apply this to everything: interviews, B-roll, cutaways, establishing shots. Before you hit stop, count silently to five. It costs you almost nothing in effort and saves significant time in the edit.

Common Mistake

Trigger-happy recording — starting and stopping constantly, cutting off actions mid-motion, reframing between every clip — produces short, unusable fragments that force the editor to work around rather than with your footage. When in doubt, keep rolling.

The Hard Stop Problem

A hard stop happens when you cut the recording in the middle of an action. A subject is walking through a doorway and you stop as they cross the threshold. A hand reaches for an object and you cut before it makes contact. A piece of equipment powers up and you stop before the light comes on. In each case, the editor has a clip that ends mid-motion and cannot be cut without leaving the sequence visually incomplete.

Hard stops can feel intentional — as though you are exercising good timing. In practice, they almost always create more problems than they solve. The solution is consistent: let actions complete before you stop. If someone is walking, let them arrive. If something is being poured, let the pour finish. If a presenter turns to face the screen, let them complete the turn and hold for a beat. You can always trim handles in post. You cannot add action that was never captured.

Maintaining Consistent Framing

Reframing mid-shot — zooming in or out, or panning to a new composition without a clear motivation — creates a clip that cannot be used in its entirety. The editor must either use the beginning of the clip (before the reframe) or the end of it (after it settles), and the reframe itself is usually unusable. This cuts your effective clip length in half and reduces the editor's options.

The discipline is to commit to a frame before you roll, hold that frame throughout the clip, and then stop rolling before you reframe for the next shot. If you want a tighter frame on the same subject, stop, reframe, then roll again as a new clip. Two clean clips from two clean frames give the editor two usable options. One clip with a reframe in the middle gives them one option and a section of footage they will never use.

Action Continuity

When you shoot coverage from multiple angles — or when you shoot a sequence of actions over several takes — action continuity matters. If your subject enters a door from camera left in the wide shot, they must enter from camera left in the medium shot and the close-up. If they pick up a cup with their right hand in one take, they must pick it up with their right hand in every take you shoot. Continuity breaks force the editor to avoid cutting between two clips that are technically good but visually incompatible.

You do not need a script supervisor for most field assignments. What you do need is to watch the action once before you roll, note which hand, which side, which direction, and then maintain those through your coverage. A quick review of your previous clip on the camera's LCD before rolling the next angle catches most continuity problems before they become editing problems.

Tip

Always have at least one locked-off safety shot running during any live or unrepeatable moment. A tripod-mounted wide shot that runs the entire duration of a speech or ceremony gives the editor an escape — they can always cut to it if a specific coverage shot fails. Think of it as insurance, not coverage.

Safety Shots

A safety shot is a locked-off, wide, stationary shot of the primary action that runs continuously. It is not your most exciting shot — it is your most reliable one. When your handheld medium shot has a shake, when your close-up has a momentary autofocus pull, when the reaction shot you planned falls through because the subject has moved — the safety shot is always there. The editor cuts to it, smooths over the problem, and cuts back out to specific coverage when it is clean again.

The safety shot discipline also prevents a common solo-shooter failure: the moment when you move to collect a different angle and the primary action happens without you covering it. A stationary camera locked off on the main action means something is always capturing what matters most, even when your attention is elsewhere in the room.

Field Notes and Mental Review

Simple field notes — written or voice-recorded — are one of the most underused tools in production. Note timecodes for key moments during an interview, flag clips with audio problems, note which takes had stumbles and which were clean. When you hand footage to an editor, even brief notes save them hours of log time and ensure that your clearest content gets used rather than the version just before it.

Before you pack up from any location, run a mental check against your original shot list and the final product you are building toward. Have you collected an establishing shot? Interview or primary audio? Close-up and detail coverage? Reaction shots? B-roll that maps to the spoken content? Room tone if there was an interview? Arrivals or transitions that the editor will need? If anything is missing, you still have time to collect it. Once you are in the car, you do not.

Key Takeaways

  • Hold every shot for at least five to ten seconds before and after the action — handles are what make clips editable.
  • Let every action complete before stopping recording; hard stops mid-motion create unusable clips.
  • Commit to a frame before rolling; reframing mid-clip splits a clip into two unusable halves.
  • A locked-off safety shot running on the main action protects against every other coverage failure.
  • Run a mental coverage checklist before leaving any location — once you leave, missing shots are gone.
