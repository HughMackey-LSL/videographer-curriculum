B-Roll Strategy

~8 min read4 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Define B-roll and explain the four editorial jobs it performs.
  • Distinguish between useful B-roll and generic "wallpaper" footage.
  • Plan B-roll by mapping it to interview content before you shoot.
  • Identify the most common B-roll mistakes and how to avoid them.

What B-Roll Is and What It Does

B-roll is any footage that is not the primary interview, presentation, or main action. It is the visual layer that plays over narration, interview audio, or music in the finished edit. While the A-roll (your primary audio track) tells the audience what to think, the B-roll shows them what to see. Without it, the editor has nothing to cut to — the talking head runs uninterrupted, the narration plays over black, and the piece loses the ability to move visually.

B-roll performs four distinct editorial functions. It illustrates — it shows the viewer the thing being discussed. If the interview subject mentions a research lab, the editor cuts to footage of the lab. It contextualizes — it establishes the environment, the people, and the scale of the story. It covers — it gives the editor a place to cut away from the talking head when they need to trim an answer or smooth an audio edit. And it paces — a well-chosen B-roll cut can shift the energy of a scene, add rhythm to a sequence, or signal a transition in the narrative.

Useful B-Roll vs. Wallpaper

Wallpaper is the term editors use for B-roll that fills time but communicates nothing. A generic exterior shot of a building, a slow pan across an empty hallway, a soft-focus bokeh shot that could belong to any piece — these are wallpaper. They occupy screen time without earning it. The editor will use them as a last resort, and they will make the finished piece feel generic regardless of how strong the A-roll is.

Useful B-roll is specific. It shows people actively doing things. It contains visual information that is directly related to the spoken content. It varies in shot size and composition. It gives the viewer something to look at that advances their understanding of the story. The test for any B-roll clip is simple: if an editor placed this clip under the relevant interview line, would a viewer who has never visited this location understand something new? If yes, it is useful. If not, it is wallpaper.

Important

B-roll of people doing nothing is almost never useful. An empty classroom, a vacant hallway, a dormant piece of equipment — these tell the editor and the viewer very little. B-roll with people actively engaged in relevant activity is almost always worth more than the same location without them.

The Four Types of B-Roll

Action B-roll shows people doing things — working, studying, building, performing, collaborating. This is the most universally useful type. It demonstrates the activity or environment being described and holds the viewer's attention because motion is inherently watchable.

Reaction B-roll shows people responding to something — listening, reacting, observing. A student listening attentively, a crowd applauding, a panel member nodding during a colleague's answer — these shots tell the audience that what is happening matters and that real people are engaged with it.

Context B-roll establishes environment and atmosphere — the exterior of a building, the layout of a space, the scale of a crowd, the geography of a location. It answers the question "where are we and what kind of place is this?" before the viewer has to wonder.

Texture and Detail B-roll consists of close-up shots of surfaces, objects, materials, and specific elements — a typed document, a pair of hands at a keyboard, an equipment dial, a wall of certificates. These shots add visual interest and specificity, and they are among the fastest to collect on location. A few minutes spent on detail shots can dramatically improve the variety of material available to the editor.

Tip

Aim for a B-roll ratio of at least 3:1 relative to your finished piece. For a two-minute video, plan to collect at least six minutes of B-roll. More is always preferable — the editor can choose what not to use, but they cannot use what does not exist.

Planning B-Roll Against Interview Content

The most effective way to plan B-roll is to map it directly against the interview or narration you expect. Before the shoot, read through the script or anticipated interview topics and identify every concrete noun — every person, place, object, or activity mentioned. Each of those nouns is a candidate for a B-roll shot.

If the interview subject is likely to say "we work with students from across the country," you need a shot of students. If they are likely to say "our equipment is state of the art," you need a shot of that equipment being actively used. Go through the anticipated content and build a B-roll list the same way you build any shot list: what will need to be illustrated, and where can you capture it? Then, once you are on location, add to that list based on what you find. Spontaneous B-roll — something you notice that perfectly captures the spirit of the story — is often the best material you will collect.

Common B-Roll Mistakes

Four mistakes account for most of the B-roll problems editors encounter. First, clips that are too short — a shot that runs for only two or three seconds cannot be used effectively in an edit. Roll every B-roll clip for at least eight to ten seconds, even if the subject completes the action in four. The editor needs handles on both ends. Second, clips that are too similar — twelve slightly different shots of the same thing from the same angle give the editor far fewer options than four shots from four different sizes or perspectives. Vary your composition deliberately. Third, no activity — empty spaces with no people or motion are rarely useful. Prioritize shots with people engaged in relevant activity. Fourth, B-roll that is disconnected from the content — beautiful footage that has no relationship to what is being said. Every clip should be traceable to a specific line in the script or interview.

Key Takeaways

  • B-roll illustrates, contextualizes, covers edits, and paces the sequence — it is not filler, it is structure.
  • Useful B-roll is specific and activity-based; wallpaper is generic and static.
  • The four B-roll types — action, reaction, context, and detail — each serve a different editorial function.
  • Plan B-roll by mapping it to every concrete noun in your expected interview or narration.
  • Aim for a 3:1 ratio, vary your shot sizes, hold every clip for at least eight to ten seconds, and prioritize people in motion.
