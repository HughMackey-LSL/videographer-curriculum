Interview Coverage

~9 min read4 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Set up an interview location for clean, editable footage.
  • Capture clean heads and tails on every clip and explain why they matter.
  • Describe what room tone is and how to record it correctly.
  • List the post-interview steps to take before packing up.

Setting Up for the Interview

An interview shoot has two phases: setup and capture. The time you invest in setup directly determines how much flexibility the editor has. A poorly set up interview — bad light, inconsistent framing, intrusive background noise — may still produce usable audio, but it will fight the editor at every cut.

Choose your location based on the subject's connection to the content, the available light, and the audio environment. A quiet room with a window providing soft directional light is a strong default. Avoid locations with loud HVAC systems, echoing walls, or uncontrolled foot traffic. Once you select the spot, position your subject with their eyeline directed slightly off-camera — looking just past the lens toward where the interviewer is sitting. Maintain that eyeline for the entire interview. If you or the interviewer move, the eyeline shifts, and the edit will reveal it.

Clean Heads and Tails

Every clip in an interview should have a clean head (a moment of silence and stillness before the subject begins speaking) and a clean tail (a moment after they finish). These handles are what give the editor the ability to trim an answer without cutting into the first or last word. When a subject starts talking the instant you roll, the editor has nothing to work with at the in-point. When you stop recording the moment they finish, the editor cannot fade or transition cleanly.

As a field practice: roll camera before you ask each question, pause for a beat after the subject finishes each answer, and let the recording continue for at least three seconds of silence. This rhythm also helps the subject settle — a subject who knows you are not rushing them will often give a more complete answer.

Tip

If a subject starts their answer before you have finished rolling, do not stop them mid-sentence. Let the answer complete, then ask them to repeat it with a clear start. Interrupting subjects mid-thought disrupts their concentration and can affect the quality of everything that follows.

False Starts and Stumbles

When a subject stumbles, misspells, or starts an answer over, keep rolling. Do not stop the camera. A false start is not a failed clip — it is a moment the editor can work around, provided you captured the clean restart that followed. If you stop and restart recording for every stumble, you create short clips with no heads or tails and disrupt the subject's rhythm. Silence after a stumble, followed by a complete clean answer, is all the editor needs.

Note the time on your camera's recording when a significant stumble or false start occurs. This is especially useful when ingesting long interview recordings — a quick note like "false start at 12:34, clean answer begins at 12:41" saves the editor significant search time.

Recording Room Tone

Room tone is the ambient sound of the space — the specific combination of HVAC hum, electrical buzz, distant traffic, and air movement that makes every room sonically unique. Every room has it, even rooms that seem completely silent. The editor uses room tone to fill gaps between interview clips, to smooth audio edits that would otherwise click or pop, and to make cuts feel seamless rather than abrupt.

Record room tone immediately after the interview concludes, while the subject is still present and the setup has not changed. Ask everyone to be completely still and silent for 30–60 seconds, and roll your primary audio recorder at the same settings used during the interview. This is one of the most commonly skipped steps in field production and one of the most useful things you can hand an editor.

Common Mistake

Packing up immediately after the interview ends is one of the most costly mistakes in field production. Before anything moves, capture room tone, get pickup lines for any key phrases that were unclear, and shoot your cutaway safety shots. Once the setup is broken, it is gone.

Pickup Lines and Cutaways

A pickup line is a clean re-recording of a phrase or sentence the subject delivered imperfectly during the interview. After the interview, review your notes and ask the subject to deliver those specific lines again cleanly. Pickups are far easier to match in the edit when the setup, framing, and audio levels have not changed — which is why you do them before you move anything.

Cutaway safety shots protect the edit from the single-camera limitation. A single camera can only be on the subject during the interview — there is no simultaneous coverage of the interviewer reacting, of the hands, of the environment. After the interview, capture the interviewer's listening face (nodding, attentive) from the subject's position. Shoot the subject's hands in their lap. Shoot any materials on the table. These cutaways give the editor visual options to cover edits in the interview track without a jump cut.

Two-Camera Setups

A two-camera interview setup — one on the subject, one on the interviewer — solves the single-camera coverage problem in real time. Camera A locks on the subject in MCU. Camera B covers the interviewer at a different size or angle. The editor can cut between them freely at any point in the conversation, use interviewer reactions exactly as they happened, and treat the conversation like a scene rather than a series of edits. If your production supports a two-camera setup, it is almost always worth the additional complexity.

Key Takeaways

  • Setup quality determines editorial flexibility — invest time in location, eyeline, and audio before rolling.
  • Every clip needs a clean head and tail: silence before the answer and after it to give the editor handles.
  • Keep rolling through stumbles; note the timecode and capture a clean pickup after the interview.
  • Record 30–60 seconds of room tone immediately after the interview while the setup is unchanged.
  • Before packing up: room tone, pickup lines, cutaway safety shots. In that order, every time.
