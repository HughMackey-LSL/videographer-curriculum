Common Editor Frustrations

~9 min read4 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Identify the most common production failures that create problems in post-production.
  • Explain the concept of "handles" and why editors require them.
  • Connect each common editor frustration to a specific preventable on-set practice.
  • Describe what an editor experiences when they receive well-organized, technically clean media.

Listening to the People Downstream

Editors rarely get to speak directly to shooters. They open a project folder, work with what is there, and deliver a finished piece — sometimes without ever meeting the person who captured the footage. Their frustrations accumulate silently, visible only in the extra hours they bill, the apologetic calls to producers about a reshoot, or the workarounds buried in the timeline that viewers will never notice but that cost real time and real money.

This lesson is their voice. Every frustration described here is real, common, and preventable. For each one, there is a specific, concrete action you can take on set that eliminates the problem entirely. The shooter who internalizes this list and acts on it builds a reputation for being easy to work with — which, in this industry, is a professional quality as valuable as any technical skill.

Mismatched Frame Rates

What the editor experiences: They build a 23.976fps timeline and begin dropping in clips. Suddenly several clips from one camera are at 29.97fps. Every one of those clips requires manual retiming — either conforming the clip to the timeline frame rate (which changes playback speed slightly) or using retiming algorithms (which can produce motion artifacts). In a long project with hundreds of clips from a mismatched camera, this remedial work can consume an entire day.

The fix: Before recording anything, confirm that every camera on the shoot is set to the same frame rate. Check after battery swaps — some cameras revert to a factory default frame rate when power-cycled. Check after switching in and out of slow-motion modes. Write the agreed frame rate on your shot sheet. Check it again at the start of each new setup.

Important

Frame rate inconsistency in a multi-camera shoot is the single easiest technical failure to prevent and one of the most time-consuming to remediate. It requires zero additional equipment and zero additional time on set to avoid. There is no acceptable excuse for delivering mismatched frame rates.

Missing Room Tone

What the editor experiences: They are cutting a dialogue sequence and need to smooth several edit points where the ambient sound changes between clips. They look through the entire project for a usable stretch of ambient sound from the location — and find none. Every clip has someone talking. They are forced to use a generic noise bed from a sound library that does not quite match, or to leave the audio discontinuities audible, or to spend an hour trying to construct a room tone substitute from the quietest moments between words in the existing clips.

The fix: At the end of every setup where dialogue or important audio was recorded, announce "room tone" (so anyone in the room stays quiet), roll for 30 seconds, and stop. That is it. This takes 45 seconds including the announcement. It is the lowest-effort, highest-value thing you can do for the audio post workflow.

Clips Without Handles

What the editor experiences: They try to trim a clip to find the best moment, and the usable content extends all the way to the first and last frames of the recording. There is no margin — no handle. Every edit requires cutting exactly at the moment the content starts, which makes clean transitions nearly impossible. Adding a dissolve or any transition effect requires frames that extend beyond the clip's content, and they are not there.

What "handles" are: Handles are the footage before and after the intended content of a clip. If an interview subject gives their answer from 0:10 to 0:55 of a recording, the handle is the footage from 0:00 to 0:10 (before the answer) and 0:55 to 1:05 (after the answer, assuming you kept rolling). Editors use handles to create clean cut points, transitions, and to find the exact frame where the action begins without being constrained to a frame-perfect start.

The fix: Roll at least five seconds before the expected start of any moment and continue rolling for at least five seconds after it ends. For interviews, keep the camera rolling throughout — there is no reason to stop between questions. For action sequences, start rolling before you call action and keep rolling until you are certain the moment is complete.

Tip

The instinct to stop recording the moment an interview answer ends is understandable — you want to save card space. In practice, modern cards hold enormous amounts of footage, and the cost of a few extra seconds of recording per clip is negligible compared to the editing value of having handles. Default to leaving the camera rolling longer than you think you need to.

No B-Roll for Critical Interview Content

What the editor experiences: A subject discusses a specific topic for two minutes. The answer is excellent and will be the centrepiece of a section. But there is no B-roll footage corresponding to that topic. The editor has no choice: they hold on the interview close-up for the full two minutes. The audience gets a talking head with no visual variety for two straight minutes, which is the default visual treatment that marks corporate video as cheap and uninspired.

The fix: Before the interview, study the outline or brief and identify every topic the subject is likely to discuss. For each topic, ask: what would illustrate this visually? Then shoot that footage. If the subject will discuss the new manufacturing floor, get footage of the manufacturing floor before or after the interview. If they will discuss the team, get footage of team members at work. The B-roll does not need to be elaborate — simple, clean coverage of the relevant subject is what the editor needs.

Audio Clipping on Critical Lines

What the editor experiences: They play through the best take of an important answer and reach a key line — exactly the line they planned to use as the soundbite — and it is clipped. The audio distorts on that specific moment. There is no other take with the same line delivered as well. The options are: use the clipped audio and hope the viewer does not notice, use a different take that is less compelling, or call a reshoot. None of these options are good.

The fix: Monitor audio levels constantly — not just at the start of the recording, but throughout. A subject who speaks at a consistent level during the pre-roll mic check may suddenly raise their voice when they reach an emotionally significant moment in their answer. If you do not have the recorder gain adjusted to handle that peak cleanly, that is the moment that clips. Set levels with headroom. Check the levels display during recording. Record a safety track at a lower gain if the recorder supports dual-channel recording.

Common Mistake

Setting gain levels based on the subject's speaking voice during setup and then not checking again during the actual recording. People speak differently when answering real questions versus when saying "testing, one, two." Their voice gets louder, faster, or more emphatic when they are engaged. Set your gain for the loudest you think they will get — and then leave a few more dB of headroom beyond that.

Inconsistent White Balance Between Cuts

What the editor experiences: They cut between two cameras covering the same interview, and the image visibly shifts color at every cut. One camera is warmer; one is cooler. The effect is distracting in the same way a brightness mismatch would be — the viewer's eye is pulled to the inconsistency rather than the content. The colorist can attempt to match them in post, but because the two cameras' color responses differ in addition to the white balance difference, a perfect match may not be achievable.

The fix: Set manual white balance on every camera in the scene. Use the same Kelvin value or perform a custom white balance from the same reference card. Never use auto white balance in a controlled interview or multi-camera setup. Lock it in and leave it alone. If the light changes significantly during a long shoot (a cloud passes over a window, the sun moves), note the timestamp and reset all cameras simultaneously, flagging the cut point for the colorist.

Unusable File Organization

What the editor experiences: They open the project folder and find hundreds of clips with camera-default names, no subfolders, no notes, and no indication of which clips are from which camera, which are primary takes, or which correspond to which parts of the brief. Every clip has to be opened and watched to understand its content. The editor's review pass — which could take 30 minutes with a well-organized handoff — takes three hours.

The fix: Follow the naming convention and folder structure covered in Lesson 4. If you do not have one established, create a simple one before the shoot and use it consistently. Include a notes file. The organization work takes 20 minutes after a shoot and saves the editor hours.

Footage Missing from the Handoff

What the editor experiences: They build their edit, then go looking for a clip they remember seeing on-set — and it is not there. After investigating, they discover the card was formatted before all footage was confirmed copied. The footage is gone permanently.

The fix: The cardinal rule of media management: never format a card until you have verified the backup. Verify by comparing file counts and total sizes between source and destination. This takes five minutes per card. The alternative is explaining to a client that their footage no longer exists.

Note

A card format is not a recoverable error. Data recovery from a formatted card is possible in some cases, but it is expensive, time-consuming, unreliable, and not guaranteed. The professional approach is to never be in the position of needing data recovery in the first place.

What Editors Say When They Are Happy

Editors do not often volunteer positive feedback — when a project goes smoothly, the shooter tends not to hear about it. But when a shooter delivers clean, organized, technically solid media, the experience is unmistakable: the edit is faster, the creative options are richer, and the finished piece is better. The editor can focus on story and pacing rather than workarounds and remediation.

The summary of what makes an editor happy: clean audio with room tone, good coverage with handles, organized files with a notes document, consistent frame rate and white balance, and no catastrophic technical failures on the critical takes. None of this requires expensive equipment. All of it requires attention, discipline, and the professional habit of thinking about what happens to the footage after you hand it off.

That mindset — the awareness that your work does not end when the shoot ends — is what separates a shooter who gets called back from one who gets a polite "we'll be in touch."

Key Takeaways

  • Mismatched frame rates between cameras require time-consuming retiming in post; verify every camera is on the same frame rate before recording anything.
  • Room tone takes 30 seconds to record and gives editors the ambient material they need to smooth audio edit transitions.
  • Handles are the extra footage before and after the intended content of a clip; editors need them to make clean cuts and transitions — roll at least five seconds before and after every moment.
  • Identify relevant B-roll topics before an interview and shoot corresponding footage; without it, editors are forced to hold on a talking head with no visual variety.
  • Clipped audio is permanent; audio gain must be monitored throughout the recording, not just at the start of a setup.
  • Never format a card before verifying that the backup copy is complete and intact.
