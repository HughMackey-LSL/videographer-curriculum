Reviewing Your Footage

~8 min read4 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Describe a structured self-review process for footage immediately after a shoot.
  • Identify what good and problem footage look like during a review pass.
  • Explain why headphone monitoring is essential during self-review.
  • Establish the habit of reviewing critical takes on set while there is still time to reshoot.

Self-Review Is a Professional Habit

The difference between a shooter who grows quickly and one who plateaus is often whether they review their own work critically and honestly. Self-review is not about second-guessing every shot you made — it is about building a clear-eyed understanding of what you captured, catching problems before they reach the editor, and developing the calibration that makes your next shoot better than your last.

Self-review happens in two distinct moments: on set, during the shoot while there is still time to fix problems, and after the shoot, before handing off media. Both are important. Missing either one means problems that could have been caught and corrected instead get handed downstream.

On-Set Review: While You Can Still Fix It

The most valuable review you do is the one that happens while you are still on location with the subject available. For critical material — interview answers, one-time moments, anything that cannot be repeated — reviewing the take immediately after recording is the only safeguard against discovering a technical problem too late.

This does not mean reviewing every shot in real time, which would slow the shoot to a crawl. It means identifying the shots where a technical failure would be catastrophic — the primary interview take, the key demonstration that cannot be staged again, the only shot of a one-time event — and making a point to quickly review those before moving on or releasing the subject.

What to check in an on-set review:

  • Focus — Scrub to a moment with the subject's face in frame. Is it sharp? Play a few seconds of motion to check for consistent focus during movement.
  • Exposure — Does the image look correctly exposed? Are highlights blown? Are shadows crushed?
  • Audio — Put on headphones. Can you hear the dialogue clearly? Is there any crackling, distortion, or interference? Is there audio on all channels?
  • Recording completion — Did the clip capture the beginning and end of the moment, or did you cut in late or stop early?
Tip

When reviewing interview takes on set, skip to a moment in the middle of the clip rather than watching from the start. Problems often appear mid-clip — a focus drift, a wireless mic dropout, an exposure shift as a cloud passed. Checking only the first few seconds of a ten-minute clip tells you very little about the clip's overall quality.

Post-Shoot Review: Before Handing Off Media

After wrapping, before the cards leave your hands, do a complete review pass. This is not the full editorial review the editor will do — it is a quick verification that nothing catastrophically wrong happened that you did not notice during the shoot.

A structured post-shoot review pass:

Step 1: Verify the Shot List

Go through the shot list item by item. For each shot, identify the clip or clips that cover it. If any shot list item has no corresponding clip, note it. Some gaps are acceptable — a shot that was replaced by something better, for example. Unplanned gaps are coverage problems the editor will encounter.

Step 2: Check All Clips Have Audio

Scroll through the clip list and confirm that audio waveforms are visible on each clip. A clip with a flat audio track — no waveform at all — has no recorded audio. This can happen when a microphone is unplugged, a channel is muted, or the wrong input is selected. Flat audio on a critical clip is a serious problem; it is better to discover it before the editor does.

Step 3: Monitor Audio Through Headphones

Speaker playback on a camera or laptop is insufficient for audio review. Built-in speakers compress and smooth over problems that headphones reveal clearly. For each significant clip, put on headphones and listen to a representative sample. Listen for: background noise levels, wireless interference artifacts, distortion on loud passages, any unexplained sounds or interruptions.

Step 4: Check Focus and Exposure on Key Clips

For the most critical clips — interview primary takes, hero shots, unrepeatable moments — scrub through and check image quality. A slightly soft clip that is the only available take is something the editor needs to know about. Note the clip number and the nature of the problem.

Common Mistake

Reviewing footage through the camera's built-in speaker and at less than full volume. Camera speakers are tiny and unrepresentative. Audio problems that are obvious through headphones — mild distortion, a faint wireless crackle, a persistent buzz — are inaudible or unidentifiable through a small built-in speaker at low volume. Always use headphones for audio review.

What Good Footage Looks Like

During self-review, you need a reference point for "acceptable" versus "problematic." Good footage has these characteristics:

  • Clean starts and stops — The camera was rolling before the action began and continued rolling for a few seconds after. The editor has handles on both ends.
  • Sharp focus on the subject — The primary subject is consistently in focus throughout the clip, or any focus changes are deliberate and smooth.
  • Well-exposed image — Highlights are not blown, shadows retain detail, and the subject's face is properly exposed.
  • Clear, intelligible audio — Dialogue is understandable through headphones at a reasonable listening level. Background noise does not compete with the primary audio.
  • No technical artifacts — No visible rolling shutter tearing, no dropped frames, no wireless interference crackling, no wind noise overwhelming the mic.
  • Adequate length — The clip is long enough that the editor can find the usable portion without the clip ending prematurely.

What Problem Footage Looks Like

Identifying problem footage quickly is a skill. Common problems and how they appear during review:

  • Soft focus — The subject's features look slightly smeared or mushy, without crisp edge definition. Text in frame, if any, appears blurry.
  • Clipping audio — The audio waveform is flat-topped rather than rounded at the peaks. On playback through headphones, loud passages sound harsh, distorted, or crackling.
  • Overexposure — Bright areas of the image are pure white with no detail. The highlight zebra warning (if used during recording) would have been active.
  • Underexposure — The image is murky and dark; shadow areas are crushed to black. Noise may be visible in dark regions.
  • Clips too short to cut with — The recording starts on the moment itself with no leader before it, or stops immediately as the action ends with no tail. The editor needs handles to make a clean cut.
  • Wireless interference — A periodic crackling or buzz in the audio that increases and decreases in intensity, often correlating with nearby mobile phone activity.
Note

Flagging a problem clip does not mean it is unusable — it means the editor should know about it before they build their edit around it. A slightly soft clip may still be the best available take and may cut together fine. The editor can make that judgment; what they cannot do is judge blind if you did not flag it.

Post-Shoot Notes as a Review Output

The self-review process should produce a document: brief notes about what was shot, any problems discovered, preferred takes, and anything the editor should know. This does not need to be elaborate — even a few lines per section of the shoot is valuable. The notes file (covered in Lesson 4) is the output of this review process.

Writing notes immediately after review, while the shoot is fresh, produces far more useful documentation than attempting to reconstruct from memory later. Note the clip numbers of preferred takes. Flag the clips with known problems and describe the problem briefly. Note any pickups that were not completed and may be needed. This documentation accompanies the media to the editor and makes their review pass faster and more informed.

Self-Review as a Learning Tool

Beyond serving the editor, self-review is the primary mechanism by which shooters improve. Every problem you catch in review is a lesson: what caused this, and how do I prevent it on the next shoot? Soft focus may reveal that you need to use manual focus more consistently. Clipping audio may reveal that your gain structure is set too high for the environments you work in. Clips that are too short reveal that you need to establish the habit of rolling earlier and stopping later.

Shooters who review their work honestly and draw operational lessons from it improve steadily. Shooters who hand off media without reviewing, or who review but do not reflect, repeat the same problems indefinitely.

Key Takeaways

  • Self-review happens in two stages: on set immediately after critical takes (while reshoot is still possible), and post-shoot before handing off media.
  • Always use headphones for audio review — built-in camera or laptop speakers cannot reliably reveal wireless interference, mild distortion, or other audio problems.
  • A flat audio waveform means no audio was recorded; a flat-topped waveform means audio was clipped. Both are serious problems the editor needs to know about.
  • Good footage has clean starts and stops, sharp focus, proper exposure, clear audio, and adequate length for the editor to cut with.
  • Post-shoot notes are the output of the review process: preferred takes, flagged problems, and pickup needs, written immediately while details are fresh.
