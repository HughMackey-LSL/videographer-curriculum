Video Releases: What They Are and Why They Matter

A video release — also called a talent release, consent form, or media release — is a legal document in which a subject grants the organization permission to use their likeness, voice, and image in specified ways. Releases are not bureaucratic formalities. They are the mechanism by which the organization establishes the legal right to publish footage of a real person, and the mechanism by which that person understands what they are agreeing to before they go on camera.

A standard release covers three things: what is being captured (likeness, voice, name, image), the context and purpose of the use (promotional video, training material, website, social media), and the duration and geographic scope of the permission (one-time use, ongoing, worldwide). Releases may also specify what the subject is and is not agreeing to — for example, agreeing to appear in an internal training video but not in external advertising materials.

When releases are required depends on how the footage will be used. For internal-only content viewed by a defined audience of staff or students who are part of the organization, releases may not be legally required — but checking with your communications or legal team before assuming this is always the right approach. For any content published externally — on a website, social media, broadcast, or external communications — releases are almost always required for identifiable individuals.

Important

A release obtained after filming is worth less than one obtained before. Subjects who have already been filmed may feel pressured to sign or may simply refuse. Coordinate releases in advance whenever possible, so there is no ambiguity about whether a subject has consented before the camera rolls.

FERPA and Student Privacy in Educational Settings

The Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA) is a US federal law that protects the privacy of student education records. In practical terms for video production in a school or university, FERPA creates specific obligations around filming students: their image, in certain contexts, may be considered part of their educational record and thus protected.

FERPA does not prohibit all filming of students. It applies primarily to situations where the footage identifies a student in connection with educational records — for example, filming a student receiving academic accommodations, participating in a disciplinary hearing, or being discussed in relation to their academic performance. General footage of students on campus, at public events, or engaged in activities that are not tied to their individual educational records is typically not covered by FERPA.

However, institutional policy — which may be stricter than FERPA itself — often governs what can be filmed and published. Many schools require parental consent for any published footage of students under 18, regardless of context. Some institutions restrict filming in classroom settings without explicit instructor and student consent. Know your institution's specific policies and follow them exactly. When in doubt, ask the communications or legal office before filming — not after.

Note

FERPA rights belong to parents until a student turns 18, at which point they transfer to the student. This matters for parental consent requirements: a parent may consent on behalf of a 16-year-old student, but an 18-year-old student must consent for themselves.

Filming Minors

Filming individuals under 18 requires additional care and, in most institutional contexts, additional consent. Parental or guardian consent is required before a minor's image can be recorded and published. Many institutions use a blanket media release form collected at enrollment or registration that covers general promotional use — but these blanket forms may not cover all types of footage or all uses. For any project involving close-up or featured footage of a minor, obtain explicit project-specific consent.

Even when consent has been obtained, filming minors requires attentiveness to context and setting. A consent form allows the organization to use the footage as described — it does not eliminate the organization's responsibility to ensure that the filming environment is appropriate and that the minor is comfortable throughout.

Public vs. Restricted Spaces

On institutional property, the filming rules are generally set by the institution rather than by general public law. Common areas — lobbies, outdoor plazas, hallways — are typically permissible to film in without individual consent, provided the footage does not identify individuals in connection with protected categories of information. Restricted spaces — classrooms during instruction, healthcare facilities, research labs, private offices — typically require explicit permission from the administrator or authority responsible for that space.

When filming in a space where there is any uncertainty about what is permitted, ask the facility manager or the event coordinator before setting up equipment. It is far easier to get permission in advance than to explain why you filmed in a restricted area after the fact.

When a Subject Does Not Want to Be Filmed

Any person who clearly indicates they do not want to be filmed must be respected, immediately and without argument. This applies regardless of whether the filming was technically permissible in that space. The organization's relationship with the person — and its reputation for respectful conduct — is more important than any individual shot.

If someone objects to being filmed, lower the camera and acknowledge them: "Of course — I'll make sure you're not in the frame." Do not negotiate. Do not explain why you were filming or why it should be acceptable. Simply comply and redirect. If the objection affects your coverage significantly, notify the producer so they can decide how to address it.

Incidental Footage: Crowds and Hallways

When filming in public or semi-public institutional spaces, people will appear in the background who did not explicitly consent. This is generally acceptable for wide shots where individuals are not identifiable or are clearly incidental to the main subject of the footage. It becomes more complicated when a specific person is inadvertently captured in a close-up or is identifiable in footage that will be widely distributed.

As a practical rule: review footage that will be published for any clearly identifiable individuals who appear unintentionally and are not covered by a release. Flag these clips to the editor and producer so they can decide whether to use the footage, obscure the individual, or obtain a retroactive release. Do not make the editorial decision yourself — flag it and let the appropriate person decide.

Tip

When shooting at events where you know the footage will be published, position yourself and frame shots so that background individuals are less clearly identifiable — using depth of field, wider shots, or avoiding tight coverage of incidental bystanders. Prevention is simpler than post-production remediation.

Coordinating Releases: In Advance vs. On the Day

Releases coordinated in advance — collected as part of the event registration process, through the communications team, or by the producer before the shoot — are significantly easier to manage than releases collected on the day. When releases are a day-of task, they frequently get skipped in the rush of setup, subjects may feel caught off guard, and there is no time to follow up with individuals who do not sign.

For any project with named interview subjects, releases should be part of the pre-production checklist: confirm that a release is on file or that the subject will sign one before the session begins. For events, work with the event coordinator to include a media release in the registration or check-in process. When this is not possible, designate a specific team member to manage release collection on the day so it does not fall through the cracks.

What Happens If Footage Is Published Without Releases

Publishing footage of identifiable individuals without appropriate consent exposes the organization to legal liability, damages relationships with subjects, and may violate platform terms of service or institutional policy. In educational contexts, violations of FERPA carry regulatory consequences. In healthcare contexts, similar privacy laws impose significant penalties. Even in lower-stakes situations, publishing footage without consent damages trust in a way that is difficult to repair.

The operator's role is not to be a legal expert. It is to know enough about releases and policy to recognize when a situation may be a problem, and to escalate it to the person who can make the appropriate decision — the producer, the communications director, or the legal office. "I don't know if we have releases for these people" is the right thing to say. Publishing footage and hoping for the best is not.

Common Mistake

Assuming that filming in a public or semi-public institutional space means releases are not needed. Permissibility to film is a separate question from permissibility to publish. You may be legally able to capture footage in a space but still need consent before using that footage in external communications. Always consider the end use, not just the capture context.

Key Takeaways

  • A video release establishes legal consent to use a person's likeness, voice, and image for specified purposes — obtain them before filming whenever possible.
  • FERPA protects student educational records; know your institution's specific policy, which may be stricter than federal law.
  • Filming minors requires parental or guardian consent; blanket enrollment forms may not cover all uses.
  • Any person who does not want to be filmed must be respected immediately — compliance is non-negotiable.
  • Permissibility to film and permissibility to publish are separate questions — always consider the end use.
  • The operator's role is to flag policy uncertainty and escalate to the appropriate person — not to make legal decisions independently.
