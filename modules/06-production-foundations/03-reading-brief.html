Reading a Brief and Planning Shots

~10 min read4 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Identify the standard elements of a creative brief or shoot request.
  • Translate a brief into a prioritized shot list.
  • Ask the right pre-shoot questions when information is missing or vague.
  • Describe the minimum intake process when no formal brief exists.

What a Creative Brief Contains

A creative brief — sometimes called a shoot request, production brief, or job order — is the primary document that communicates what a video project needs to accomplish. In institutional settings, briefs range from detailed, well-structured documents to a single-paragraph email. Regardless of format, a useful brief contains the same core information: purpose, audience, deliverables, deadline, location, subject, and tone.

Purpose describes what the video is meant to achieve — recruit students, train new staff, document a program milestone, promote a department. Purpose is the most important element in the brief because it determines every other creative and logistical decision. If the purpose is unclear, ask before doing anything else.

Audience describes who will watch the finished video. A recruitment video for prospective students requires different energy, imagery, and pacing than an internal training video for existing staff. Knowing the audience helps you make moment-to-moment decisions on set about what to prioritize.

Deliverables specify what the finished product should be — a three-minute highlight reel, a 90-second social clip, a raw archival recording, a series of interview segments. Deliverables define the scope of coverage required. If the deliverable is a series, you need to capture enough distinct material for multiple edits. If it is a single short clip, you can focus your coverage accordingly.

Deadline affects how quickly media needs to be ingested, organized, and handed off. A video needed for a board meeting tomorrow requires a same-day handoff. A promotional video planned for a campaign three months out has more flexibility — but that flexibility should not be used as an excuse to skip media organization.

Location tells you where the shoot will happen and what pre-scouting may be needed. Even a familiar location changes: furniture moves, light shifts seasonally, construction adds ambient noise. Never assume a location is exactly as you last left it.

Subject identifies who will be on camera — a faculty member, a group of students, an event speaker. Knowing your subjects in advance allows you to research them, understand their context, and anticipate how they may behave on camera.

Tone describes the emotional register of the piece — formal and authoritative, warm and accessible, energetic and dynamic. Tone guides your framing choices, your lighting approach, and how you interact with subjects to help them perform authentically.

Reading a Brief Actively

Reading a brief passively — skimming it for the date and location — leaves most of its value on the table. Active reading means interrogating the document: What is the finished product, exactly? What moments are must-haves, and what is bonus coverage? What flexibility exists in the schedule, the location, or the subject list? Where are the gaps that need clarification?

A useful technique is to read the brief once for orientation, then read it again asking "What could go wrong?" A brief that specifies an outdoor location in spring raises a weather contingency question. A brief that lists five interview subjects for a half-day shoot raises a scheduling feasibility question. A brief that asks for "authentic student reactions" in a staged environment raises a question about how those moments will be created. These questions are not obstacles — they are opportunities to make the shoot more resilient before it happens.

Tip

Highlight or note the three most critical must-have shots from any brief. If the day goes sideways and you only get three things, what absolutely must be in the can? This exercise forces clarity and helps you triage under pressure on location.

Questions to Ask Before Arriving

Even a well-written brief leaves gaps. The following questions should be standard pre-shoot intake for any project:

  • What is the event agenda or schedule? Knowing the sequence of events lets you plan positions and anticipate transitions. A ceremony with a processional, remarks, and a reception requires three entirely different coverage strategies.
  • Who specifically are the subjects? Names, titles, and roles. For events, who are the featured speakers or participants? For interviews, who is the interviewee and what is their relationship to the topic?
  • Are there photography or filming restrictions? Some spaces, events, or individuals have policies that restrict filming. Knowing this before arriving prevents an awkward and time-consuming confrontation on location.
  • What are the quiet areas or off-limits zones? Patient areas in a healthcare facility, restricted research spaces, or confidential meetings require clear boundaries.
  • What is the final delivery format? Resolution, aspect ratio, file format, and delivery destination. This affects your camera settings, your codec choice, and your post-production workflow.
  • Who is the on-site contact? The person you call when you cannot find the room, when the subject is running late, or when something changes on the day.
Common Mistake

Arriving without an on-site contact name and number. When something changes — and something always changes — you need a person who can make decisions or direct you to the right resource. Never go on location without this information.

Translating a Vague Request into a Shot List

"Just get some footage" is not a brief. But it is a request you will receive frequently, and it is your job to translate it into something actionable. When a request is vague, use the brief elements as a framework: ask about purpose, audience, deliverable, deadline, location, and subject. Even a five-minute conversation guided by these questions typically produces enough information to build a functional shot list.

A shot list is not a rigid script. It is a prioritized list of coverage goals — the specific images, moments, or sequences that the edit will need to work. A shot list for a simple event recap might look like this:

  • Wide establishing shot of the venue exterior (priority: high)
  • Wide shot of full audience before the program begins (priority: high)
  • Medium shot of each speaker at the podium (priority: high)
  • Tight reaction shots of audience members during remarks (priority: medium)
  • Detail shots: name tags, signage, printed programs (priority: medium)
  • Candid shots of attendees networking during break (priority: low)
  • Interviews with two or three attendees after the event (priority: low)

The priority designations tell you what to sacrifice if time runs short. They also tell the producer what was captured if questions arise later about missing coverage.

When You Cannot Get a Brief

Sometimes a brief simply does not exist or cannot be produced in time. In these situations, establish your minimum intake by answering five questions before arriving: What is this video for? Who will watch it? What is the key moment or subject? Where is it happening and how long will it last? When does the finished video need to be delivered? These five questions will not produce a detailed shot list, but they will orient your coverage and prevent you from arriving completely blind.

Important

Do a brief review the night before every shoot. Spend ten minutes confirming your call time, contact information, equipment checklist, location logistics, and your three must-have shots. This single habit prevents the majority of day-of scrambles and ensures you arrive prepared rather than reactive.

The Shot List in Practice

A shot list should be a living document, not a static one. On location, cross off shots as you capture them, add new shots you identify while on site, and reprioritize based on what is actually happening. A shot list is a plan, not a contract. The goal is complete, usable coverage — not perfect adherence to a pre-written list that was created before you saw the location in person.

After the shoot, keep the shot list. It becomes documentation of what was captured, which is useful when questions arise in post about missing coverage or when the project is revisited months later. A shot list attached to the media handoff is one of the most helpful things you can provide to an editor.

Key Takeaways

  • A complete brief includes purpose, audience, deliverables, deadline, location, subject, and tone — if any are missing, ask before arriving.
  • Read briefs actively: identify must-have shots, potential problems, and gaps that need clarification.
  • A shot list is a prioritized coverage plan, not a rigid script — update it on location as conditions change.
  • When no formal brief exists, answer five minimum intake questions before arriving.
  • Do a ten-minute brief review the night before every shoot.
  • After the shoot, keep the shot list and include it in the media handoff to the editor.
