The Three Phases of Production

Every video project — whether it runs for two hours or two weeks — passes through the same three phases: pre-production, production, and post-production. Understanding all three phases, even when your primary responsibility is only one of them, makes you a dramatically more effective camera operator. Problems that appear during the shoot almost always originate in pre-production. Problems that slow down editing almost always originate in production. The phases are interdependent, and the people who understand this produce work that is faster to finish and easier to use.

Pre-Production

Pre-production is everything that happens before the camera rolls. This phase includes reviewing the creative brief, identifying the purpose and audience for the finished video, scouting the location, securing permissions, drafting a shot list, arranging logistics (parking, access, equipment check-out), and confirming schedules with subjects and stakeholders. It is also when you identify unknowns: Is there ambient noise at the location? Will the event have a fixed program or will things change on the fly? Are there any institutional policies that restrict filming in certain areas?

Pre-production is the cheapest phase to fix problems. A scouting visit that takes 30 minutes can prevent two hours of unusable footage. A phone call to confirm a subject's availability prevents a wasted trip. Operators who treat pre-production as optional frequently arrive to find locked doors, competing events, or a subject who thought the shoot was next week.

Tip

Do a brief review the night before every shoot. Confirm the call time, location, equipment list, and at least three must-have shots. This ten-minute habit prevents the majority of on-site scrambles.

Production

Production is the shoot itself — the period when the camera is rolling and media is being captured. This is where your technical skills, situational awareness, and interpersonal instincts all operate simultaneously. Good production practice means arriving early, setting up before action begins, anticipating what will happen next, and capturing complete coverage rather than just the highlights. It also means remaining organized: labeling cards, logging what was shot if time permits, and monitoring your storage capacity so you never run out mid-event.

Production is also where improvisation happens. A subject cancels, the light shifts unexpectedly, the event runs 40 minutes over schedule. Operators who enter production with a clear plan — built in pre-production — handle these changes without panic because they know which shots are essential and which are bonus coverage.

Post-Production

Post-production covers ingesting and organizing footage, editing, adding graphics and music, color correction, audio mixing, review and revisions, and final delivery. For most institutional operators, post-production is handled by a separate editor. This makes the handoff from production to post a critical moment: the editor can only work with what you deliver. Disorganized files, unlabeled cards, and missing coverage all cost hours of editor time and may result in a weaker finished product.

Post-production also includes archiving — storing the project files and original media so the organization can return to the footage in the future. Archiving is frequently skipped or done poorly. Treating it as part of the production workflow ensures the organization's media assets remain accessible and useful.

Common Mistake

Handing off footage without any documentation. Even a brief text message or sticky note telling the editor "Card A is the interview, Card B is B-roll, the wide shot at the start of Card A had a noise issue" saves significant time and prevents confusion.

Core Roles in a Small Production Team

Institutional video teams are typically small — often two or three people, sometimes just one. Understanding the standard roles helps you communicate clearly regardless of team size and ensures responsibilities are not accidentally duplicated or dropped.

Producer / Project Lead

The producer owns the brief and the timeline. They communicate with the client or internal stakeholder, manage expectations, make decisions when priorities conflict, and ensure the finished product meets the original goals. On a shoot day, the producer may also direct — giving guidance to subjects, managing the schedule, and deciding when coverage is complete. In institutional settings, this role is sometimes filled by a communications manager or department coordinator who may not have production training. In that case, the camera operator needs to provide informed input about what is or is not feasible.

Camera Operator

The camera operator is responsible for the image and the audio. This means making exposure, framing, and focus decisions, monitoring levels on both the camera and any external recorder, and maintaining consistent quality throughout the shoot. The camera operator is also responsible for media management in the field: keeping cards organized, monitoring storage, and protecting the footage until it reaches the edit drive. In many institutional shoots, the camera operator is also the de facto director, producer, and audio engineer all at once.

Editor

The editor receives organized media and turns it into the finished piece. Great editors can rescue mediocre footage — but only up to a point. Their work is made dramatically easier by complete coverage, clean audio, and clear organization. When operators understand what editors need, they capture footage with the edit in mind: covering transitions, getting establishing shots, leaving handles on clips, and capturing natural sound.

Single-Operator Shoots

In many institutional environments, one person handles all roles: they review the brief, scout the location, operate the camera, monitor audio, manage media, and sometimes deliver the final edit. Single-operator work is demanding precisely because the mental overhead of each role does not disappear — it all falls to one person. The discipline of separating pre-production, production, and post-production thinking — even when one person does all three — keeps the work organized and prevents costly oversights.

Single operators must be especially systematic about pre-production because there is no one else to catch missed details. A checklist is not optional — it is a cognitive tool that compensates for the attention demands of simultaneous roles.

The Two Critical Handoff Moments

Every production has two moments where problems from one phase transfer to the next. The first handoff is from pre-production to production: Are you prepared? Do you have a shot list, confirmed access, charged batteries, and formatted cards? If pre-production was skipped or rushed, you will discover the gaps on location — where fixing them is expensive and sometimes impossible.

The second handoff is from production to post: Is the media organized? Are the cards labeled, the files named correctly, and the footage complete? If this handoff is messy, the editor inherits the disorder. Every hour of disorganized media can cost two hours of editor time to sort out.

Treating these handoffs as formal checkpoints — even a brief mental review before moving to the next phase — builds the discipline that separates reliable operators from inconsistent ones.

Why Phase Awareness Makes You a Better Shooter

Camera operators who think only about the shoot itself — the moment the camera is rolling — frequently create work that is technically competent but difficult to finish. They capture beautiful images that do not cut together. They miss establishing shots because they were not thinking about the edit. They return with footage that the editor cannot identify because naming was skipped. Phase awareness shifts your thinking from "am I getting good shots right now?" to "will this project be complete and usable when I hand it off?" That shift in perspective is one of the most important professional upgrades a developing operator can make.

Key Takeaways

  • Every project passes through pre-production, production, and post-production — understanding all three makes you a better operator even when you only cover one.
  • Problems that appear during the shoot almost always originate in pre-production.
  • The camera operator owns the image, the audio, and media management in the field.
  • Single-operator shoots demand systematic pre-production discipline because there is no one else to catch missed details.
  • The two handoff moments — pre to production, and production to post — are where organizational discipline pays off most clearly.
