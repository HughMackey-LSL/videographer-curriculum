Types of Video Projects

~10 min read4 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Identify seven common types of video projects and their defining characteristics.
  • Explain how project type affects pacing, coverage requirements, and shot priorities.
  • Describe what distinguishes a documentary approach from an archival one.
  • Use project type as a planning tool before arriving on location.

Why Project Type Matters Before You Arrive

Not all video projects are the same, and treating them as if they were leads to coverage that does not serve the edit. An operator who shoots an instructional video like an event recap will miss the tight B-roll that illustrates each step. An operator who shoots a documentary like a promotional video will produce polished but artificial moments that undermine the film's credibility. Knowing the type of project you are shooting before you arrive shapes your shot list, your pace, your relationship with subjects, and even your equipment choices. It is one of the most important pieces of information you can extract from the brief.

Instructional Video

Instructional videos teach a process or skill. They are typically scripted, shot in a controlled environment, and edited to guide a viewer through sequential steps. Coverage requirements include a clean wide shot of the presenter or process, tight B-roll that illustrates each individual step — close-ups of hands, materials, tools, or screens — and cutaway shots that allow the editor to trim narration without jump cuts. Multiple takes are standard and expected. The pace is methodical. Because viewers may pause and rewind, clarity and precision matter more than dynamism.

For the camera operator, instructional shoots require patience and careful attention to continuity. If a presenter demonstrates a step from a certain angle, you need enough coverage from complementary angles to give the editor options. Inconsistent framing or lighting between takes creates visual discontinuity that distracts from the instruction.

Tip

On instructional shoots, photograph or sketch the initial setup before rolling so you can rebuild the exact frame if you need to stop and restart. Continuity errors are much harder to fix in the edit than they are to prevent on set.

Promotional Video

Promotional videos represent an organization, product, or initiative in its best light. They tend to be brand-focused, visually polished, and emotionally engaging. Creative direction from a communications team is common — expect specific requests about framing, color palette, or the feeling a shot should convey. Voice-over narration is frequently added in post, which means the editor needs strong visual variety to cut against the audio. Beautiful, well-composed imagery matters enormously here. Generic or flat footage that could belong to any organization undermines the promotional intent.

Operators shooting promotional content should think like visual storytellers: What image communicates energy, excellence, or warmth? What moment, if frozen in time, would make someone want to learn more? Planning and scouting pay significant dividends on promotional shoots because the best images rarely happen by accident.

Documentary

Documentary video is observational. It captures authentic moments, real conversations, and genuine reactions rather than staged or scripted events. The camera follows events as they unfold rather than directing them. Heavy use of reaction shots, environmental details, and contextual footage — the world around the subject — is what gives documentary footage its texture and credibility.

Documentary work demands anticipation. Because you cannot call for a second take of a spontaneous moment, you need to be positioned, focused, and rolling before things happen. This requires reading people and spaces carefully. What is about to happen? Who is about to speak? Where will the energy be in the next thirty seconds? Documentary operators are always half a step ahead of the scene.

Note

Documentary and promotional approaches can exist in the same project. A promotional video might use documentary-style footage of real people to build authenticity. When this happens, clarify with the producer which sections are scripted and which are observational — your approach in each section should differ.

Archival Video

Archival video is primarily for record-keeping. It documents an event, speaker, or process so the organization has a permanent reference. The audience is often small — staff, researchers, or future producers revisiting the material — and polish matters less than completeness. Missing the first five minutes of a lecture or cutting away at a critical moment is a far more serious failure on an archival shoot than imperfect exposure.

Archival work often involves long, static shots. A locked-off wide shot of a lecture or ceremony may run for 45 minutes. This demands careful equipment setup at the start, a plan for monitoring battery and storage levels over the duration, and the discipline to avoid unnecessary adjustments that would introduce camera movement into footage meant to be neutral.

Event Recap

Event recaps distill a longer event into a short highlight reel — typically 60 to 180 seconds. They move quickly, use a variety of shot sizes, and emphasize energy, emotion, and moments over comprehensive documentation. Coverage requirements are broad: wide establishing shots, medium shots of speakers or participants, tight reaction shots, environmental details, and transitions between spaces. The pace on set is fast because you often have only one opportunity to capture each moment.

Event recap operators need to move efficiently without being disruptive. You cannot afford to miss a moment because you were repositioning, but you also cannot afford to block sightlines or create distractions. Prioritization is essential: know which moments are must-haves (the keynote speaker's opening, the award presentation, the group photo) and which are bonus coverage, and allocate your position and attention accordingly.

Interview

Interview video is controlled and audio-centered. A subject speaks directly or semi-directly to camera or an interviewer, and audio quality is paramount. Poor audio in an interview is almost always unfixable in post; even expensive noise reduction tools leave artifacts that distract viewers. Interview setups require careful microphone placement, a clean and appropriate background, controlled lighting to separate the subject from the environment, and enough time to build rapport with the subject before recording begins.

B-roll coverage is critical to interview editing. The editor needs visual material to cut to when trimming answers, covering edits, or illustrating points the subject makes. Without adequate B-roll, the edit becomes a talking-head video with no visual variation. Operators shooting interviews should always allocate time after the main interview to gather relevant B-roll before striking the set.

Social Clip

Social clips are short, punchy videos optimized for distribution on social media platforms. They may be vertical (9:16 aspect ratio for Instagram Reels, TikTok, or YouTube Shorts), horizontal, or square depending on the platform. They are designed to be watched without audio — so visual communication must be strong enough to stand alone — but should also be engaging with audio on. Text overlays, captions, and graphics are often added in post.

For the camera operator, social clip production often means planning for vertical framing from the start, keeping action in a tighter portion of the frame, and shooting shorter, punchier moments rather than extended sequences. If a project will be distributed as both a long-form and a social clip, consider capturing some shots specifically for vertical use in addition to your standard horizontal coverage.

Common Mistake

Shooting horizontal-only footage for a project that will ultimately be delivered vertically for social media. Cropping horizontal footage to vertical typically cuts off important parts of the frame. When a social format is planned, confirm this during pre-production and adjust your framing strategy.

Identifying Your Project Type Before Arriving

The brief or shoot request should tell you the project type. If it does not, ask directly: "What will the final video be used for?" and "Where will it be published?" The answers tell you the pacing, coverage priorities, and technical requirements that should drive your planning. A shoot request for "a video of the faculty workshop" could mean an archival recording, a documentary clip, a promotional highlight reel, or social content — each requiring a different approach. Clarifying this before you arrive is one of the most valuable things you can do.

Key Takeaways

  • Project type determines pacing, coverage priorities, and your relationship with subjects — identify it before arriving.
  • Instructional videos need tight B-roll of each step and multiple take coverage; continuity matters.
  • Documentary work demands anticipation; you cannot call a second take on an authentic moment.
  • Archival video prioritizes completeness over polish — never miss the beginning or a critical moment.
  • Interview shoots are audio-first; plan for B-roll immediately after the main interview before striking the set.
  • Social clips may require vertical framing — confirm delivery format during pre-production, not after.
