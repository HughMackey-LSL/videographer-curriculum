Camera and Lens

~8 min read5 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Locate and operate the essential controls on a professional video camera body.
  • Understand the key operational differences between common institutional cameras.
  • Change a lens safely without introducing dust to the sensor.
  • Match lens choice to assignment requirements.
  • Perform a complete pre-shoot camera check.

Knowing Your Camera Body

Every professional assignment starts with one non-negotiable requirement: you must be able to operate your camera body without looking up instructions. That means knowing the location of every control before you arrive on set. The record button, the mode dial, the media slot door, the battery door, the input ports — all of these need to be second nature.

On most institutional video cameras you will encounter the record button on the top right of the body (Sony FX series) or near the grip (Canon C series). The mode dial or dedicated video/photo switch is typically on the top plate. Media slots — often two — are usually accessed through a door on the left or right side of the body. The battery door is at the rear or base. Input ports (HDMI out, XLR audio in, USB-C for firmware and tethering) are typically protected by rubber covers on the body's sides.

Tip

Before your first shoot with any new camera body, spend 30 minutes in a quiet room navigating every menu page. You do not need to memorize every option — you need to know where everything lives so you can find it under pressure.

Changing Lenses Safely

Sensor contamination from dust is one of the most avoidable and costly problems in field production. It shows up as dark spots in the frame — often invisible until you review footage in post. Follow this protocol every time you change a lens:

  1. Power off the camera. A live sensor can attract dust electrostatically.
  2. Find a sheltered position — turn your back to any wind or air conditioning vent.
  3. Have the replacement lens ready with its rear cap removed and body cap ready to place.
  4. Point the camera body downward as you remove the lens, so gravity pulls dust away from the sensor.
  5. Attach the new lens immediately. Do not leave the body mount exposed for more than a few seconds.
  6. Replace all caps as soon as the lens is seated and locked.
Common Mistake

Never change lenses with the camera pointed upward or in a dusty environment without shielding the mount. Even a single particle on the sensor can ruin a day's worth of footage.

Lens Care and Storage

Front and rear elements are the most vulnerable parts of a lens. The front element faces the scene and is exposed to fingerprints, moisture, and impact. The rear element sits millimetres from the shutter or sensor and must be kept spotless. Always replace both caps immediately after removing a lens. Store lenses vertically or in dedicated foam-padded compartments — never loose in a bag where they can roll and strike each other.

Clean the front element only with a proper lens cloth or air blower. Never use compressed air from a can at close range; the propellant can leave residue. If the element is smudged, breathe gently on it and wipe in a single circular motion from centre outward with a microfibre cloth.

Matching Lens to Assignment

The choice between a zoom and a prime lens is mostly a practical one. A versatile zoom (24–70mm or 28–135mm equivalent) lets you reframe without moving the camera — essential when shooting alone or in tight quarters. A fast prime (35mm or 50mm at f/1.4 or f/1.8) delivers significantly more light to the sensor, which matters in dimly lit venues or when you need background separation. Know which situation you are walking into before you pack.

After mounting any lens, confirm it is fully seated by pressing lightly and rotating until you hear or feel the locking click. A half-mounted lens will produce a blurry image with unpredictable autofocus behaviour — and it can fall off the camera entirely if the body is moved.

Pre-Shoot Camera Checks

Run this checklist every single time you pick up the camera for a shoot — not just when you are new to the equipment:

  • Battery level: Is a full battery installed? Is a spare charged and in the bag?
  • Card space: Is a formatted card with sufficient space in the correct slot?
  • Recording slot: Is the camera set to record to the slot where the card is installed?
  • Codec and resolution: Are they correct for today's deliverable?
  • Frame rate: Is it set for the project (24p, 25p, 30p)?
  • Lens cap: Is it off?
  • ND filter: Is it cleared (off or at minimum ND)?
Important

The single most common on-set mistake is rolling to the wrong card slot — either an empty slot or a slot containing no card. Always verify the recording destination in the menu before calling "rolling."

Key Takeaways

  • Know every control location on your camera body before arriving on set.
  • Change lenses with the camera powered off, pointed downward, in a sheltered position.
  • Always confirm lens seating with a locking click before recording.
  • Choose zooms for flexibility, primes for low-light performance.
  • Run a full pre-shoot checklist — battery, card, recording slot, codec, frame rate — every time.
