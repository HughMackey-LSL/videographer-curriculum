Power, Media, and Consumables

~8 min read5 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Calculate battery requirements for a shoot of a given duration.
  • Identify the correct media card type and speed rating for your camera.
  • Format cards correctly and manage them safely in the field.
  • Build a complete cables inventory for a standard shoot.
  • Understand the role of gaffer tape and a small toolkit in field production.

Camera Batteries

Running out of battery mid-shoot is an entirely preventable failure. The rule is simple: bring more than you think you need, and check them the night before — not the morning of.

Most professional camera batteries (Sony NP-FZ100, Canon LP-E6NH, Blackmagic pocket batteries) provide approximately 60–90 minutes of continuous recording under moderate use — active autofocus, EVF on, recording to card rather than external media. Higher-capacity variants of the same battery type (often marked "plus" or with a higher mAh rating) extend this by 30–50%. A half-day shoot with transitions, setup time, and retakes typically consumes 2–3 batteries. A full-day shoot should have 4–6 charged batteries available.

Charger TypeNotes
Single-slot chargerCharges one battery at a time; standard included with most cameras. Slow but sufficient if you charge the night before.
Dual-slot chargerCharges two batteries simultaneously; halves overnight charging time for a 4-battery kit.
In-car USB chargingUseful for topping up batteries during transit between locations; not a replacement for overnight mains charging.
Important

Check charge level on every battery before packing the kit. A battery showing full charge on the indicator may have dropped significantly since it was last checked. Verify with the camera body, which will show remaining percentage or bar indicators more accurately than the battery's own LED indicator.

Media Cards

The wrong card type — or a card that is too slow — will cause your camera to stop recording mid-shot and display an error. There is no recovering footage that was not written to card. Know your camera's exact card requirement before you buy cards or pack the kit.

The three main card formats in current use are:

  • SD / SDHC / SDXC: The most common consumer and prosumer format. Found as a secondary slot in many professional cameras. Maximum speed limited by the UHS-I or UHS-II bus.
  • CFexpress Type A: Used in Sony FX3, FX6, and A7/A1 series as the primary slot. Much faster than SD; required for high-bitrate 4K and above.
  • CFexpress Type B / CFast 2.0: Used in Canon C300 Mk III, URSA Mini Pro, and similar cinema cameras. Physically larger than Type A; not interchangeable.

Card Speed Ratings

Card speed is rated by the Video Speed Class system: V30, V60, and V90 — these numbers represent the minimum sustained write speed in MB/s. Most 4K video codecs at moderate bitrates require V60 as a minimum. High-bitrate 4K RAW and Cinema RAW typically require V90 or CFexpress.

The consequence of using an undersized card is not gradual degradation — the camera will record normally, then abruptly stop and display a "card write speed" error when the buffer fills. You will lose the take that was in progress. This is non-negotiable: use the correct speed class for your resolution and codec.

Tip

Check your camera's manual for the specific minimum write speed required for each recording mode. Manufacturers list approved cards on their websites — if a card is not on the approved list, treat it as suspect regardless of what the packaging says.

Card Management in the Field

For a half-day shoot (3–4 hours of active recording) with a 4K codec at moderate bitrate, plan for 256GB of card capacity as a comfortable minimum, allowing for retakes and coverage. Full-day shoots should carry 512GB or more.

Format cards in-camera before every shoot — not on a computer. In-camera formatting creates the correct file structure for that specific camera body and clears any hidden files from previous sessions that could cause conflicts. Never format a card that has not been fully backed up.

Label every card. A label maker or strip of white gaffer tape on the card body with a number or date prevents confusion. Critically, use a physical separation system for used versus unused cards: place fresh cards label-up in the case, used cards label-down (or in a separate compartment). Never return a card with footage to the same slot as empty cards — the risk of reformatting a full card is real.

Cables Inventory

A missing cable on location is an emergency. Build and verify this inventory for every shoot:

  • HDMI cable (A to A or A to Micro): For outputting to an external monitor or client monitor.
  • XLR cables (minimum 2): Microphone to recorder, recorder to camera. Label male and female ends.
  • USB-C cable: For firmware updates, tethering, and charging accessories.
  • Power cables for lights and chargers: Verify length against the location's power outlet positions.

Gaffer Tape and the Small Toolkit

Gaffer tape is the single most versatile consumable in field production. Unlike standard duct tape, it tears cleanly by hand, leaves minimal residue on most surfaces, and holds reliably on fabric, metal, and painted walls. Uses include: taping extension cords to floors, securing lav cables to clothing, labeling cards and batteries, temporary fixes to broken mounts, and marking positions on the floor for the subject.

Carry at least one full roll per shoot. It is not a backup item — it is a primary tool. A small toolkit should accompany every kit:

  • Multi-tool or Leatherman (screwdrivers, pliers, blade)
  • Adjustable spanner for light stand bolts and thread adapters
  • AA and AAA batteries (wireless systems, remote controls, follow focus units)
  • 9V batteries (some older wireless systems and meters)
  • Permanent marker and white label tape
Note

Gaffer tape should be replaced when the roll starts to unravel from the inside or the adhesive becomes inconsistent. A roll that fails on location is worse than no roll at all — it wastes time and leaves sticky residue everywhere.

Key Takeaways

  • Plan for 2–3 batteries per half-day shoot; verify charge level on every battery the night before.
  • Match card type and speed class to your camera body's specific requirements — using an undersized card causes mid-take recording errors.
  • Format all cards in-camera before the shoot; never format a card that has not been backed up.
  • Use a physical system to separate used cards from fresh cards — never rely on memory alone.
  • Gaffer tape is a primary field tool, not a backup; carry at least one full roll per shoot.
