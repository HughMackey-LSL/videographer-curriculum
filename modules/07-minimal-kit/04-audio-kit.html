Lavalier Microphone Components

A lavalier (lav) microphone is a small-capsule mic designed to be clipped to a subject's clothing, close to the mouth, for consistent, isolated dialogue capture. Understanding its parts prevents most field failures:

ComponentPurpose
CapsuleThe tiny microphone element, usually at the tip of the cable. This is what captures sound — handle it with care, never touch it directly.
ClipAttaches the capsule to clothing. Position it 15–20 cm below the chin, on a flat surface of fabric that will not rustle when the subject moves.
Cable (TRS or XLR)TRS (3.5mm) connects to cameras and wireless transmitters. XLR connects to professional recorders and mixers. Know which your system uses.

Attaching a Wired Lav

The placement of the lav clip determines the sound quality for the entire shoot. Position the capsule on the sternum area — far enough from the chin to avoid plosives and breath noise, close enough to the mouth that the voice is clearly captured. Avoid placing it on a lapel edge that moves when the subject turns their head, or under a layer of fabric that produces clothing noise.

Route the cable down inside the subject's clothing if possible, or along the seam of a jacket. Secure any loose cable with small tape loops to prevent it from swinging and creating noise. The cable should have enough slack that normal movement — leaning forward, turning — does not pull the clip out of position.

Tip

Ask the subject to take a deep breath and speak a few sentences before you finalise clip placement. Listen through headphones for clothing rustle, breath noise, and level. It is far easier to reposition now than to discover a problem mid-interview.

Wireless Lavalier Systems

A wireless lav system adds a transmitter (worn by the subject) and a receiver (connected to the camera or recorder) to the standard lav capsule. The transmitter receives audio from the capsule via TRS and converts it to a radio signal; the receiver decodes that signal back to audio.

Pairing procedure for most systems (Rode Wireless GO, DJI Mic, Sennheiser EW series):

  1. Power on the receiver first and wait for it to enter pairing mode (usually indicated by a flashing LED).
  2. Power on the transmitter and hold the pairing button until the units link (LEDs on both should become solid).
  3. Confirm the link by speaking into the transmitter capsule and watching the receiver's level meter respond.
  4. Set the gain on the transmitter to a level that peaks around –12 dBFS on the receiver's meter during loud speech.
  5. Check the frequency or channel — in environments with many wireless devices (conference centers, event venues), you may need to scan for a clear frequency.
Common Mistake

Always check transmitter battery level before fitting the mic to the subject. A transmitter that dies mid-interview leaves you with no audio. Replace or charge batteries the day before every shoot, not on the day.

Shotgun Microphone

A shotgun microphone is a long, directional condenser mic designed to capture sound from a distance — typically used on a boom pole above the subject or mounted to the camera's hot shoe for run-and-gun work. Its directional pickup pattern (supercardioid or lobar) rejects sound from the sides and rear, isolating the target subject.

The shock mount is a cradle of elastic bands or internal isolation material that suspends the microphone body, decoupling it from the pole or camera. Without a shock mount, every touch of the pole, footstep, or camera adjustment travels directly through the body of the mic and appears as low-frequency rumble in the audio. Always use a shock mount — it is not optional.

To attach a shotgun to a boom pole: thread the pole into the shock mount's mounting thread (standard 3/8" on most poles), seat the mic in the shock mount, and route the XLR cable down the inside of the pole to exit at the bottom, connecting to the recorder or camera. Secure the cable at the pole butt with a wrap of gaffer tape to keep it from swinging.

Audio Recorder Operation

A field audio recorder (Zoom F series, Sound Devices MixPre series) provides higher-quality preamps, more inputs, and more precise metering than most camera bodies. For any shoot where audio quality is critical — interviews, corporate presentations, narrative work — use a separate recorder and sync in post.

ControlFunction
Input gain knobsSet the level for each XLR input independently. Start low and bring up until peaks hit around –12 to –6 dBFS during loud speech.
Level metersVU or PPM meters showing real-time signal. Watch for consistent average levels; peaks should not regularly exceed 0 dBFS.
Headphone output and volumeMonitor the recorded signal, not just the input. Turn headphone volume high enough to hear subtle noise and distortion.
SD card slotThe recording media. Verify a card is inserted and has sufficient space before recording.

XLR Cables and Headphones

XLR connectors are the professional standard for balanced audio. The male end (3 pins, no socket) connects to the output side — a microphone, the output of a transmitter receiver, or a mixer output. The female end (3 pins in a socket) connects to the input side — a recorder, camera XLR input, or mixer input. Balanced XLR cables reject electromagnetic interference over long runs, which is why they are used in production rather than unbalanced TRS cables.

Use closed-back headphones for field monitoring. Closed-back designs isolate your ears from ambient noise, allowing you to hear exactly what the microphone is capturing — not what the room sounds like to you. Manage the headphone cable by looping it through your shirt collar or clip it to your clothing to prevent it from swinging in front of the camera during a take.

Note

Set your headphone monitoring level high enough to hear subtle problems — a distant air conditioning hum, intermittent wireless interference, or clothing rustle. If you monitor too quietly, you will miss issues that will be immediately obvious to an editor playing back at normal levels.

Key Takeaways

  • Position the lav capsule 15–20 cm below the chin on a flat, non-rustling fabric surface.
  • Power on the wireless receiver first, then pair the transmitter to it.
  • Always check transmitter battery level before fitting the mic — never rely on yesterday's batteries.
  • A shock mount is mandatory for boom-pole shotgun use; it prevents handling noise from reaching the recording.
  • Monitor audio through closed-back headphones at a high enough volume to catch subtle issues before they become post-production problems.
