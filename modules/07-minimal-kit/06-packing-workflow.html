Packing and Case Workflow

~9 min read5 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Explain why consistent packing is a professional discipline, not a preference.
  • Organise a kit across multiple cases by category.
  • Execute a complete day-before preparation sequence.
  • Follow a safe and efficient tear-down order on location.
  • Handle media cards correctly from end of shoot through to handoff.

The Principle of Consistent Packing

Professional kit management is built on a single discipline: every item has a place, and it is always returned to that place. This is not about neatness for its own sake — it is a system that eliminates the most common and damaging on-set problem: discovering that something is missing or broken after you have driven to location.

When every item lives in the same position in the same bag, you can check the kit in seconds by looking at each position rather than trying to remember whether you packed something. Missing items are immediately visible. Damaged items are discovered during pack-out, not during the shoot. The crew who opens your bag after you should find the same organised layout every time.

Tip

Photograph the inside of each packed case once you have settled on a layout that works. Print or store the image inside the lid. New crew members can repack correctly without being trained on every item's location, and you can verify your own pack against the photo as a final check.

Case Types and Their Trade-offs

The choice of case determines how well your equipment survives transport and how quickly you can access it on location:

Case TypeStrengthsWeaknesses
Pelican / hard caseMaximum impact and waterproof protection; stackable; pressure-equalization valve for air travelHeavy; bulky in small vehicles; may trigger airline carry-on restrictions
Padded camera bagLightweight; ergonomic carry; fast access; less conspicuousLess impact protection; not waterproof; cannot be stacked under heavy items
Roller bagWheels reduce fatigue over long distances; large capacityStairs and uneven terrain are difficult; does not fit in tight car boots

For institutional video, the most practical approach is one padded camera bag for the camera and lenses (with you at all times), one medium hard case for audio equipment, and one large hard case or roller for lighting gear and stands. Keep the camera bag as carry-on luggage during air travel; check the hard cases.

Organising by Category

Group items by the order in which they are used on set, not by size or convenience of packing:

  • Camera and lens bag: Body, lenses (caps on), batteries (charged), cards (formatted and labelled), monitoring cable, lens cloth, blower.
  • Audio bag: Recorder, lavs and clips, wireless transmitters and receivers (batteries checked), XLR cables, headphones, boom pole, shock mount, shotgun mic.
  • Lighting bag / case: LED panels (handles protected), softboxes (folded), power supplies, extension cords (coiled and secured), barndoors, spare bulbs if applicable.
  • Accessories bag: Tripod, gaffer tape, toolkit, sandbags (in a separate heavy bag), labels, pens, cable ties.

The Day-Before Sequence

The most effective shoots are prepared the day before. The morning-of scramble is a symptom of poor workflow — not a normal condition. Complete this sequence the evening before any shoot:

  1. Charge all batteries — camera batteries, wireless transmitters, recorder, gimbal. Confirm they are charging; do not assume.
  2. Format all cards in-camera. Confirm all previous footage has been backed up before formatting.
  3. Review the brief. Confirm location, call time, subject names, deliverable specifications, and any special requirements (e.g. B-roll list, room layout).
  4. Run the pre-pack checklist. Physically check each item as it goes into the bag. If you cannot touch it, it is not checked.
  5. Load the car. Place the heaviest cases first (lighting, sandbags). Place fragile cases (camera bag) on top, never under anything heavy. Leave padding between cases if the boot is shared.
Common Mistake

Packing in a rush on the morning of a shoot guarantees that something is left behind. The day-before sequence exists specifically to prevent this. If circumstances force morning packing, slow down and use the physical checklist — do not try to remember items mentally under time pressure.

On Location: Cases as a Workstation

Once you arrive on location, open your cases in order of use. Camera bag first — get the camera out, confirm it is functioning, and decide on lens. Audio bag second — lay out the wireless system, test pairing, check levels. Lighting last — this takes the most space to set up and is often the last component needed before rolling.

Position open cases away from walkways but within arm's reach of your workstation. A closed, latched case that gets kicked is safe. An open case in a walkway that gets stepped on is a liability. Get into the habit of closing and latching cases that are not actively being accessed.

Tear-Down Order

Tear-down order is the reverse of setup priority and reflects safety and media integrity:

  1. Audio first. Remove the lav from the subject (with their consent and in private if it was routed through clothing). Power off transmitters. Coil XLR cables. Pack the recorder last so you can confirm the final level meters one more time.
  2. Lights second. Lower each light before removing it from the stand. Allow panels to cool. Collapse softboxes. Coil power cables. Collapse stands. Sandbags last.
  3. Camera third. Stop recording. Remove the card from the camera and immediately place it in a dedicated "used" compartment or a card wallet labelled with the date and shoot name. Do not return it to the same slot as blank cards. Remove the battery. Cap the lens. Cap the body. Pack the camera.
  4. Accessories last. Tripod, gaffer tape, toolkit, cables.
Important

The card comes out of the camera before anything else is moved at wrap. A card still in the camera while the bag is being packed is a card that can be accidentally reformatted or lost. Remove it, label it, and put it in a secure, dedicated location the moment the shoot ends.

Post-Shoot Card Handling

The footage on the card is the product. Until it is backed up in at least two locations, it is irreplaceable and must be treated accordingly. After tear-down:

  • Keep used cards physically separate from empty cards at all times.
  • Do not reformat a used card until both the primary backup and the secondary backup have been verified as complete and playable.
  • Return gear in the same condition in which you received it. Clean lens elements, coil all cables, return each item to its designated position in the case, and note any damage or missing items before returning the kit to storage.

Key Takeaways

  • Consistent packing — every item in the same place, every time — is the foundation of reliable field production.
  • Complete the day-before sequence: charge batteries, format cards, review the brief, run the physical checklist, load the car.
  • Tear down in order: audio first, lights second, camera third, accessories last.
  • Remove the camera card immediately at wrap and isolate it from blank cards — the footage is the product.
  • Return gear in the same condition you received it, with all damage and missing items noted before handoff.
