Lighting Kit

~9 min read5 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Identify LED panel types and read a basic spec sheet.
  • Attach and position a softbox correctly.
  • Assemble a light stand safely, including sandbag placement.
  • Handle extension cords and power safely on location.
  • Follow a safe tear-down sequence for all lighting equipment.

LED Panel Types

Modern field lighting is almost entirely LED-based. The three types you will encounter most frequently are:

TypeColor RangeBest Use
BicolorAdjustable between ~2700K (tungsten) and ~6500K (daylight)Mixed environments, interviews where ambient light color varies
Daylight-onlyFixed at ~5600KOutdoor work, studios with controlled daylight-balanced ambient
RGBWWFull color spectrum plus white and warm whiteCreative color work, events, situations requiring unusual hues

For most institutional video — interviews, corporate shoots, documentary coverage — a bicolor LED panel is the most practical choice. It lets you match whatever ambient light exists in the room without gels.

Reading the Spec Sheet

Before you use a light you have not used before, spend two minutes with its spec sheet. The numbers that matter most are wattage (how much power it draws and, roughly, how bright it is), CRI (Color Rendering Index — how accurately the light renders colors compared to natural sunlight), and color temperature range (the minimum and maximum Kelvin values for a bicolor unit).

For professional work, target a CRI of 90 or higher. A light with a CRI below 85 will render skin tones with a muddy, greenish, or reddish cast that is difficult to correct in post without shifting the entire image. Cheaper panels often claim high wattage but ship with low CRI — always check both figures.

Tip

When evaluating a new panel in a pinch, hold your hand in front of it and look at your skin tone. Good CRI should produce natural-looking skin. Greenish or magenta shifts are a warning sign — adjust the color temperature dial to compensate or flag the issue before the shoot.

Softbox Attachment and Use

A softbox wraps the LED panel in a larger diffusion surface, turning a small, harsh point source into a large, soft, wrapping light. For interviews and talking-head shoots, a softbox-equipped panel is the standard key light choice.

To attach a softbox: first unfold the speed ring (the circular frame that connects the softbox to the light's mounting point) and push the rods into their locking slots. Attach the ring to the front of the LED panel using the panel's Bowens mount or proprietary adapter. Pull the fabric body over the rods and fasten it securely. Finally, attach the front diffusion panel — the white inner baffle — and zip or velcro it closed. The diffusion panel should be flat and taut, not sagging.

Position the softbox as close to the subject as practical — the closer a large light source is to the subject, the softer the light quality. Back it off and the light hardens, shadows become more defined, and the overall character changes.

Light Stand Assembly and Safety

A light stand that falls over is a danger to crew, subjects, and equipment. Build the habit of setting stands up correctly every single time:

  1. Spread the legs fully. All three legs should be extended to their maximum spread before you raise the stand. A partially spread stand is unstable.
  2. Extend sections in order from widest to narrowest. The widest (lowest) section goes up first, locked before the next section is extended. This keeps the heaviest sections low and the center of gravity stable.
  3. Do not over-extend. Every stand has a maximum height — usually marked or limited by the locking mechanism. Extending beyond the design limit risks tipping or collapse.
  4. Place a sandbag on the lowest rung of the leg nearest to you. The sandbag goes on the lowest part of the stand, on the leg closest to where you or the crew will be standing — not thrown over the top bar, which raises the center of gravity and makes the problem worse.
Important

A sandbag is not optional when a light stand is carrying a panel and softbox. Any unexpected bump, cable pull, or air conditioning draft can tip an unsandbaged stand. A falling light stand can injure a subject or crew member and destroy the light in a single incident.

Extension Cord Safety

Lighting often requires power from a wall outlet that is not conveniently located. Extension cords are the solution — but they carry their own risks. Use cords that are rated for the current you will draw. A 14-gauge cord handles most LED panels; avoid thin lamp-cord-gauge extensions for anything above a few hundred watts. Keep cord runs as short as practical — longer cords have more resistance and can cause voltage drop that affects the light output and color temperature of sensitive LEDs.

Never run extension cords under rugs or carpeting — they can overheat without being visible. Tape all cord runs across walkways with gaffer tape to prevent trips. Do not overload a single circuit; a standard office circuit is rated for 15–20 amps (roughly 1800–2400 watts). Check how many other devices share the circuit before plugging in.

Common Mistake

Coiling an extension cord while it is carrying current can create a choke coil that generates heat. If a cord feels warm during use, uncoil it fully — a coiled cord under load is a fire risk.

Controls and Modifiers

Most LED panels have a brightness knob or touch-screen dimmer and a color temperature dial or slider. Start with brightness at 100% and use distance to control intensity — moving the light back reduces output following the inverse square law. Use the dimmer only when distance is not an option. The color temperature dial should be set to match your camera's white balance setting — if you are shooting at 5600K, set the light to 5600K.

Barndoors, when included, are hinged metal flaps that attach to the front of the panel and allow you to block spill light from hitting walls, ceilings, or the camera lens. Adjust them to control where the light falls without affecting the overall brightness or quality.

Tear-Down Order

Tear down lighting in this sequence: lower the light to its lowest position on the stand before attempting to remove it — a light at full height is heavy and awkward to manage safely. Remove the light from the stand. Allow the panel to cool if it has been running at high power (LEDs run warm, not hot, but softbox fabric can retain heat). Collapse the softbox and detach it. Coil all power cables neatly — use an over-under coiling technique to prevent tangles. Collapse the stand, and remove the sandbag last. Sandbags are the last thing off and the first thing on.

Key Takeaways

  • Bicolor LED panels are the most versatile choice for mixed-environment institutional video.
  • Target CRI 90+ to ensure accurate, broadcast-quality color rendering.
  • Sandbag the lowest rung of the nearest leg — every time, no exceptions.
  • Run extension cords at full uncoil length; never under rugs; tape across walkways.
  • Tear down by lowering the light first, then collapsing the stand, and removing the sandbag last.
