Windows and Color Temperature

~10 min read5 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Set up a window as a key light source correctly.
  • Describe the mixed color temperature problem and why auto white balance cannot fully solve it.
  • List at least three practical solutions for mixed color temperature on location.
  • Explain how to modify and control window light using diffusion and blocking materials.
  • Identify the specific color temperature challenge posed by institutional fluorescent lighting.

Using a Window as a Key Light

A large window on an overcast day is one of the best available light sources for an interview — large, soft, and free. Using it well requires deliberate positioning. The goal is to turn the window into a controlled key light rather than an uncontrolled ambient source that spills everywhere.

Position the subject so the window is to their left or right, not in front of them or behind them. The camera is placed roughly perpendicular to the window, so the window is to one side of frame. This creates classic side lighting: the window side of the face is lit, the far side falls into shadow. Place a reflector or white bounce card on the shadow side to fill the contrast to an acceptable ratio. This is a complete one-light interview setup using only the window and a reflector.

The camera angle matters. If the camera is placed between the subject and the window (with the window behind the camera), the light becomes frontal and flat. If the camera is placed on the opposite side of the subject from the window (window behind the subject), you get a silhouette unless you expose for the subject and blow out the background. Perpendicular camera placement gives you the soft side lighting that window setups are prized for.

Common Mistake

Shooting directly into a window — even a moderately bright one — almost always results in a silhouette or severely underexposed subject. If you see a window in your frame and the subject is in front of it, your camera will almost certainly expose for the window and render the subject dark. Reposition or block the window before rolling.

Controlling Window Light

Window light can be modified, controlled, and even eliminated with straightforward materials. Diffusion applied to the window glass softens the quality of the light, especially useful when direct sun is hitting the window and creating harsh, hard light. White ripstop nylon, diffusion silk, and commercially available window diffusion film all work. The material only needs to span the area of window that would strike your subject.

Curtains and sheers can provide partial diffusion. Blackout blinds or black wrap taped to the frame can block a window entirely, useful when the window is on the wrong side of the room or is providing an unwanted competing light source. Blocking all the windows in a room and building the light from scratch is a completely valid approach when you need full control and have the time.

Time of day is the hardest variable to control. Direct sun moves. A setup that looks perfect with beautiful indirect morning light may be wrecked by direct sun shafts at midday. When you scout a location, note which way the windows face, what time direct sun will enter, and plan your shooting schedule around it. If that is not possible, budget time to modify windows as the light changes.

The Mixed Color Temperature Problem

Color temperature becomes a critical field problem when two or more light sources of different Kelvin values illuminate the same subject simultaneously. The most common scenario: daylight from a window (approximately 5600K, blue-white) and tungsten or warm LED ceiling fixtures (approximately 3200K, orange) both light the subject. Set the camera white balance to daylight and the window side looks correct, but the ceiling light side looks orange. Set white balance to tungsten and the ceiling side looks correct, but the window light side looks blue.

Auto white balance attempts to find a compromise and will often shift back and forth if the auto system is responsive, creating inconsistent color throughout the clip. This is one of the most visible and distracting technical errors in amateur video — the skin tones shift orange to blue to back repeatedly as the auto white balance oscillates. Always set white balance manually on location.

Important

Auto white balance cannot simultaneously correct for two light sources of different color temperatures in the same frame. It can only choose one balance point. Set white balance manually to match your dominant source, then solve the mixed temperature problem by adjusting your sources to match each other — not by adjusting the camera setting.

Solutions for Mixed Color Temperature

There are four practical approaches when you encounter mixed color temperature on location, and the right choice depends on your time, equipment, and the specific situation.

The first and simplest solution: turn off all artificial lights and expose solely for window light. If the window provides sufficient intensity and acceptable direction, this eliminates the problem entirely. Set white balance to daylight (5600K) and shoot. The room may look dim away from the window, but if your frame is composed tightly on the subject, the background darkness may actually look better than a bright room with mixed colors.

The second solution: gel the windows with CTO (Color Temperature Orange). CTO gel shifts daylight from 5600K down toward tungsten at 3200K. Applied to the window, it makes daylight appear warm like the room's tungsten fixtures. Set white balance to 3200K and all sources now match. This is a common studio solution but requires gel material, time to apply it, and enough window area that the application is practical.

The third solution: use a bicolor LED set to 5600K as your key light, turn off or reduce warm-toned ceiling fixtures, and set white balance to 5600K. You are committing to the daylight color temperature and eliminating sources that do not match it. This is often the fastest approach when you carry a bicolor LED in your kit.

The fourth solution: set a compromise white balance (around 4000–4500K) and plan to correct the remaining color cast in post-production. This is the last resort — it trades the clean, definitive fix for speed at the cost of post-production work and potentially softer results.

The Fluorescent Office Problem

Institutional fluorescent lighting — particularly older T8 and T12 fluorescent tubes — presents a specific challenge beyond just color temperature. In addition to being at a different Kelvin value than daylight, older fluorescent tubes emit light with a spiked spectral output that includes a significant green component not captured by the Kelvin scale. Even when white-balanced correctly for the daylight equivalent of the fluorescent color temperature, skin tones under fluorescent light can look greenish and unappealing.

The correction is a plus-green camera setting combined with a plus-magenta gel on your lights, or a minus-green camera setting. Many cameras have a green/magenta adjustment in their white balance settings. Set it to reduce the green if you are forced to shoot under fluorescent light without supplementing. If you are bringing in an LED key, set the LED to match the fluorescent color temperature as closely as possible, check the result on your waveform and vectorscope if available, and adjust. Modern high-CRI LED tubes and panels have much better spectral output than older fluorescents and typically do not have the green spike problem.

Key Takeaways

  • Position the subject perpendicular to the window, with the camera also perpendicular — this turns the window into a side key light.
  • Never shoot with a bright window in the background unless deliberately creating a silhouette.
  • Mixed color temperature cannot be corrected by white balance alone — the light sources themselves must be matched.
  • The four solutions for mixed color temperature are: turn off artificial lights, gel windows with CTO, match your LED to daylight and eliminate warm sources, or compromise and correct in post.
  • Always set white balance manually on location — auto white balance will oscillate and create inconsistent footage.
  • Older fluorescent tubes may have a green spectral spike requiring magenta correction beyond simple Kelvin adjustment.
