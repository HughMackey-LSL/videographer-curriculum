Natural Versus Artificial Light

~9 min read4 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Describe the strengths and limitations of natural light for field production.
  • Explain how to position a subject relative to a window for soft, flattering light.
  • Identify the types of artificial light commonly found in institutional settings.
  • Decide when to supplement, replace, or work entirely with available light.

Natural Light: Free but Not Free of Problems

Natural light costs nothing and, at its best, is extraordinarily beautiful. On a uniformly overcast day, the entire sky becomes a giant softbox — massive, soft, and wrapping. The light is diffuse, shadow edges are gradual, and skin tones look clean and even. This is the condition many photographers and cinematographers prize above all others, and it requires zero equipment to exploit.

Direct sunlight is a different matter. The sun is technically a very large object, but its distance from Earth makes it appear very small — roughly the size of a fingernail held at arm's length. That means direct sun behaves like a hard, small source: sharp-edged shadows, high contrast, and unflattering rendering of facial features. Shooting an interview in direct sunlight also forces your subject to squint. The workaround is to move the subject into open shade — the shadow cast by a building, tree, or overhead structure — where they are lit by the sky rather than the sun directly.

Common Mistake

Shooting into a bright window or outdoor background is one of the most frequent errors in field production. The camera exposes for the bright background, turning your subject into a silhouette. Always position the window or bright exterior behind the camera, not behind the subject, unless you are deliberately using a silhouette effect.

Working with Window Light

Windows are the most useful source of natural light for interior interviews. A large window on an overcast day is essentially a very large softbox mounted in the wall — and the quality of light it produces is excellent. The key to using it well is positioning.

Position the subject perpendicular to the window — that is, they face neither toward nor away from it, but rather have the window to their left or right. The camera is placed at roughly 90 degrees to the window, level with the subject. This arrangement turns the window into a side key light. The shadow side of the subject's face can be controlled with a reflector placed on the opposite side, bouncing some window light back to reduce the contrast ratio.

The biggest variable with window light is time of day. In the morning, a window facing east receives direct sun; in the afternoon, the same window is in shade. A north-facing window in the northern hemisphere receives consistent indirect daylight throughout the day — this is why studios and photographers prize north-facing light. Before a shoot, check when direct sun hits each window in your location and plan accordingly. A window that gives beautiful soft light at 10 a.m. may produce harsh, moving shafts of direct sun by noon.

You can modify window light with diffusion material — white ripstop nylon, diffusion silk, or even a thin white sheet — taped or clamped over the pane. This softens and slightly dims the light, which is useful when direct sun is unavoidable. Heavy drapes, blackout curtains, or black wrap can block a window entirely, useful when you want to exclude an undesired source and replace it with a controlled artificial light.

The Consistency Problem

The fundamental limitation of natural light is that it changes. Clouds move, the sun angles shift, and what looked perfect when you arrived may shift substantially by the time you roll camera. For short interviews of five to ten minutes this is manageable. For longer recordings, natural light drift can produce visible inconsistency between the beginning and end of a shoot, which is problematic if footage needs to cut together.

If shooting over a long period, monitor the light on a scoped waveform or at minimum check the frame on your monitor regularly. When you see the overall exposure level shifting, you may need to adjust camera exposure or reposition the subject relative to the window. Setting up a dedicated artificial light and using the window only as fill or background can stabilize the image considerably.

Tip

Overcast days are your best friend for natural-light interviews. If you have scheduling flexibility, plan outdoor or window-lit interviews for overcast conditions. The soft, even light requires minimal equipment to look professional.

Artificial Light in Institutional Settings

When shooting in offices, classrooms, conference rooms, or public buildings, you will encounter existing artificial light that you did not install and may not be able to remove. Understanding what each type does helps you decide whether to use it, supplement it, or fight against it.

Source TypeTypical KelvinPractical Notes
Fluorescent tubes (older)4000–5000K, often with green spikeOverhead direction is unflattering. Green cast requires magenta correction gel or camera setting.
LED troffer panels (modern office)3500–5000K, usually high CRIOften better color rendering than older fluorescents. Still overhead and directional.
Tungsten / incandescent practical lamps~3200KWarm and usually low power. Good for background warmth; rarely sufficient as key light.
Halogen downlights~3200KCommon in older institutional buildings. Overhead and directional; similar limitations to fluorescents.

The most common problem with institutional overhead lighting is direction: the light comes straight down from the ceiling, producing deep shadows in the eye sockets and under the nose and chin. For a static interview subject, this overhead direction is rarely acceptable on its own. You will generally want to introduce a side key light to override the overhead direction.

Reading the Room on Arrival

The first thing to do when you arrive at a location is read the light. Walk through the space and note: where is the brightest source? What direction is it coming from? What color temperature does it appear to be? Can any of the artificial sources be turned off or dimmed? Is there a window that could serve as a key or fill? What is the background doing — is it darker or brighter than the subject position?

Based on this assessment, you will make one of three decisions. First, work purely with available light: this is fastest and requires no equipment, but only works when the available light is genuinely good — sufficient intensity, acceptable direction, consistent color temperature. Second, supplement available light: use your kit to reinforce and improve what is already there, without trying to replace it entirely. Third, take control: turn off or block available sources that are problematic and build your lighting from scratch using your kit. This takes more time but gives you complete control over the result.

When "Good Enough" Is Not Good Enough

Available light often looks adequate on location in person, but the camera sees things differently. Your eyes have extraordinary dynamic range and adapt to color temperature automatically. A camera does neither. What looks fine to your eyes may appear flat, color-cast, or inconsistently exposed on camera. Get in the habit of always checking your image on a monitor, not just looking at the scene with your eyes.

Even when available light looks acceptable on camera, adding a modest key light to strengthen direction and a reflector to manage contrast frequently elevates the image from "acceptable" to "professional." The additional setup time is usually ten to fifteen minutes — a worthwhile investment for the quality improvement it delivers.

Key Takeaways

  • Overcast daylight is a natural softbox; direct sun is a hard source that requires subjects to be moved into open shade.
  • Position subjects perpendicular to windows, with the window acting as a side key light.
  • Never shoot with a bright window or exterior behind the subject unless deliberately creating a silhouette.
  • Natural light changes over time; artificial light gives you consistency and control.
  • Institutional overhead lighting creates unflattering downward shadows — plan to supplement it with a side key.
  • Reading the room on arrival determines whether to use, supplement, or replace available light.
