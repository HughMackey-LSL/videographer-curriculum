Minimal Kit Setup

~9 min read4 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • List the items in a minimal viable lighting kit and explain the purpose of each.
  • Manage power safely using circuit capacity calculations.
  • Execute the setup order drill consistently to reduce errors under time pressure.
  • Pack and transport lighting equipment correctly to protect gear and maximize efficiency.

The Minimal Viable Lighting Kit

A minimal viable lighting kit is the smallest collection of equipment that can handle the full range of common field lighting situations: interviews, talking-head videos, corporate documentary, and institutional content. Every item on this list earns its place. If an item does not solve a specific problem that arises regularly, it does not belong in a minimal kit — it belongs in a larger kit for specialized productions.

ItemWhy It Earns Its Place
One bicolor LED panel, 100W+Provides the key light. Bicolor (3200–5600K) matches any dominant ambient source without gels.
Softbox or diffusion frame for the LEDConverts the hard LED output into a soft, flattering key. Non-negotiable for interview work.
Small accent or hair light (LED, any color)Provides separation and back light when needed. Small enough to store on a boom or second stand.
42-inch or larger 5-in-1 reflectorProvides fill light, negative fill, and bounce surface — four functions in one collapsible disc.
Two medium light standsOne for the key, one for the accent or reflector. Medium stands (10–12 feet maximum) handle most field ceilings.
Sandbags (minimum two)Required safety measure for any raised stand. Never raise a stand without a sandbag on the base.
Extension cords (two, 25-foot, grounded)Outlets are never where you need them. 25-foot cords handle most room configurations.
Power strip (grounded, surge-protected)Allows both lights and a laptop or monitor to share a single outlet position.
Gaffer tape (one roll)Tapes cables to floors, attaches diffusion to windows, secures flags, holds everything else together.
Gel kit (small)CTO, CTB, plus-green, minus-green: four gels solve the most common color temperature correction needs.

Power Management

Every location you shoot in has a finite electrical capacity, and you are responsible for not tripping breakers or overloading circuits. A standard commercial circuit in the United States provides 15 amperes at 120 volts, for a maximum continuous load of approximately 1800 watts (safety standard: use no more than 80% of rated capacity, so 1440 watts). A modern 100W LED panel draws approximately 100 watts — less than 1 amp. Two LED panels and a laptop draw well under 5 amps, far within a 15-amp circuit's safe capacity.

Problems arise when you plug into a circuit that is already loaded with other devices — desktop computers, monitors, HVAC equipment, or other production gear. Before setting up, identify which outlets you plan to use and ask whether they share a circuit with heavy equipment. In office environments, conference room circuits are frequently shared with copiers, coffee machines, and space heaters — all high-draw devices. If in doubt, use a clamp meter to measure existing load before adding your lights.

Important

Always plug your lights into a grounded (three-prong) outlet using a grounded cord. Never use a two-to-three-prong adapter (cheater plug) for professional lighting equipment. The ground protects both the equipment and any person who touches the fixture in a fault condition.

Sandbag Protocol

A light stand is stable when the load on it is below a certain height-to-weight ratio. When a stand is fully extended — especially with a heavy softbox on top — it is top-heavy and can tip over if bumped, if the floor is uneven, or if a cable is pulled. A tipped stand with a heavy LED panel on it can injure a person or destroy equipment.

Always sandbag any raised stand. The sandbag goes over the lowest leg of the tripod base — the leg that is pointing away from where people are walking. A 10-pound sandbag significantly improves stability. Two sandbags are better. Do not use weight bags, camera bags, or other improvised weight — they shift and do not distribute weight correctly. Commercially available sandbags are inexpensive and purpose-designed. Route power cables along the floor toward the stand's base and tape them down with gaffer tape to prevent tripping or pulling the stand over by cable tension.

Common Mistake

Rolling a cable directly from an outlet to a stand without taping it to the floor creates a trip hazard and a stand-tipping hazard simultaneously. Every cable that crosses a walkable area must be secured to the floor with gaffer tape — every time, without exception.

The Setup Order Drill

Consistency in setup order prevents errors, saves time, and allows you to set up quickly even when rushed or distracted. Practice this sequence until it is automatic.

Step one: assess the space. Identify the camera position, subject position, background, available power outlets, and any problem light sources. This takes two to three minutes and determines everything else. Step two: route extension cords from outlets to your planned light positions. Tape cables to the floor at any crossing point. Step three: set up and extend the key light stand. Attach the softbox. Attach the LED panel to the softbox. Connect the power cable. Position the stand. Do not raise it to full height yet — keep it low until positioned. Step four: sandbag the stand. Step five: raise the stand and LED to the correct height. Angle the softbox to the key position. Step six: set the key light color temperature to match the dominant ambient source. Turn it on and set power level. Step seven: set the camera and check exposure. Adjust key power or distance as needed. Step eight: position the reflector on the shadow side. Check the shadow quality on your monitor. Step nine: if using the accent or hair light, set up the second stand, sandbag it, position and aim the light, connect power. Check for lens flare. Step ten: do a complete final check of the full frame on your monitor with the subject in place.

Gaffer Tape Uses

Gaffer tape is a heavy cloth-backed pressure-sensitive tape with a matte black finish that does not leave adhesive residue when removed carefully. It is the universal problem-solver on a lighting kit. Core uses: securing cables to floors and walls; attaching diffusion material to windows; masking small gaps where light is spilling undesirably; labeling stands and cables with a marker; temporarily securing flags and cutters in position; and holding reflectors at angles when no one is available to hold them. Do not substitute duct tape — it leaves adhesive residue and damages surfaces.

Packing and Transportation

Lighting equipment is heavy, fragile, and expensive. Proper packing protects the gear and makes setup faster at the next location because everything is organized and in its place. LED panels should be stored in padded bags or cases — either the manufacturer's case or a padded equipment bag of the correct size. Softboxes collapse and pack into the LED panel bag or a separate flat bag. Light stands pack into a dedicated stand tube or bag — a 36-inch or 42-inch stand bag holds two medium stands with room for extension cords rolled around them. The reflector collapses and packs in its own pouch, which fits in the stand bag.

Before packing up, do a complete walkthrough of the shooting space to check for gear left behind: gels removed from lights, gaffer tape left on walls or floors, cables behind furniture, sandbags under stands that have already been moved. A packing checklist matched to your kit list ensures nothing is left at the location.

Key Takeaways

  • A minimal viable lighting kit covers the key, fill (via reflector), accent/back, and all necessary support and safety items.
  • A bicolor LED is the most versatile panel for field work — it matches any ambient source without gels.
  • A standard 15-amp circuit safely handles up to 1440 watts; two LED panels draw well within this limit.
  • Always sandbag raised stands and tape cables to the floor across any walkable area — without exception.
  • The setup order drill — assess, cable, stand, sandbag, key, exposure, fill, accent, final check — should be practiced until automatic.
  • Store and transport LED panels in padded cases; stands in a dedicated stand bag; complete a walkthrough before packing to avoid leaving items at the location.
