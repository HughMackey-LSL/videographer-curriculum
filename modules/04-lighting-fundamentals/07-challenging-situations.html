Challenging Lighting Situations

~10 min read5 objectives

Learning Objectives

  • Correctly expose and light darker skin tones using technical monitoring tools.
  • Select a high-CRI LED for better color rendering across varied skin tones.
  • Adapt your lighting approach for offices, conference rooms, classrooms, and event spaces.
  • Address harsh outdoor sunlight with reflectors and open shade positioning.
  • Describe what to do when existing theatrical or event lighting cannot be changed.

Exposing Darker Skin Tones Correctly

Camera metering systems are calibrated to expose for a mid-tone average of the scene. When the subject has dark skin tones, the camera's auto exposure — and even an operator's eye trained on a typical LCD monitor — can systematically underexpose. The result is footage where the darkest skin tones are crushed into featureless shadow, losing all the detail and texture of the subject's face.

The solution is to use technical monitoring tools rather than trusting the screen alone. A waveform monitor shows the luminance of the image as a graph, where the subject's skin tones appear as a traceable band. For darker skin tones, this band should typically read in the 40–60 IRE range on a waveform display, depending on the specific tone. False color display — a camera or monitor feature that maps luminance values to a color overlay — is even faster to read in the field: skin tones appear in a defined color band that tells you at a glance whether exposure is correct.

When lighting darker skin tones, the key light needs sufficient power to correctly expose the skin without simultaneously blowing out lighter elements in the frame such as a white shirt or pale background. If the scene contrast is high, consider reducing background brightness or flagging spill from lighter surfaces rather than reducing key power. Diffusion is especially important — harsh direct sources create pronounced specular highlights that can produce very high local contrast on darker skin. A softbox or large bounce card produces a more gradual, flattering specular that reveals facial detail rather than overwhelming it.

Important

Never rely on a built-in LCD monitor in bright conditions to judge exposure for darker skin tones. The screen brightens in high ambient light, making everything look underexposed and tempting you to overexpose the image. Use a waveform or false color display, or shade the monitor carefully.

Color Rendering Index and LED Quality

Not all LED light sources render color equally. CRI (Color Rendering Index) is a measurement from 0 to 100 of how accurately a light source renders colors compared to a reference source. A CRI of 100 renders all colors identically to the reference. LED sources with a CRI below 85 can make skin tones look inaccurate, shift the apparent color of clothing, and produce results that do not respond predictably to white balance correction.

For video production, use LEDs rated at CRI 90 or higher. This applies particularly to key lights that are illuminating faces. Fill lights, accent lights, and background lights have somewhat less critical color rendering requirements, but maintaining consistent CRI across all sources simplifies white balance and color correction.

When evaluating an LED fixture you have not used before, the TLCI (Television Lighting Consistency Index) is a more accurate measure than CRI for broadcast and video applications. A TLCI of 90 or above is the standard for broadcast-quality work. Consumer-grade LEDs may list impressive CRI ratings but have low TLCI, meaning they look acceptable to the eye but render poorly on camera.

Office Environments

Offices present two consistent challenges: overhead lighting direction and mixed color temperature. The overhead direction produces the unflattering shadows under brows and nose described in earlier lessons. Mixed temperature occurs when overhead LEDs or fluorescents combine with daylight from windows.

The most effective approach in a typical office is to position the subject near a window, turn off the overhead lights in the immediate shooting area if possible, and use the window as the key or supplement it with your LED. If overheads cannot be turned off, they become a fill and you override the overhead direction with your LED key from the side. Gel your LED to match the overheads or set it to daylight and eliminate the overheads in the frame. Watch for table reflections in conference areas — a large conference table can create an unwanted fill light by reflecting the ceiling fixtures upward onto the underside of the subject's face.

Conference Rooms

Conference rooms typically have low ceilings, limited floor space, and no rigging points for overhead light fixtures. Work on stands and keep them as low as possible — a high key light stand in a conference room with a low ceiling may need to be extended horizontally rather than vertically, requiring a counterweight or sandbag to prevent tipping. Position stands out of the background frame rather than between the camera and subject. Beware of table reflections: a shiny conference table picks up ceiling light and bounces it upward, creating an underlit, slightly eerie fill from below. Moving the subject to the head of the table rather than the middle, or changing the camera angle to reduce table visibility, can solve this.

Tip

In conference rooms, the wall behind the camera position is often the best bounce surface. Point your LED softbox at the wall and use the reflected light as your key. This keeps stands out of the background, reduces the visual footprint of your kit, and often produces very good soft light.

Classrooms

Classrooms typically have overhead fluorescent or LED lighting that runs in rows parallel to the whiteboard. When a subject is seated at a desk or standing at the front, the overhead light direction is unflattering. If possible, move the subject away from the directly overhead fixture — standing a person slightly between two rows of ceiling fixtures gives them slightly more angled light from two directions, which is marginally better. Supplementing with a side LED key is the definitive solution. Turn off the overhead section directly above the subject if individual switches are available; the adjacent overheads will still provide ambient fill.

Event Spaces

Large event spaces — halls, ballrooms, auditoriums — often have theatrical or architectural lighting that you did not install and cannot move. Theatrical lighting may shift color and intensity during the event as the lighting operator changes looks. For pre-event interviews, work in a specific area of the space where you can control your light by bringing your own fixtures. For footage during the event itself, you generally must work within the existing theatrical lighting rather than fight it.

When working within theatrical lighting, identify the dominant source color and match your camera white balance to it. Check whether the theatrical fixtures are producing a CRI high enough for good skin rendering — many theatrical LED fixtures used for wash lighting have low CRI and render skin poorly. Raising your own LED to match the theatrical color and supplementing to provide more intensity on the subject can improve results. Accept that event lighting will change and your footage may not be perfectly consistent throughout.

Outdoor Daylight

Full midday sun produces hard light from directly overhead or slightly angled, creating deep eye socket shadows and a harsh nose shadow on the subject's upper lip. The two standard solutions are open shade and reflector fill.

Moving the subject into open shade — the shadow cast by a building, overhang, or large tree — eliminates direct sun and replaces it with soft indirect sky light. This is almost always superior to trying to manage direct sun with reflectors or diffusion panels. In open shade, set white balance to shade (approximately 7000K) or custom white balance by pointing at a white card in the shade.

If open shade is not available and you must shoot in direct sun, position the subject so the sun is behind them — becoming a rim or back light — and use a large reflector in front of and slightly above to bounce light back onto the face. This requires someone to hold the reflector and careful positioning, but it turns the directional problem into an advantage. Alternatively, a small LED panel outdoors can overpower the shadow side in limited close-up situations, though matching color temperature requires setting the LED to 5600K and accepting high power draw.

Key Takeaways

  • Use a waveform or false color display — not the LCD screen alone — to confirm correct exposure on darker skin tones.
  • Use LEDs rated CRI 90 or above for key lights; TLCI 90+ for broadcast-quality work.
  • In offices, turn off overheads near the subject if possible and use a window or side LED as the key.
  • Conference room table reflections can create unwanted under-lighting — change the subject's position or camera angle to address it.
  • Event lighting may change during the event; match white balance to the dominant theatrical source and supplement where possible.
  • For outdoor direct sun, move to open shade if available; otherwise turn the sun into a backlight and use a reflector for front fill.
