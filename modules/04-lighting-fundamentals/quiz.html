Question 1

What determines whether a light source produces hard or soft light?

A larger source relative to the subject produces softer, more diffuse light with gentler shadow edges. A small or distant source produces hard light with sharp shadows. Wattage, color temperature, and natural vs. artificial are not the determining factors.
Question 2

What is the standard recommended position for an interview key light?

The standard key light position for an interview is 30 to 45 degrees to one side of the camera axis and approximately 30 to 45 degrees above eye level. This angle models the face three-dimensionally and creates a pleasing shadow pattern.
Question 3

What is the primary purpose of the fill light in a three-point lighting setup?

The fill light illuminates the shadow side of the subject, reducing the contrast ratio between the key-lit and shadow sides. It should never overpower the key light or create its own conflicting shadows.
Question 4

What does the back light (hair light) contribute to a three-point lighting setup?

The back light is positioned behind and above the subject to create a bright edge around the hair and shoulders. This separation prevents the subject from visually merging with the background and adds three-dimensional depth to the image.
Question 5

Why can auto white balance not fully solve a mixed color temperature problem in a scene?

White balance — whether automatic or manual — sets a single reference point for what "white" looks like. When two sources of different color temperatures both illuminate the subject, balancing for one makes the other look color-cast. The light sources themselves must be matched to each other.
Question 6

To use a window as a soft key light for an interview, how should the subject be positioned relative to the window?

Positioning the subject perpendicular to the window turns the window into a side key light. The camera is placed at roughly 90 degrees to the window. This is the classic window portrait position that produces soft, directional light with a clean shadow side that can be controlled by a reflector.
Question 7

What is the effect of adding diffusion material in front of a light source?

Diffusion material scatters light as it passes through, enlarging the effective source size. A larger effective source produces softer light with more gradual shadow edges. Diffusion also reduces the total output of the fixture, typically between half a stop and two stops depending on the material's density.
Question 8

In a one-light interview setup, what fills the shadow side of the subject's face?

A one-light setup uses a single LED as the key light and a reflector on the shadow side to provide fill. The reflector bounces light from the key back toward the shadow side, providing fill that is inherently proportional to the key and cannot create conflicting shadows of its own.
Question 9

What is negative fill and what does it accomplish?

Negative fill uses a black, light-absorbing material on the shadow side of the subject. In environments with significant ambient light, this material prevents ambient light from accidentally filling the shadows, increasing contrast and adding three-dimensional depth without touching the key light.
Question 10

When lighting a subject with darker skin tones, what is the most reliable way to confirm correct exposure?

Camera auto exposure and the LCD screen can both systematically underexpose darker skin tones. A waveform monitor shows the luminance of skin tones as a measurable value on a scale, and false color maps those values to visible colors that confirm correct exposure independently of the screen's appearance.
