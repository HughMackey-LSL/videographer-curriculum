Hard Light and Soft Light

Every light source produces either hard light or soft light, and the determining factor is not wattage, color, or whether the source is natural or artificial. It is the apparent size of the source relative to the subject. A large source close to the subject produces soft light. A small source — or a large source moved very far away — produces hard light.

Hard light is defined by the sharpness of its shadow edges. Hold your hand up in direct midday sun and the shadow of your fingers on the ground has a crisp, defined edge. That is hard light. It reveals surface texture vividly, creates high contrast between lit and shadowed areas, and can look dramatic in the right context. On a human face, however, hard light is generally unflattering: it exaggerates wrinkles, pores, and imperfections, and creates deep, harsh shadows under the brow and nose.

Soft light has gradual shadow edges that transition slowly from lit to shadow. An overcast sky is the classic example — the entire cloud cover becomes a massive diffuse source that wraps around your subject. Soft light is flattering on skin, easier to expose correctly, and easier to control. Most interview and documentary lighting deliberately pursues soft light, even when using hard artificial sources as the starting point.

Tip

To make a hard source softer, move it closer or add diffusion material in front of it. Both strategies increase the effective size of the source relative to the subject. Moving a light farther away always makes it harder, even if the surface area seems the same.

Direction

Where a light source is positioned relative to the subject and camera shapes the three-dimensional appearance of the face and body. Lighting direction is described in terms of horizontal angle from the camera axis and vertical angle above the subject's eye line.

DirectionEffect
Frontal (0°)Flat, shadowless, minimal texture. Makes subjects look two-dimensional. Common for news but rarely flattering.
45° above and to one sideThe standard interview position. Creates a small triangle of light on the shadow cheek (Rembrandt triangle), models the face pleasingly.
Side (90°)Half the face lit, half in shadow. Dramatic, used for stylized or high-contrast looks. Rarely used for straightforward interviews.
Rim / BacklightLight from behind, above. Creates a bright edge around the subject's hair and shoulders, separating them from the background. Used as a secondary source.
Under-lightingLight from below eye line. Unnatural and often unsettling — almost never used for professional interviews.

Vertical angle matters as much as horizontal angle. Too high and you create deep eye socket shadows. Too low and the light is unflattering and draws attention to the chin. The sweet spot for a key light is roughly 30 to 45 degrees above eye level, angled to one side.

Intensity and Distance: The Inverse Square Law

Light intensity falls off with distance. The formal relationship is the inverse square law: double the distance between the light and subject, and the intensity at the subject drops to one quarter, not one half. This has two practical implications.

First, moving a light closer dramatically increases its power at the subject — useful when you need more output from a small LED. Second, the falloff between your subject and the background is also governed by this law. A light placed close to the subject will fall off sharply, leaving the background much darker. A light placed far away falls off more gradually, spilling more evenly onto both subject and background. Use this to your advantage when you want to control background brightness independently of subject brightness.

Note

In practical terms: if your subject looks underexposed and you double the light's power, you gain one stop of exposure. If instead you halve the distance between light and subject, you gain two stops. Distance is often more powerful than power output when working with small LEDs.

Color Temperature

All light has a color. Human vision adapts to color temperature automatically, but cameras must be told (via white balance) what "white" looks like. Color temperature is measured in Kelvin (K). Lower numbers are warmer (more orange/red); higher numbers are cooler (more blue).

SourceApproximate Kelvin
Candlelight~1800K
Tungsten / Incandescent bulb~3200K
Warm white LED~3500K
Overcast daylight / Flash~5600K
Clear blue sky6500–8000K

When multiple light sources of different color temperatures appear in the same frame, you have a mixed color temperature problem. Set the white balance for one source and the other looks wrong. This is covered in depth in Lesson 5, but understanding that every source has a color is the first step.

Bicolor LED panels let you dial the color temperature between approximately 3200K and 5600K, making them extremely versatile for matching any dominant source in the room. This is one reason bicolor LEDs have become the standard tool for portable field lighting.

Contrast Ratio

Contrast ratio describes the brightness difference between the key-light side of the subject's face and the shadow side. A 2:1 ratio means the lit side is twice as bright as the shadow side — low contrast, soft, even corporate lighting. A 4:1 ratio means the lit side is four times brighter — more dramatic, with visible shadows. A 8:1 ratio produces very deep shadows and a strongly stylized look.

Your camera's dynamic range determines how much contrast it can capture without clipping highlights or crushing shadows. Most modern cameras handle 8–12 stops of dynamic range. A high contrast ratio can push your image outside that range and force you to choose between losing shadow detail or highlight detail. For interview work, keeping the ratio between 2:1 and 4:1 gives you clean, well-exposed images without looking flat.

How the Five Qualities Interact

Hardness, direction, intensity, color temperature, and contrast ratio are not independent choices — they interact. Moving a light closer makes it softer (hardness) and brighter (intensity), which in turn affects your contrast ratio if you don't compensate. Changing direction changes the contrast on the face. Adding diffusion changes the hardness and reduces the intensity, requiring a compensation in distance or power. Choosing a bicolor LED affects color temperature but also limits maximum power output compared to a fixed-temperature fixture.

Skilled lighting operators think about all five qualities simultaneously and understand how adjusting one will ripple through the others. As you practice, you will develop intuition for these relationships. The goal in field production is to make quick, confident decisions that produce clean, consistent images regardless of what the location throws at you.

Key Takeaways

  • Hard vs. soft light is determined by source size relative to the subject, not by power or source type.
  • The standard interview key light position is 30–45 degrees above eye level and to one side.
  • Halving the distance to a light gains two stops of exposure; doubling it loses two stops.
  • Tungsten sources are approximately 3200K (warm); daylight is approximately 5600K (cooler).
  • A contrast ratio between 2:1 and 4:1 is appropriate for most interview and documentary work.
  • All five qualities of light — hardness, direction, intensity, color temperature, and contrast — interact and must be considered together.
